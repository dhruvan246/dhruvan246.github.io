Wrinkled, comical and famously affectionate, the Pug is one of the oldest known toy breeds, with roots tracing back to ancient China. Bred purely as a companion, the Pug thrives on human attention and has earned a reputation as a sturdy little clown that fits happily into almost any home.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |

| Lifespan | 12–15 years |

| Energy | Low to moderate |
| Good with kids | Yes, very |

## Appearance & temperament

The Pug is a compact, square, thickset dog typically weighing 14–18 pounds (6–8 kg). Its most recognizable features are the large round head, deeply wrinkled forehead, short muzzle, and big dark expressive eyes. The tightly curled tail sits high over the hip, and coats come in fawn or black, usually with a dark "mask" on the face.

Temperamentally, Pugs are even-tempered, playful and people-loving. They form strong bonds with their families, get along well with children and other pets, and rarely show aggression. They can be stubborn during training but are eager to please when motivated. Bred to be lap dogs, they dislike being left alone for long periods.

## Diet & nutrition

Pugs are enthusiastic eaters and gain weight very easily, so portion control is essential. Feed a quality complete dog food measured by the manufacturer's guidance for your dog's weight, usually split into two meals a day. Because obesity worsens the breathing and joint problems Pugs are prone to, limit treats and avoid free-feeding. Use treats and table scraps sparingly, and keep fresh water available at all times. Always consult your veterinarian about the right diet and quantity for your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

Despite their love of the couch, Pugs need regular gentle exercise to stay fit and manage their weight. A couple of short daily walks plus some indoor play is usually enough. Because of their flat faces, Pugs overheat quickly and struggle to breathe in heat or humidity, so avoid strenuous activity and walk during cooler parts of the day.

Training should be positive, patient and reward-based. Pugs are intelligent but stubborn, and respond best to short, fun sessions. Early socialization and gentle leadership help curb the stubborn streak.

## Grooming & care

The Pug's short coat is easy to brush but sheds noticeably year-round, especially the fawn variety. Weekly brushing helps control loose hair. The facial wrinkles need special attention: clean and dry the folds regularly to prevent moisture, irritation and infection. Check and clean the ears, trim nails routinely, and brush teeth often, as small breeds are prone to dental disease.

## Health & lifespan

Pugs typically live 12 to 15 years. As a brachycephalic (flat-faced) breed, they can suffer from breathing difficulties known as brachycephalic obstructive airway syndrome. Other concerns include eye injuries due to their prominent eyes, skin fold dermatitis, obesity, and joint conditions such as hip dysplasia and patellar luxation. Buying from a responsible breeder and keeping your Pug lean and cool greatly reduce many risks. Regular veterinary check-ups are important.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Pugs good for first-time owners?**
Yes. Their affectionate, adaptable nature and modest exercise needs make them well suited to first-time owners, though their grooming and health care should not be underestimated.

**Do Pugs bark a lot?**
Pugs are generally quiet and not prone to excessive barking, though they may snore, snort and make other distinctive noises because of their short muzzles.

## Conclusion

The Pug is a loving, low-maintenance companion in spirit but one that needs attentive care for its weight, wrinkles and breathing. Kept lean, cool and close to their people, Pugs reward owners with years of devoted, good-humored company.
