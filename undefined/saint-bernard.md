Famous as the patient rescue dog of the snowy Swiss Alps, the Saint Bernard is a true gentle giant. Bred by monks at the Great St. Bernard Hospice to find and warm travelers lost in mountain passes, this massive, good-natured breed is calm, devoted and remarkably tolerant of children.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Size | Large to giant (140-180 lb; 26-30 in tall) |
| Lifespan | 8-10 years |
| Coat | Short (smooth) or long (rough), dense double coat |
| Energy | Low to moderate |
| Good with kids | Yes, very patient and tolerant |

## Appearance & temperament

The Saint Bernard is one of the heaviest dog breeds, with a broad, powerful body, a large head, and the signature wrinkled brow and gentle, dark eyes. The coat comes in two varieties, short-haired and long-haired, typically in red-and-white or brindle-and-white markings. A black "mask" around the eyes and ears is common.

Despite their imposing size, Saint Bernards are famously sweet, steady and affectionate. They bond closely with their families, are patient with children, and generally welcome strangers calmly. They are not high-strung or aggressive, though their sheer size means early socialization and basic training are essential. Many drool heavily, especially the loose-jowled lines.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality diet formulated for large or giant breeds. As puppies, Saint Bernards grow extremely fast, so a controlled-growth large-breed puppy food helps protect developing joints; avoid overfeeding to prevent too-rapid growth. Adults do well on measured meals rather than free feeding.

Because of their deep chests, Saint Bernards are at risk of bloat (gastric torsion). Splitting food into two or more meals a day, avoiding heavy exercise right after eating, and using a slow-feeder bowl can help reduce that risk. Keep them lean, since excess weight strains their joints and heart. Always provide fresh water.

## Exercise & training

Saint Bernards have modest exercise needs for their size. A couple of daily walks plus gentle play are usually enough. They tolerate cold far better than heat, so exercise in cool parts of the day and never overexert them in warm weather, as their bulk and thick coat make overheating dangerous.

Start training and socialization early, while the dog is still small enough to handle easily. They are intelligent and eager to please but can be stubborn, so positive, consistent methods work best. Teaching good leash manners is important, because a fully grown Saint can easily pull an adult off balance.

## Grooming & care

Both coat types shed, with heavier seasonal shedding. Brush a few times a week (more during shed seasons) to manage loose hair and keep the coat healthy. Wipe the facial folds and jowls regularly to control drool and prevent skin irritation. Check and clean ears, trim nails, and bathe as needed. Provide a cool, roomy resting spot, as these large dogs need space and good ventilation.

## Health & lifespan

Saint Bernards typically live about 8 to 10 years, which is shorter than smaller breeds, as is common for giant dogs. Responsible breeders screen for hip and elbow dysplasia, heart conditions such as dilated cardiomyopathy, and eye problems. Bloat, joint disease, and certain cancers are concerns to watch for.

Keeping your dog at a healthy weight, providing joint-supportive care, and scheduling regular veterinary checkups all help. Watch for signs of overheating and seek prompt care for a bloated, restless dog that is retching without producing vomit.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Saint Bernards drool a lot?**
Yes. Many Saint Bernards drool heavily, especially after drinking or eating, due to their loose lips and jowls. Keeping a cloth handy and wiping their face regularly helps manage it.

**Are Saint Bernards good family dogs?**
Very much so. They are gentle, patient and affectionate, and typically excellent with children. Their large size means supervision around small kids and early training are still important.

## Conclusion

The Saint Bernard is a calm, loving giant that thrives as a devoted family companion. With a cool living environment, large-breed nutrition, moderate exercise, routine grooming and attentive health care, this iconic rescue breed rewards its owners with years of steady, affectionate loyalty.
