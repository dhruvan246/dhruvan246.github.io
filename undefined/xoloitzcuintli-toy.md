The Mexican Hairless Dog, known in its homeland as the Xoloitzcuintli (or "Xolo"), is one of the world's oldest and rarest breeds, with roots tracing back thousands of years to ancient Mesoamerica. It comes in three sizes; this guide focuses on the toy variety, the smallest of the three. Prized by the Aztecs and named for the dog-god Xolotl, the breed remains a calm, devoted, and dignified companion today.

## Overview

| Trait | Detail |
|---|---|
| Size | Toy variety, roughly 10-14 inches tall, about 10-15 lb |
| Lifespan | Around 13-18 years |
| Coat | Hairless (smooth, warm skin) or a coated variety with a short coat |
| Energy | Moderate |
| Good with kids | Yes, with supervision and early socialization |

## Appearance & temperament

The toy Xolo is a compact, elegant dog with a sleek, athletic outline. The hairless variety has smooth, tough skin that may carry a few sparse hairs on the head, feet, or tail tip; colors range from black and slate to bronze and red. A coated variety also exists, born within the same litters, with a short, flat coat. Both share large bat-like ears, almond eyes, and an alert, thoughtful expression.

In temperament, the Xolo is calm, loyal, and intelligent. It bonds closely with its family, often attaching strongly to one or two people, and can be reserved with strangers. Despite its serene nature indoors, it is watchful and makes a good alert dog. Early socialization helps temper its natural caution.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality diet formulated for small breeds, divided into measured meals rather than free-feeding. Because the toy Xolo is small and prone to weight gain when underexercised, portion control matters. Provide constant access to fresh water. Some hairless dogs are sensitive to certain foods, which can show up in their skin, so introduce new foods gradually. Ask your veterinarian to confirm correct portions for your dog's age, weight, and activity level.

## Exercise & training

The Xolo has moderate energy and is content with daily walks plus play and mental stimulation. A couple of short-to-moderate walks a day, along with games, will keep a toy Xolo happy. They are notably intelligent and trainable but can be independent, so use consistent, reward-based methods and keep sessions engaging. Their cleverness means they learn quickly, including how to escape under-stimulating routines, so mental enrichment is as important as physical exercise.

## Grooming & care

Skin care defines this breed. The hairless Xolo needs regular gentle bathing and wiping to keep skin clean, but over-washing or harsh products can dry or irritate it. Protect skin from sunburn with shade and pet-safe sunblock, and provide warmth in cold weather with a sweater. Coated Xolos need only routine brushing. For both varieties, keep nails trimmed, ears clean, and teeth brushed regularly.

## Health & lifespan

The Xolo is generally a hardy, long-lived breed, often reaching 13-18 years. Hairless individuals frequently have an incomplete set of teeth, a trait linked to the hairless gene. Skin issues such as acne, dryness, or sunburn are the most common concerns and are usually managed with good skin care. Routine veterinary checkups, dental care, and weight management support a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Mexican Hairless Dogs hypoallergenic?** No dog is truly hypoallergenic, but hairless Xolos shed no coat and produce little dander, which some allergy sufferers tolerate better. Spend time with one before committing.

**Does the toy Xolo need special skin care?** Yes. Hairless skin needs gentle cleaning, sun protection, and warmth in cold weather to stay healthy and comfortable.

## Conclusion

The toy Mexican Hairless Dog is an ancient, dignified companion that pairs a calm, loyal temperament with modest exercise needs and a long lifespan. With attentive skin care, sun and cold protection, and early socialization, the Xolo rewards its owner with quiet devotion and centuries of living history at its side.
