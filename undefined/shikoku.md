The Shikoku is a rare, medium-sized Japanese spitz breed that originated in the mountainous Kochi region of Shikoku island, where it was developed to hunt wild boar and deer over rugged terrain. One of Japan's six native "Nihon Ken" breeds, it was designated a Living Natural Monument in 1937. Agile, hardy, and deeply loyal, the Shikoku combines wolf-like looks with a spirited, intelligent character.

## Appearance & temperament

The Shikoku has a balanced, sturdy frame built for stamina rather than speed. Its wedge-shaped head, erect triangular ears, and tightly curled or sickle tail give it a distinctly primitive, fox-like appearance. The breed is best known for its sesame coat, where black-tipped hairs blend over a red or cream base, though red, black-and-tan, and brindle also occur.

Temperamentally, the Shikoku is alert, brave, and devoted to its family while remaining naturally reserved with strangers. It tends to bond closely with one household and can be vocal and watchful, making a capable watchdog. Its strong prey drive and independent streak mean it benefits from a confident, consistent owner.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality diet formulated for an active, medium-sized dog, choosing a recipe with quality animal protein as the first ingredient. Portion according to age, weight, and activity level, typically split across two meals a day to support steady energy. Because the breed stays lean and muscular, monitor body condition closely and avoid overfeeding, as excess weight strains the joints. Always provide fresh water, and introduce any diet changes gradually. Consult your veterinarian for guidance tailored to your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

As a former hunting dog, the Shikoku needs substantial daily exercise: brisk walks, hikes, and secure off-leash running in a fenced area help burn its considerable energy. Mental stimulation through scent games, puzzle toys, and varied activities is just as important. The breed is intelligent and quick to learn but also independent, so positive, reward-based training that keeps sessions engaging works best. Early, thorough socialization is essential. Given its high prey drive, the Shikoku should be supervised around small animals and kept leashed in unsecured areas.

## Grooming & care

The Shikoku's double coat is relatively low-maintenance for everyday care, needing weekly brushing to remove loose hair and distribute natural oils. During the twice-yearly seasonal shed, daily brushing helps manage the heavy blow-out of undercoat. Bathe only as needed, since the coat is naturally clean and somewhat weather-resistant. Routine care also includes regular nail trimming, dental brushing, and checking the ears for dirt or moisture.

## Health & lifespan

The Shikoku is generally a healthy, robust breed with a typical lifespan of around 11 to 14 years. As with many breeds, responsible breeders screen for hip dysplasia, and patellar and eye conditions can occasionally appear. Because the gene pool is small, working with a reputable breeder who health-tests is valuable. Routine veterinary checkups, parasite prevention, dental care, and a healthy weight all support a long, active life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Shikoku dogs rare?**
Yes. The Shikoku is uncommon even in Japan and quite scarce elsewhere, so prospective owners often join waiting lists with dedicated breeders.

**Is the Shikoku good for first-time owners?**
It can be challenging for novices due to its independence, prey drive, and need for firm, consistent guidance. Owners with some dog experience tend to fare best.

## Conclusion

The Shikoku is a striking, spirited companion that rewards committed owners with loyalty, athleticism, and an engaging personality. With ample exercise, early socialization, and consistent training, this rare Japanese breed thrives as a devoted family dog and an able outdoor partner.
