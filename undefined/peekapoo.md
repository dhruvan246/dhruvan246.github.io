The Peekapoo is a small companion dog created by crossing a Pekingese with a Toy or Miniature Poodle. One of the earliest "designer" crossbreeds, it has been bred since the 1950s and 1960s for people seeking an affectionate lap dog with a low-shedding coat. Devoted and people-oriented, the Peekapoo bonds closely with its family.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| Size | Small (typically 8-11 in / 20-28 cm tall) |
| Lifespan | 12-15 years |
| Coat | Soft, wavy to curly; low-shedding |
| Energy | Low to moderate |
| Good with kids | Yes, best with gentle, older children |

## Appearance & temperament

Peekapoos are small dogs, usually weighing between 4 and 9 kg (roughly 9-20 lb) depending on whether a Toy or Miniature Poodle was used. They have a rounded head, dark expressive eyes, and a soft coat that may be wavy or curly and comes in colors such as cream, apricot, silver, black, and brown. Appearance varies, as crossbreeds do not breed to a fixed standard.

In temperament the Peekapoo is loyal, loving, and eager to be near its owner. It often forms a strong attachment to one person and can be a confident little watchdog, alerting the household to visitors. Early socialization helps curb excessive barking and wariness toward strangers.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality dog food formulated for small breeds, divided into two measured meals a day. Because Peekapoos are small and can gain weight easily, portion control is important—follow the feeding guide on the food and adjust for your dog's age, weight, and activity level. Fresh water should always be available. Limit treats to about ten percent of daily calories, and avoid feeding table scraps or foods toxic to dogs such as chocolate, grapes, onions, and xylitol. Small dogs can be prone to dental issues, so dry kibble or vet-approved dental chews may help.

## Exercise & training

The Peekapoo has modest exercise needs that suit apartment living. A couple of short daily walks plus indoor play usually keep it content. Take care in hot or humid weather, since the Pekingese ancestry can give some Peekapoos a slightly shortened muzzle that makes them less tolerant of heat and strenuous activity.

Thanks to the intelligent Poodle side, Peekapoos generally respond well to training. Use positive, reward-based methods with patience, as they can be a little stubborn or sensitive to harsh handling. Consistent house-training and early socialization with people and other pets pay off.

## Grooming & care

The Peekapoo's coat needs regular care. Brush it several times a week—ideally daily—to prevent mats and tangles, and arrange professional grooming or clipping every six to eight weeks. Trim or keep hair clear of the eyes, and check the eyes regularly, as the breed can be prone to tear staining and irritation. Routine ear cleaning, nail trims, and tooth brushing round out basic care.

## Health & lifespan

Peekapoos typically live 12 to 15 years. As a crossbreed they may inherit conditions from either parent. Watch for eye problems such as progressive retinal atrophy and cataracts, patellar luxation (slipping kneecaps), dental disease, and, in dogs with flatter faces, breathing difficulties. Buying from a responsible breeder who health-tests the parent dogs reduces risk. Keep up with routine veterinary checkups, vaccinations, parasite prevention, and weight management.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Peekapoos shed a lot?** No. The Peekapoo is a low-shedding breed and is often described as a good choice for allergy-sensitive households, though no dog is truly hypoallergenic.

**Are Peekapoos good apartment dogs?** Yes. Their small size, modest exercise needs, and affectionate nature make them well suited to apartments, provided they get daily attention and barking is managed.

## Conclusion

The Peekapoo is a devoted, low-shedding companion that thrives on human closeness. With regular grooming, gentle training, and routine veterinary care, this small crossbreed makes a loving, adaptable pet for individuals and families with older children alike.
