The Briard is an old French herding breed prized for its devotion, intelligence, and unmistakable shaggy coat. Once used to guard and move flocks across the French countryside, this large, hardworking dog forms deep bonds with its family and thrives when given a job to do.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |

| Lifespan | 10-12 years |

| Energy | High |
| Good with kids | Yes, with early socialization |

## Appearance & temperament

The Briard is a muscular, rectangular dog standing roughly 22-27 inches (56-69 cm) at the shoulder. Its long, coarse outer coat falls in slight waves and comes in solid colors such as black, tawny, and gray, often with the long "eyebrows" that veil the eyes. A distinctive breed trait is the double dewclaws on each hind leg, a feature required in the breed standard.

Temperamentally, the Briard is loyal, confident, and naturally protective. It is affectionate and playful with its own family but can be reserved or watchful around strangers, making it an alert watchdog. Bred to think independently while herding, the Briard is intelligent but can show a stubborn streak, so consistent guidance suits it best.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet formulated for large, active breeds, choosing a life-stage-appropriate food for puppies, adults, or seniors. Because the Briard is a deep-chested large breed, divide daily food into two meals rather than one large serving to support steady digestion.

Monitor body condition closely, as excess weight stresses the joints of a working dog. Provide constant access to fresh water, and adjust portions based on age, activity level, and your veterinarian's guidance. Treats should stay within a small fraction of daily calories.

## Exercise & training

The Briard is a high-energy herding dog that needs substantial daily activity, ideally an hour or more of walks, play, and mentally engaging work. Without enough exercise and stimulation, this clever breed may become bored and develop unwanted habits. Activities like herding trials, agility, obedience, and tracking channel its drive well.

Training should begin early and rely on positive, reward-based methods. The Briard is smart and eager to please its family but responds poorly to harsh handling. Early, ongoing socialization with people and other animals helps temper its protective instincts and shapes a confident, well-mannered adult.

## Grooming & care

The Briard's long double coat demands serious upkeep. Brush thoroughly several times a week, ideally most days, to prevent the coarse hair from matting close to the skin. Pay special attention to areas behind the ears, under the legs, and around the face.

Bathe as needed, check and clean the ears regularly, trim nails, and keep the hair around the eyes and feet tidy. Routine dental care and parasite prevention round out a sound grooming and care routine.

## Health & lifespan

With good care, the Briard typically lives about 10-12 years. As a deep-chested large breed, it can be prone to bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus), and the breed may also be affected by hip dysplasia and certain eye conditions, including night blindness and progressive retinal atrophy.

Choosing a responsible breeder who screens for hereditary issues, keeping your dog at a healthy weight, and scheduling regular veterinary checkups all support a long, comfortable life. Watch for early signs of joint or eye trouble.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Briards good family dogs?**
Yes. Briards are devoted, affectionate companions that bond closely with their families and are typically gentle with children when raised and socialized properly. Their protective nature makes them attentive watchdogs.

**Do Briards shed a lot?**
Briards are relatively low shedders, but their long coat requires frequent brushing to prevent mats and tangles. The grooming commitment is significant even though loose hair is modest.

## Conclusion

The Briard is a loyal, energetic, and strikingly coated herding dog best suited to active owners who can meet its needs for exercise, training, and regular grooming. In return, this French shepherd offers steadfast devotion, intelligence, and a watchful, loving presence in the home.
