The Sussex Spaniel is a low-set, sturdily built flushing spaniel best known for its distinctive golden-liver coat and famously mellow, easygoing disposition. Developed in the county of Sussex in southern England as a slow, methodical hunting companion, it remains one of the rarer spaniel breeds today, prized by enthusiasts for its calm character and devoted nature.

## Appearance & temperament

The Sussex Spaniel is longer than it is tall, with a deep chest, heavy bone and a rolling gait that sets it apart from other spaniels. Its signature feature is the rich golden-liver coat, a colour unique to the breed, complemented by hazel eyes and long, low-set ears. The coat is abundant on the chest, legs, ears and tail.

In temperament, Sussex Spaniels are typically calm, friendly and affectionate. They tend to be less hyperactive than many sporting breeds, forming strong bonds with their families. Some individuals can be a touch stubborn or reserved with strangers, and the breed is known for being somewhat vocal, often "talking" with a range of sounds.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food appropriate to your Sussex Spaniel's age, size and activity level. Because the breed is low-set and prone to weight gain, portion control matters: measure meals rather than free-feeding, and limit treats. Carrying excess weight adds strain to the joints and back, so keeping your dog lean is one of the simplest ways to protect its long-term health. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian for guidance on the right food and feeding amounts.

## Exercise & training

Despite their relaxed reputation, Sussex Spaniels are working gundogs at heart and need regular daily exercise to stay fit and content. Around an hour a day of walking, sniffing and play usually suits them well; they enjoy nose-led activities that engage their hunting instincts. They are intelligent but can be independent-minded, so training works best with patience, consistency and positive, reward-based methods. Early socialisation helps temper any natural wariness toward strangers.

## Grooming & care

The dense coat needs brushing several times a week to prevent mats and remove dead hair, with extra attention to the feathering on the ears, legs and underside. The long, pendulous ears trap moisture and debris, so check and gently clean them regularly to reduce the risk of ear infections. Routine nail trimming, dental care and seasonal coat maintenance round out a sensible grooming schedule.

## Health & lifespan

Sussex Spaniels generally live around 11 to 14 years. As a low-set, long-bodied breed they can be predisposed to certain conditions, including hip dysplasia and intervertebral disc issues, as well as ear infections linked to their heavy ear flaps. Some lines may carry heart concerns or eye conditions. Choosing a breeder who health-tests their dogs, keeping your pet at a healthy weight, and maintaining regular veterinary checkups all support a long, comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Sussex Spaniels good family dogs?**
Yes. They are typically gentle, affectionate and tolerant, making them well suited to families. Early socialisation and supervised, respectful interaction with young children help ensure a happy household.

**Do Sussex Spaniels bark a lot?**
The breed is known for being fairly vocal and may "talk" or bark more than some spaniels. Consistent training and adequate mental and physical stimulation help keep nuisance barking in check.

## Conclusion

The Sussex Spaniel is a calm, affectionate and characterful companion that rewards owners willing to meet its grooming, exercise and weight-management needs. Rare but devoted, this golden-liver gundog brings a gentle, sociable presence to active households that appreciate a slower-paced, loving spaniel.
