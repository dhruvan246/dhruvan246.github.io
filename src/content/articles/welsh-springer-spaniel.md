The Welsh Springer Spaniel is a hardy, affectionate gundog from Wales, instantly recognizable by its rich red-and-white coat. Devoted and even-tempered, "Welshies" make loyal family companions for active homes that can meet their need for exercise and company.

## Overview

| Trait | Typical range |
| --- | --- |
| Size | Medium; roughly 17–19 inches tall, 35–55 lb |
| Lifespan | About 12–15 years |
| Coat | Medium-length, straight, soft red-and-white double coat with feathering |
| Energy | High; bred to work all day in the field |
| Good with kids | Yes; gentle and patient with considerate children |

## Appearance & temperament

The Welsh Springer Spaniel is compact and well-balanced, slightly smaller than its English cousin. Its defining feature is a clear red (often described as rich chestnut) and white coat, with feathering on the ears, chest, legs and underside. The ears are set fairly low and shaped like a vine leaf.

In personality, Welshies are loyal, gentle and devoted to their families. They tend to be more reserved with strangers than some spaniels, so early socialization helps them stay confident. They are sensitive dogs that thrive on companionship and can become anxious or bored if left alone for long periods.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food appropriate for your dog's life stage, size and activity level. Active adult Welshies may need a bit more fuel during heavy exercise, while less active or older dogs need less to avoid weight gain. Divide the daily amount into two meals and measure portions rather than free-feeding. Keep fresh water available at all times, and check body condition regularly. Ask your veterinarian for guidance tailored to your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

As a working gundog, the Welsh Springer Spaniel needs substantial daily exercise: long walks, off-lead runs in safe areas, fetch and scent games all help burn energy and keep the mind engaged. A bored, under-exercised Welshie may become restless or destructive.

The breed is intelligent and eager to please, which makes training rewarding. They respond best to consistent, positive, reward-based methods; harsh corrections can make these sensitive dogs shut down. Early socialization with people, dogs and new situations builds a steady, friendly adult.

## Grooming & care

The medium-length coat needs brushing a few times a week to prevent mats and tangles, especially in the feathered areas behind the ears and on the legs. Like other spaniels, the Welshie's drop ears trap moisture and debris, so check and gently clean the ears regularly to reduce the risk of infection. Trim excess hair around the feet and ears, keep nails short, and maintain routine dental care.

## Health & lifespan

The Welsh Springer Spaniel is generally a robust, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of about 12–15 years. As with many breeds, some individuals can be affected by hip dysplasia and by eye conditions; their pendulous ears also make them prone to ear infections if not kept clean and dry. Responsible breeders screen for hereditary issues. Routine veterinary checkups, parasite prevention, dental care and maintaining a healthy weight all support a long, comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Welsh Springer Spaniels good family dogs?**
Yes. They are affectionate, gentle and loyal, and generally do well with considerate children when properly socialized. They bond closely with their people and prefer not to be left alone for long stretches.

**How much exercise does a Welsh Springer Spaniel need?**
Quite a lot. As an active gundog breed, a Welshie typically needs at least an hour or more of vigorous activity each day, plus mental enrichment such as training games or scent work.

## Conclusion

The Welsh Springer Spaniel is a loving, energetic companion best suited to active owners who can provide plenty of exercise, companionship and consistent positive training. Give a Welshie enough activity and attention, plus routine grooming and veterinary care, and you'll have a devoted friend for many years.
