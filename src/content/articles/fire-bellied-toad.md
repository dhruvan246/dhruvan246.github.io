The Fire-Bellied Toad (genus *Bombina*) is one of the most popular beginner amphibians, prized for its bright belly, lively daytime activity, and modest housing needs. Native to Europe and Asia, these small frogs are hardy and long-lived when given a clean, semi-aquatic home.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Size | 1.5-2 inches (4-5 cm) |
| Lifespan | 10-15 years in captivity |
| Enclosure | 10-15 gallon semi-aquatic tank |
| Temperament | Active, social, diurnal |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & behaviour

Fire-Bellied Toads have a mottled green or brown, slightly warty back that camouflages them against pond plants. Their defining feature is the vivid orange or red belly marbled with black, a warning signal to predators. When threatened, they may arch their backs and limbs in a defensive posture called the "unken reflex" to flash this colour.

Despite their name, they are technically frogs and are far more aquatic than typical toads. They are active during the day, frequently swimming, floating at the surface, and calling with a soft, barking croak. Kept in small groups, they are engaging and rarely shy.

## Habitat & water

A semi-aquatic setup works best, with roughly equal areas of shallow water and dry land. Provide a gentle slope or cork bark so toads can easily climb out. Keep water shallow (1-3 inches) since they are not strong divers, and use a low-flow filter to maintain quality.

Use dechlorinated water only, as tap water chemicals harm amphibian skin. Maintain room temperatures of 70-78F (21-26C); they tolerate a slight night drop and do not need a heat lamp in most homes. Live plants, smooth stones, and floating cork give cover and basking spots. Perform partial water changes weekly to control ammonia.

## Diet & feeding

Fire-Bellied Toads are insectivores. Offer a varied diet of appropriately sized crickets, supplemented with mealworms, waxworms, earthworms, and the occasional bloodworm. Prey should be no wider than the space between the toad's eyes to prevent choking.

Dust insects with a calcium and vitamin D3 supplement at most feedings and a multivitamin once weekly to prevent metabolic bone disease. Feed adults every two to three days and juveniles daily. Remove uneaten prey so it does not foul the water or stress the toads.

## Handling & health

Handle these toads as little as possible. Their skin secretes a mild toxin that can irritate human eyes and mucous membranes, and human skin oils and salts harm the amphibian in return. Always wear clean, wet gloves if you must move them, and wash your hands thoroughly afterward.

Healthy toads are alert, plump, and active with clear skin. Watch for warning signs such as lethargy, bloating, cloudy eyes, weight loss, or reddened skin, which can indicate bacterial infection or poor water quality. Clean water and a varied, supplemented diet prevent most common problems. Consult an exotic-animal veterinarian if symptoms persist.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Fire-Bellied Toads poisonous to touch?** They secrete a mild skin toxin that can irritate eyes, broken skin, and mouths but is not dangerous through brief, careful contact. Wash your hands well before and after any handling, and avoid touching your face.

**Can Fire-Bellied Toads live together?** Yes, they are social and do well in small same-species groups, provided the tank is large enough and feeding is monitored so all individuals eat. Avoid mixing them with other amphibian species.

## Conclusion

Fire-Bellied Toads reward attentive keepers with years of colourful, active companionship. With a clean semi-aquatic tank, dechlorinated water, a supplemented insect diet, and minimal handling, they make an excellent introduction to amphibian care. Always check that keeping them is legal in your region, as exotic-pet rules vary.
