The axolotl (*Ambystoma mexicanum*) is a neotenic salamander that keeps its larval, fully aquatic form for life rather than metamorphosing into a land-dwelling adult. Famous for its feathery external gills and perpetual "smile," it has become a popular aquarium amphibian. With cool, clean water and the right setup, axolotls are hardy and long-lived companions.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|

## Appearance & behaviour

Axolotls have broad heads, lidless eyes, and three pairs of branching external gills behind the head. Common colours include wild-type (dark mottled brown), leucistic (pale pink with dark eyes), albino, and melanoid (solid black). They move slowly along the bottom, occasionally darting to the surface for a gulp of air, since they breathe through gills, skin, and rudimentary lungs. They are largely nocturnal and prefer a quiet tank with hiding spots. Axolotls are best kept singly or with same-sized tankmates, as they may nip each other's gills or limbs. Thanks to their remarkable regenerative ability, lost limbs, gills, and even parts of organs can regrow.

## Habitat & water

Cool water is the single most important requirement. Aim for 60-64 degrees F (16-18 degrees C); temperatures above 72 degrees F (22 degrees C) cause stress and illness, so an aquarium chiller or a cool room is often needed. Keep ammonia and nitrite at zero with a fully cycled tank, and nitrate below 20 ppm through weekly partial water changes. Use a gentle filter, as axolotls dislike strong currents. Choose fine sand or a bare bottom for substrate; loose gravel can be swallowed and cause deadly impaction. Provide caves, PVC pipes, or driftwood for shelter, and dim lighting since they have no eyelids. A pH around 7.4-7.6 and dechlorinated water are essential.

## Diet & feeding

Axolotls are carnivores. Staple foods include earthworms (nightcrawlers), which are nutritious and easy to digest, along with blackworms, soft sinking carnivore pellets, and occasional bloodworms or daphnia. Juveniles eat daily, while adults are typically fed every 2-3 days. Offer only what they finish in a few minutes, and remove uneaten food promptly to protect water quality. Avoid feeder fish, which can carry parasites or disease, and never feed mammalian meat, which is hard to digest. Feeding with tongs or a turkey baster helps target food and keeps the substrate clean.

## Handling & health

Axolotls have soft, delicate skin with no protective scales, so handling should be minimal and only when necessary, using clean wet hands or a soft net. They should never be kept out of water for long. Common health problems stem from poor water quality and heat: stress curls in the gills or tail, refusal to eat, and floating from trapped gas. Fungal patches can often be addressed with cool, clean water and improved husbandry. Persistent floating, sores, or impaction warrant advice from an exotic or aquatic-animal veterinarian.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do axolotls need a heater?** No, they need the opposite. Axolotls thrive in cool water and a heater can quickly overheat them. Most setups instead require a chiller or fan to keep temperatures below 72 degrees F.

**Are axolotls legal to keep?** Legality varies by region. They are banned or restricted in some places, such as California and parts of the United States, so check your local laws before acquiring one.

## Conclusion

Axolotls reward attentive keepers with a decade or more of fascinating, low-drama companionship. The keys to success are cool, pristine water, a safe substrate, gentle filtration, and a simple meaty diet. Master those basics, confirm they are legal where you live, and your axolotl can thrive for many years.
