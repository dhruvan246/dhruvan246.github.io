The Redbone Coonhound is a striking American scenthound, instantly recognizable by its solid, glossy red coat. Bred in the southern United States to trail and tree raccoons and other game over rugged terrain, it pairs a serious working nose with a famously easygoing, affectionate manner at home.

## Overview

| Trait | Details |
|---|---|
| Size | Large (typically about 45–70 lb / 20–32 kg) |
| Lifespan | Around 11–12 years |
| Coat | Short, smooth, dense; solid red |
| Energy | High |
| Good with kids | Yes, generally very good |

## Appearance & temperament

Redbones are well-balanced, athletic hounds standing roughly 21–27 inches at the shoulder. The hallmark is a short, smooth coat in a rich, even red, sometimes with a small white marking on the chest or feet. They carry long, low-set ears, a soulful expression, and an effortless, ground-covering stride.

Temperamentally, the Redbone is friendly, mellow, and devoted to its family. It is typically good-natured with children and sociable with other dogs, especially when raised together. As a scenthound, it can be independent and easily distracted by interesting smells, and many are enthusiastic, melodious barkers and bayers.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet formulated for an active large-breed dog, divided into two meals a day. Portion according to your dog's age, weight, and activity level rather than free-feeding, since hounds are food-motivated and can gain weight when under-exercised. Because this is a deep-chested breed, avoid heavy exercise immediately before or after eating. Keep fresh water available at all times, and consult your veterinarian about the right food and portions for your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

This is a high-energy hunting breed that needs substantial daily exercise: long walks, jogging, hiking, or supervised play in a secure area. A bored, under-exercised Redbone may bark excessively or become destructive. Because their nose can override recall, keep them leashed or in a fenced yard near roads.

Redbones are intelligent but can be independent-minded. Reward-based, patient, and consistent training works best; early socialization and basic obedience pay off. Their strong prey drive means introductions to cats or small pets should be careful and supervised.

## Grooming & care

Grooming needs are modest. The short coat sheds moderately and benefits from a weekly brushing with a hound mitt or soft brush to remove loose hair and distribute oils. Bathe only as needed. Check and gently clean the long, dropped ears regularly, as their shape can trap moisture and debris. Trim nails routinely and maintain regular dental care.

## Health & lifespan

The Redbone Coonhound is generally a hardy, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of around 11 to 12 years. As with many large, deep-chested dogs, owners should be aware of the risk of bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus) and learn its warning signs. Hip dysplasia and ear infections (linked to the pendulous ears) are also worth monitoring. Routine veterinary checkups, parasite prevention, dental care, and weight management support a long, healthy life. Always seek a veterinarian's guidance for any specific health concerns.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Redbone Coonhounds good family dogs?** Yes. They are typically affectionate, even-tempered, and good with children, making them rewarding family companions for owners who can meet their exercise needs.

**Do Redbone Coonhounds bark a lot?** They can be vocal. As scenthounds bred to bay on a trail, many are loud and enthusiastic barkers, especially if bored or under-exercised.

## Conclusion

The Redbone Coonhound rewards active owners with loyalty, an even temperament, and an eye-catching red coat. Give it plenty of exercise, secure containment for its powerful nose, consistent positive training, and routine health care, and it makes a devoted and good-natured family companion.
