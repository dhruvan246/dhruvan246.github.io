Known in its homeland as the Gammel Dansk Hønsehund, the Old Danish Pointer is a sturdy, medium-sized Danish gundog developed to hunt and point game birds. Calmer and steadier than many pointing breeds, it pairs a strong working drive in the field with a notably gentle, affectionate nature at home.

## Appearance & temperament

The Old Danish Pointer is solidly built and rectangular in outline, more powerful and less rangy than slimmer pointers, with a broad head and a calm, attentive expression. The short, close coat is white with brown markings, ranging from large patches to fine ticking. Temperament is one of the breed's defining traits: it is steady, balanced and good-natured, devoted to its family and patient with children. Bred to work closely with the hunter at a measured pace, it tends to be calmer indoors than many gundogs, though it remains alert and keen outdoors.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet matched to the dog's age, size and activity level. A working Old Danish Pointer in the hunting season may need more calories than a companion dog kept at a steadier routine, so adjust portions to maintain a trim, muscular body condition rather than feeding a fixed amount. Dividing the daily ration into two meals and keeping fresh water available are sensible practices. Avoid overfeeding and limit rich treats, as excess weight strains the joints of an active working breed.

## Exercise & training

This is an energetic hunting breed that needs substantial daily exercise — long walks, free running in secure areas and activities that engage its scenting and pointing instincts. While calmer than some pointers, it still requires real physical and mental work to stay content. The breed is intelligent, willing and naturally inclined to cooperate with its handler, which makes it responsive to calm, consistent, reward-based training. Early socialisation and reliable recall are valuable given its strong instinct to track and point birds. A well-exercised Old Danish Pointer settles readily at home.

## Grooming & care

Grooming is straightforward thanks to the short, easy-care coat. Weekly brushing removes loose hair and keeps the coat clean and glossy, with occasional baths when needed. Check the drop ears regularly and keep them clean and dry, since folded ears can trap moisture. Trim nails as they grow, brush the teeth routinely, and after time in the field inspect the coat and paws for ticks, burrs and minor cuts. Overall the breed is low-maintenance in the grooming department.

## Health & lifespan

The Old Danish Pointer is generally a robust, healthy breed, typically living around 11 to 14 years. As with many active sporting dogs, hip dysplasia is a relevant concern, and the drop ears can be prone to infection if not kept clean and dry. Responsible breeders screen breeding stock for hereditary issues, and routine veterinary checkups, parasite control, dental care and healthy weight management all support a long, active life. Any persistent change in appetite, mobility or behaviour warrants a veterinary assessment.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Old Danish Pointer a good family dog?**
Yes. It is known for a calm, affectionate and balanced temperament and generally does very well with children, making it a loyal companion for active families who can meet its exercise needs.

**Does the Old Danish Pointer need a lot of exercise?**
Yes. As a working gundog it needs plenty of daily physical activity and mental stimulation, though it tends to be calmer and easier to settle indoors than many other pointing breeds.

## Conclusion

The Old Danish Pointer is a steady, good-natured gundog that combines genuine working ability with an easygoing family temperament. Give it ample daily exercise, consistent positive training and simple routine care, and this short-coated Danish breed makes a devoted, low-maintenance companion.
