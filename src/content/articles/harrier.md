Athletic, friendly and built for the chase, the Harrier is an English scent hound traditionally used to hunt hares in packs. Often described as looking like a larger Beagle or a smaller English Foxhound, it is a sturdy, tireless medium-sized dog that thrives on activity and company.

## Appearance & temperament

The Harrier is a well-balanced, muscular hound standing roughly 48–55 cm at the shoulder, with a deep chest, strong legs and the powerful, free-moving gait of a working pack dog. It carries low-set, rounded ears and an expressive, gentle face. The short coat is most often tricolour (black, tan and white), though other hound colours occur. By temperament Harriers are outgoing, cheerful and people-oriented, and because they were developed to work cooperatively in packs they generally get along well with other dogs and with children. They are alert and curious rather than aggressive, but their strong prey drive and independent streak mean small pets and off-lead freedom call for caution.

## Diet & nutrition

Active hounds like the Harrier benefit from a steady, well-managed diet.

- Feed a complete, good-quality food suited to the dog's age, size and activity level.
- Measure meals and adjust portions to keep the dog lean and well-muscled.
- Like many hounds, Harriers are food-motivated and can gain weight if overfed, so monitor body condition.
- Count training treats within the daily ration and always provide fresh water, especially after exercise.

## Exercise & training

This is a high-energy working breed that needs substantial daily exercise, comfortably an hour or more of walking, running and play, plus opportunities to use its nose. An under-exercised Harrier can become bored, vocal and destructive, so it suits an active household with space rather than a sedentary one.

Harriers are intelligent but independent, a legacy of being bred to think for themselves in the field. Patient, reward-based training with consistency works best. Recall is a particular challenge, because a Harrier on a scent may ignore commands entirely, so secure fencing and on-lead walks in open areas are important. Early socialisation helps them grow into confident, well-mannered adults.

## Grooming & care

The short, dense coat is easy to look after.

- Brush weekly to remove loose hair and keep the coat healthy; the breed sheds moderately.
- Bathe only when needed.
- Check and gently clean the ears regularly, as their drop shape can trap moisture.
- Keep up with routine nail trims and regular tooth brushing.

## Health & lifespan

Harriers are generally a hardy, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of around 12 to 15 years. As with many medium and larger dogs, hip dysplasia is among the conditions worth being aware of, and their pendulous ears can make ear infections more likely if not kept clean and dry. Maintaining a healthy weight protects their joints over a long working life. Routine veterinary check-ups, parasite prevention and good dental care all support a full, active life. Always consult your veterinarian about any health concerns rather than self-treating.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Harriers good family dogs?**
Yes. Their friendly, sociable, pack-oriented nature makes them good companions for active families, though young children should always be supervised around any dog.

**Do Harriers need a lot of exercise?**
Very much so. They are energetic working hounds that need vigorous daily activity and mental stimulation; without it they can become bored and noisy.

## Conclusion

The Harrier is a cheerful, hard-working medium hound that rewards an active owner with loyalty and good humour. Give one plenty of exercise, secure fencing, consistent positive training and clean, dry ears, and you will have a sturdy, affectionate companion for many years.
