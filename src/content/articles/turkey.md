The domestic turkey (*Meleagris gallopavo*) is a large, sociable farm bird kept for meat, eggs, breeding, and increasingly as a backyard companion. With ample space, good feed, and patient handling, turkeys can become surprisingly tame and engaging members of a small flock.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Size | Hens 8-16 lbs; toms 16-30+ lbs |
| Lifespan | 3-5 years (heritage breeds up to 10) |
| Space needs | Large; 10+ sq ft per bird plus outdoor run |
| Temperament | Curious, social, often friendly |
| Care level | Intermediate |

## Appearance & temperament

Turkeys are heavy-bodied birds with bare, fleshy heads, a dangling snood, and a wattle that flushes red, blue, or white with mood. Toms (males) fan their tail feathers and gobble during courtship; hens are quieter and smaller. Heritage breeds such as Bourbon Red and Narragansett retain natural coloring and the ability to fly short distances, while broad-breasted commercial types are much larger and ground-bound.

Despite their size, turkeys are gentle and inquisitive. Many follow their keepers around, respond to a familiar voice, and tolerate handling once accustomed. Toms can become territorial during breeding season, so supervise interactions with children and other animals.

## Housing & space

Turkeys need more room than chickens. Provide a draft-free, predator-proof coop with at least 10 square feet of indoor space per bird and a secure outdoor run or pasture. Sturdy roosts set a foot or two off the ground suit lighter breeds, though very heavy birds prefer clean, dry bedding on the floor.

Good ventilation, deep straw or shavings, and protection from rain and wind are essential. Fencing should be tall and secure, as agile heritage turkeys can fly over low barriers and predators readily target them.

## Diet & nutrition

Turkeys require higher protein than chickens, especially as poults. Start young birds on a 28% protein game-bird or turkey starter for the first 6-8 weeks, then step down to grower (around 20-24%) and finally a 16-18% maintenance ration for adults. Free feeding is common, but monitor heavy breeds to avoid excess weight.

Supplement with leafy greens, vegetables, and modest fruit, plus grit to aid digestion and a calcium source like oyster shell for laying hens. Provide constant access to clean, fresh water. Avoid moldy feed, avocado, and salty or processed human foods.

## Care & handling

Handle turkeys calmly and from a young age to build trust. Support the body and keep the wings controlled when lifting; never grab by the legs or wings alone. Keep bedding clean to prevent foot problems, trim overgrown nails if needed, and watch for pasted vents in young poults.

Turkeys thrive on routine and company, so keep at least two together. Offer dust-bathing areas and enrichment such as perches, low platforms, and foraging space.

## Health & lifespan

With good care, turkeys live 3-5 years, and lighter heritage breeds may reach 10. They are vulnerable to blackhead disease (histomoniasis), so avoid housing them with chickens that can carry the parasite. Watch for respiratory illness, mites, lice, and leg or foot issues common in fast-growing heavy breeds.

Signs of trouble include lethargy, ruffled feathers, drop in appetite, diarrhea, or labored breathing. Quarantine new or sick birds and consult an avian or poultry veterinarian for diagnosis and treatment rather than self-medicating.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do turkeys get along with chickens?** They can share space, but it is risky because chickens may carry blackhead disease, which is often fatal to turkeys. Many keepers house them separately.

**Are turkeys legal to keep at home?** In most rural and many suburban areas, yes, but local zoning, flock-size limits, and noise rules vary, so check municipal regulations before starting a flock.

## Conclusion

Turkeys reward attentive keepers with personality, useful eggs and meat, and entertaining flock dynamics. Give them roomy, predator-proof housing, high-protein feed, clean water, and gentle handling, and a healthy turkey flock can flourish for years.
