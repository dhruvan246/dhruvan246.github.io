Miniature pigs, often sold as "teacup" or "mini" pigs, are intelligent, social farm animals that have become popular companion pets. Despite marketing claims, even the smallest adults are substantial animals requiring serious commitment.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Size | 30-65 kg (66-150 lb); 35-50 cm tall |
| Lifespan | 12-18 years |
| Space needs | Secure outdoor yard plus indoor shelter |
| Temperament | Smart, social, curious, food-motivated |
| Care level | Moderate to high |

## Appearance & temperament

Miniature pigs are simply small breeds of domestic pig, including the Vietnamese pot-bellied, Juliana, and Kunekune types. They have stout bodies, short legs, a sloping snout, and coarse hair that ranges from black and pink to spotted patterns. "Teacup" is a marketing term, not a real breed; piglets that look tiny grow into pigs weighing well over 45 kg.

They are highly intelligent, comparable to dogs in problem-solving, and form strong bonds with their keepers. Pigs are social herd animals and generally do best with companionship, whether another pig or attentive human interaction. They can be affectionate but also stubborn and territorial without proper boundaries.

## Housing & space

Pigs need a secure outdoor area to root, graze, and exercise, with sturdy fencing because they are strong and persistent escape artists. Provide a draft-free, insulated shelter with deep straw bedding for warmth, as pigs are sensitive to both cold and heat. A shaded area and a wallow or mud spot help them regulate temperature, since pigs cannot sweat effectively.

Indoors, pigs need space to move, a designated bathroom area (they can be litter-trained), and protection from slippery floors that strain their joints. Always check local zoning laws, as many areas classify pigs as livestock and prohibit them in residential zones.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a commercial mini-pig pellet formulated for their nutritional needs, supplemented with fresh vegetables and leafy greens. Avoid feeding standard pig grower feed meant for rapid fattening, and never free-feed, as obesity is the single most common health problem in pet pigs. Limit fruit and high-calorie treats.

Provide constant access to clean water and opportunities to root for food, which is a natural, enriching behavior. Do not feed salty, sugary, or processed human foods, and avoid chocolate and toxic plants.

## Care & handling

Pigs respond well to positive, reward-based training and enjoy mental challenges like puzzle feeders and rooting boxes. Hooves need periodic trimming, and tusks in males may require management. Acclimate young pigs to gentle handling early, but respect that most pigs dislike being picked up.

Daily routine, social interaction, and enrichment prevent boredom-driven behaviors such as destructive rooting or aggression. Spaying or neutering greatly improves temperament and reduces health risks.

## Health & lifespan

With good care, miniature pigs commonly live 12-18 years. Common issues include obesity, arthritis, skin conditions, respiratory problems, and dental disease. Overgrown hooves and tusks cause discomfort if neglected.

Find a veterinarian experienced with pigs before adopting, as not all clinics treat them. Routine vaccinations, parasite control, and weight monitoring support a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do miniature pigs really stay tiny?** No. "Teacup" pigs are a myth; healthy adults typically weigh 30-65 kg, far larger than buyers often expect.

**Can a miniature pig live indoors?** They can be house-trained, but they still need outdoor space to root and exercise, plus a warm, secure shelter.

## Conclusion

Miniature pigs are clever, affectionate companions that reward dedicated owners, but they are long-lived, sizable animals with real needs. Confirm local laws, plan for their adult size, and commit to proper diet and vet care before bringing one home.
