Easily recognised by its mass of tight curls, the Curly-Coated Retriever is one of the oldest retriever breeds. Bred to retrieve game from land and water, this confident, athletic dog combines a sporting heritage with a loyal, independent nature that suits active owners.

## Appearance & temperament

The Curly-Coated Retriever is a tall, well-balanced dog covered almost entirely in small, crisp curls, typically in solid black or liver. The curly coat is the breed's signature feature and provides protection in water and dense cover. Built for endurance, the breed is leaner and more upstanding than many other retrievers.

In temperament, Curlies are intelligent, self-assured and somewhat more independent and reserved than the Labrador or Golden Retriever. They tend to be devoted to their families and can be aloof with strangers, but are rarely aggressive. Early socialisation helps them grow into confident, well-mannered companions.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food appropriate to your dog's age, size and activity level. As an active large breed, the Curly-Coated Retriever benefits from quality food that supports a lean, muscular build. Measure portions and avoid overfeeding, since excess weight strains the joints of any large dog.

Splitting the daily ration into two meals and providing constant fresh water is a sensible routine. Your veterinarian can advise on the right diet and portion size for your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

This is a high-energy sporting breed that needs substantial daily exercise. Long walks, running, swimming and retrieving games help satisfy both physical and mental needs. A bored or under-exercised Curly may become restless or develop unwanted habits.

Curlies are clever but can be independent thinkers, so training works best with patience, consistency and positive, reward-based methods. They enjoy having a job to do and often excel at activities such as retrieving, scent work and canine sports. Begin socialisation and basic training early.

## Grooming & care

Despite its dramatic look, the curly coat is fairly low-maintenance compared with longer-haired breeds. The curls generally do not need brushing in the conventional sense; over-brushing can cause frizz. Many owners simply wet the coat or trim stray hairs occasionally. The breed sheds seasonally.

As with all dogs, check and clean the ears regularly, especially after swimming, trim nails as needed, and maintain routine dental care.

## Health & lifespan

The Curly-Coated Retriever is generally a hardy breed with a typical lifespan of around 9 to 12 years. Like many large and deep-chested dogs, it can be prone to conditions such as hip and elbow dysplasia, certain eye disorders, and bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus). Responsible breeders screen breeding stock for known issues.

Routine veterinary check-ups, parasite prevention, appropriate exercise and a healthy weight all support a long, comfortable life. Always consult your veterinarian about any health concerns.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does the Curly-Coated Retriever shed a lot?**
The breed sheds, often more noticeably during seasonal changes, but its tight curls tend to make loose hair less obvious than with some other large breeds. It is not considered hypoallergenic.

**Is the Curly-Coated Retriever a good family dog?**
Yes. Curlies are typically loyal and good with children when properly socialised and supervised, though their high energy and independent streak suit active, engaged owners best.

## Conclusion

The Curly-Coated Retriever is a striking, athletic and devoted companion for owners who can meet its exercise and mental-stimulation needs. With early training, regular activity and routine health care, this distinctive retriever can be a rewarding partner for an active household.
