The Argentine tegu (genus *Salvator*, most often the black-and-white *Salvator merianae*) is one of the largest and most intelligent lizards kept as a pet. Known for its dog-like curiosity and ability to recognize its keeper, it rewards dedicated owners but demands serious space, heat, and commitment.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 3–4.5 ft long (males larger) |
| Lifespan | 15–20 years |
| Enclosure size | 8x4 ft floor minimum for an adult |
| Temperament | Generally calm and tameable, but powerful |
| Care level | Intermediate to advanced |

## Appearance & temperament

Argentine black-and-white tegus have a stocky, muscular body, a thick tail, and a beaded pattern of black and white scales arranged in broken bands. Hatchlings often show a green tint on the head that fades within months. Adult males develop pronounced jowls.

Tegus are notably intelligent and, with consistent gentle interaction, become some of the most trusting reptiles available. That said, they are strong animals with sharp claws and a firm bite, so respect and patience are essential.

## Habitat & enclosure

Tegus are terrestrial and need floor space far more than height. Hatchlings can start in a 40-gallon enclosure, but an adult requires a custom-built cage of at least 8 feet by 4 feet. A deep substrate of cypress mulch, topsoil, or a soil-sand mix lets them burrow, which is a natural and important behavior. Provide a sturdy hide and a water dish large enough for the lizard to soak in.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Create a thermal gradient. A basking surface of 100–115°F (38–46°C) is needed, with a cooler ambient zone around 80–85°F (27–29°C) and nighttime temperatures no lower than the low 70s°F. Use overhead halogen flood bulbs for basking heat rather than heat mats, since tegus absorb warmth from above.

A strong UVB source is essential for vitamin D3 synthesis and healthy bones; use a high-output linear UVB tube spanning much of the enclosure. Maintain humidity around 60–80% by misting and using moisture-retentive substrate.

## Diet & feeding

Argentine tegus are omnivores, and a varied diet is key. Offer whole prey such as appropriately sized rodents, insects, raw eggs, and lean meats, along with fruits and some vegetables. Younger tegus eat more protein and insects, while adults benefit from more fruit and plant matter. Dust feedings with calcium and use a vitamin supplement as appropriate. Feed juveniles frequently; adults can be fed a few times per week. Obesity is common, so monitor body condition.

## Handling & health

Tame tegus generally tolerate handling well once they trust you. Support the body fully, never grab the tail, and let the animal come to you during early sessions. Wash your hands before and after contact, as reptiles can carry *Salmonella*.

Common health concerns include metabolic bone disease from poor UVB or calcium, obesity, and respiratory infections from incorrect temperatures. Tegus brumate (a dormant winter slowdown) for weeks to months, during which appetite and activity drop sharply. Find an exotics-experienced veterinarian before problems arise.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Argentine tegus legal to own?**
Legality varies widely by region. Some U.S. states and many countries restrict or ban tegus, partly because escaped animals have become invasive. Always confirm local laws before acquiring one.

**Do tegus really bond with their owners?**
Many keepers report genuine recognition and calm, trusting behavior with regular interaction. While not affection in a human sense, tegus are among the most responsive reptiles to their caretakers.

## Conclusion

The Argentine tegu is a rewarding companion for keepers ready to provide a large enclosure, strong heating and UVB, a varied diet, and years of patient handling. With proper care, this clever, personable lizard can thrive as a long-lived member of the household.
