The Pacman Frog (genus *Ceratophrys*), also called the South American horned frog, is one of the most popular pet amphibians. Named after the round, wide-mouthed arcade character, it is a sit-and-wait ambush predator that spends most of its life half-buried, waiting for a meal to wander close.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Size | Females 4-7 in; males smaller, around 2.5-4 in |
| Lifespan | 6-10 years, sometimes longer with good care |
| Enclosure | 10-20 gallon terrarium for one adult |
| Temperament | Solitary, sedentary, will bite if provoked |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & behaviour

Pacman Frogs have a squat, rounded body that is nearly as wide as it is long, dominated by an enormous mouth. Captive-bred lines come in many colours, including green, brown, albino (apricot), and the high-contrast "strawberry" morph. Some species have small fleshy "horns" above the eyes.

Behaviourally they are famously inactive. A healthy frog will burrow into the substrate with only its eyes and mouth exposed, ambushing prey with a fast lunge. They are not social and must be housed alone, as they are cannibalistic and will attempt to eat tank mates of any species.

## Habitat & water

House a single adult in a 10-20 gallon terrarium with more floor space than height. Provide 2-4 inches of moisture-retaining substrate such as coconut fibre or sphagnum moss so the frog can burrow. Keep humidity high, around 60-80%, by misting regularly.

Maintain daytime temperatures of about 75-82°F (24-28°C), with a slight night drop. Avoid hot basking spots. Because amphibians absorb water through their skin, always supply a shallow dish of dechlorinated water and never use chlorinated tap water directly. Spot-clean waste daily and replace substrate regularly to prevent harmful bacteria and ammonia buildup.

## Diet & feeding

Pacman Frogs are voracious carnivores. Juveniles can be fed every 1-2 days; adults need feeding only every 3-7 days, as they are prone to obesity. Offer appropriately sized prey such as crickets, dubia roaches, earthworms, and the occasional pinky mouse for large adults. A good rule is that prey should be no wider than the space between the frog's eyes.

Dust insect prey with a calcium supplement, and use a calcium-with-vitamin-D3 and multivitamin supplement periodically to prevent metabolic bone disease. Always use a feeding routine rather than free-feeding, since these frogs will eat until they harm themselves.

## Handling & health

Pacman Frogs are display pets, not handling pets. Their permeable skin readily absorbs oils, lotions, and toxins, so handle only when necessary, with clean wet hands or moistened gloves, and keep it brief. They have a strong bite and may latch on if they mistake fingers for food.

Common health issues include obesity from overfeeding, metabolic bone disease from poor supplementation, impaction from loose substrate, and bacterial skin infections from dirty conditions. A frog that stops eating, sits in water constantly, or sheds excessively may be unwell and should be seen by an exotics or reptile-and-amphibian veterinarian.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Pacman Frogs need a water filter?**
No. They are not aquatic and do not live in deep water. Instead they need a shallow water dish and consistently damp substrate, with the water changed daily.

**Are Pacman Frogs legal to own?**
In many regions they are widely available captive-bred and legal, but amphibian laws vary by country, state, and city. Always confirm local regulations before purchasing one.

## Conclusion

The Pacman Frog is a hardy, low-maintenance amphibian that rewards keepers with a striking appearance and a fascinating ambush-feeding lifestyle. Give it a humid burrowing terrarium, dechlorinated water, a controlled feeding schedule, and minimal handling, and a well-kept frog can thrive for many years.
