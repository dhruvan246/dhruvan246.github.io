The domestic goose is one of the oldest farmed birds, kept for centuries for meat, eggs, down, and weeding. Hardy, intelligent, and surprisingly affectionate when raised well, geese make rewarding additions to a homestead. Most breeds descend from the European Greylag (*Anser anser*) or the Asian Swan goose (*Anser cygnoides*), the ancestor of Chinese and African breeds.

## Appearance & temperament

Geese are large waterfowl with long necks, broad bills, and webbed feet. Plumage ranges from pure white (Embden, Roman) to grey (Toulouse, Greylag) and buff or pied patterns (Pilgrim, Pomeranian). Some Chinese and African breeds carry a distinctive knob at the base of the bill. They are flock animals that bond strongly with their keepers and one another, and they grieve lost companions. Geese are famously good "watchdogs," honking loudly at strangers. Ganders (males) can become assertive during breeding season but settle when handled calmly from a young age.

## Housing & space

Geese are primarily grazers and need access to a grassy run or pasture, ideally several hundred square feet per bird. They require a predator-proof shelter at night, dry and draft-free, with clean straw bedding and roughly 8-10 square feet of floor space per goose. Sturdy fencing at least 3 feet high keeps them contained and deters foxes and dogs. While geese do not strictly need a swimming pond, they thrive with water deep enough to dip their heads and clean their nostrils and eyes; a child's wading pool refreshed regularly works well.

## Diet & nutrition

A goose's natural diet is grass, and good pasture can supply much of their intake in the growing season. Supplement with a waterfowl or poultry pellet (avoid medicated chick feed, which can harm them) and offer leafy greens, vegetables, and whole grains like wheat. Provide insoluble grit to aid digestion and crushed oyster shell for laying females. Fresh, clean water must always be available alongside food, since geese need water to swallow. Avoid bread, salty foods, and moldy feed.

## Care & handling

Keep at least two geese, as a lone goose becomes lonely and stressed. Handle goslings gently and often to build trust. To pick up a goose, restrain the wings against the body and support the breast, never lifting by the legs, neck, or wings. Trim nothing routinely, but check feet, eyes, and vent during regular health inspections. Geese are weather-hardy and tolerate cold well, but provide shade and ample water in heat. Clean shelters and water containers frequently to limit disease.

## Health & lifespan

Well-kept geese are robust and can live 15-25 years, with some reaching their thirties. Common issues include bumblefoot (foot infections from hard or dirty surfaces), parasites such as worms and lice, angel wing in fast-growing goslings fed too much protein, and impacted crops. Provide clean dry footing, routine deworming as advised locally, and balanced feed to prevent most problems. A vet experienced with poultry or waterfowl should evaluate lameness, weight loss, or respiratory signs promptly.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do geese need a pond to be healthy?** No. They do not require deep swimming water, but they must have water deep enough to submerge their heads to keep their eyes and nostrils clean. A shallow pool or tub works fine.

**Are geese aggressive pets?** They can be territorial, especially ganders in breeding season, but geese raised with gentle, consistent handling are calm and even affectionate. Most "aggression" is protective behavior toward their flock.

## Conclusion

Domestic geese are long-lived, low-cost grazers that reward attentive keepers with eggs, weeding, companionship, and a vigilant alarm system. Given a companion, secure shelter, good pasture, clean water, and routine health checks, a small flock can thrive for decades as one of the most characterful residents of any farm or backyard.
