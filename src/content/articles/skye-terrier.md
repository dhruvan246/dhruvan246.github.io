The Skye Terrier is one of Scotland's oldest terrier breeds, instantly recognizable for its long, low body, draping coat, and feathered ears. Originally bred on the Isle of Skye to hunt fox, badger, and otter, this small but sturdy dog combines terrier grit with an unusually loyal, dignified personality.

## Appearance & temperament

The Skye Terrier is twice as long as it is tall, weighing roughly 25–40 pounds (11–18 kg) despite its short stature. Its hallmark is a long, hard-textured outer coat over a soft undercoat, with hair that falls in a part down the back and veils the eyes. Ears may be either prick (erect) or drop, both heavily feathered. Coat colors include black, blue, gray, fawn, and cream, usually with darker points.

In temperament, the Skye is loyal and devoted, often bonding deeply with one person or family. It is reserved and cautious with strangers, fearless when challenged, and far calmer indoors than many terriers. Early socialization helps temper its naturally wary streak.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food appropriate for the Skye's age and activity level. Because of its low-slung frame and long back, keeping the dog at a lean, healthy weight is especially important to reduce strain on the spine. Measure meals rather than free-feeding, and divide the daily amount into two servings. Limit treats to a small fraction of daily calories, and provide constant access to fresh water. Consult your veterinarian about portion sizes suited to your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

Skye Terriers need moderate daily exercise: a couple of walks and some play usually satisfy them. They enjoy sniffing and exploring but are not high-endurance dogs, and puppies' joints and backs should be protected from repeated jumping or stair-climbing while growing.

Training requires patience. The breed is intelligent but independent and strong-willed, so short, positive, reward-based sessions work best. Harsh corrections tend to make a Skye shut down or grow stubborn. Consistent early socialization builds confidence around new people and other animals.

## Grooming & care

The long coat needs regular brushing—several times a week—to prevent mats and tangles, with extra attention behind the ears and on the legs. Many owners trim hair around the eyes and feet for comfort. Bathe as needed, and keep nails trimmed, ears clean, and teeth brushed regularly. The breed sheds modestly. Because the coat hides the body, run your hands over the dog during grooming to check skin and condition.

## Health & lifespan

Skye Terriers typically live 12–14 years and are generally hardy. Like other long-backed breeds, they can be prone to intervertebral disc issues, so discourage jumping from heights. The breed is also associated with a liver condition sometimes called "Skye limp" or hepatitis in some lines, along with occasional luxating patella and orthopedic concerns. Routine veterinary checkups, dental care, and weight management support a long, comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Skye Terriers good apartment dogs?**
Yes. Their moderate energy and calm indoor manner suit apartments, provided they get daily walks and you protect their long backs from excessive stairs and jumping.

**Do Skye Terriers shed a lot?**
They shed only moderately, but their long double coat needs frequent brushing to stay free of mats and tangles, so grooming time is significant.

## Conclusion

The Skye Terrier is a devoted, dignified companion wrapped in a striking flowing coat. With consistent grooming, gentle training, weight management, and care for its long back, this rare Scottish terrier rewards owners with steadfast loyalty and quiet charm for many years.
