The Senegal Parrot (*Poicephalus senegalus*) is a small, stocky African parrot prized for its quiet nature, charming personality, and manageable size. Native to the woodlands of West Africa, it has become a popular companion bird for those who want an engaging parrot without the volume of larger species.

## Overview

| Trait | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Size | Roughly 9–10 inches (23–25 cm) long |
| Lifespan | Commonly 20–30 years in good care |
| Noise level | Low to moderate; quieter than many parrots |
| Talking ability | Limited but capable of a few words and sounds |
| Care level | Intermediate; suits attentive beginners |

## Appearance & temperament

Senegal Parrots have a charcoal-grey head, green wings and back, and a yellow-to-orange V-shaped patch across the chest and belly. Their eyes are typically yellow in mature birds. Compact and short-tailed, they are sturdy little parrots.

Temperamentally, they are intelligent, curious, and often deeply bonded to one person. Many are calmer and less needy than larger parrots, but they can become territorial or "one-person" birds without regular, varied socialization. Early, consistent handling encourages a friendly, confident companion.

## Housing & cage setup

Provide the largest cage your space allows, with a minimum interior big enough for the bird to fully spread and flap its wings. Bar spacing of about 5/8 inch (1.5 cm) prevents escapes and injuries. Include perches of varying diameters and natural materials to support foot health.

Place the cage in a social part of the home, away from drafts, direct sun, and kitchen fumes. Offer daily out-of-cage time in a supervised, bird-safe room. Avoid non-stick cookware fumes and aerosols, which are dangerous to all birds.

## Diet & nutrition

A high-quality formulated pellet should form the foundation of the diet, supplemented with a variety of fresh vegetables and modest amounts of fruit. Offer leafy greens, carrots, peppers, and cooked legumes. Seeds and nuts are fine in small amounts as treats but should not dominate the diet, as they are high in fat.

Always provide clean, fresh water. Never feed avocado, chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, or salty foods, all of which are toxic to parrots.

## Enrichment & handling

Senegals are smart and need daily mental stimulation. Rotate foraging toys, chewable wood, shreddable paper, and puzzle feeders to prevent boredom and feather-plucking. Short, positive training sessions build trust and reinforce step-up and recall behaviors.

Handle your bird gently and consistently. Respect its moods, watch body language, and avoid forcing interaction. Regular, calm handling keeps a Senegal sociable rather than nippy.

## Health & lifespan

With proper care, Senegal Parrots often live two to three decades. Signs of illness include fluffed feathers, lethargy, appetite loss, or changes in droppings; birds hide sickness, so any change warrants attention. Schedule routine checkups with an avian veterinarian and seek prompt care for concerns. Provide stable temperatures, a clean environment, and a balanced diet to support long-term health.

As with any exotic bird, legality and import rules vary by region, and they require specialist avian care, so research local regulations and locate an avian vet before acquiring one.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Senegal Parrots good for apartments?** Yes. Their relatively low noise level makes them better suited to apartments than many louder parrot species, though they still vocalize daily.

**Do Senegal Parrots talk?** Some learn a handful of words and mimic household sounds, but they are not strong talkers. They communicate more through whistles and body language.

## Conclusion

The Senegal Parrot is an affectionate, intelligent companion that fits households wanting a quieter, smaller parrot. With a spacious cage, balanced diet, daily enrichment, and avian veterinary care, these charming birds can thrive as devoted companions for many years.
