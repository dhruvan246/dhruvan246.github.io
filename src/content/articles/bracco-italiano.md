The Bracco Italiano is one of the oldest pointing breeds in Europe, a versatile Italian gundog prized for its keen nose and gentle, affectionate nature. With its distinctive long ears, loose skin and dignified expression, the "Bracco" combines serious hunting ability with the temperament of a devoted family companion.

## Appearance & temperament

The Bracco Italiano is a substantial, athletic dog with a muscular build, a noble head, pendulous lips and long, low-set ears. Males generally stand around 58–67 cm at the shoulder and females a little smaller, with weight scaling accordingly. The short coat comes in white, white with orange or chestnut markings, or roan patterns.

Temperamentally, the breed is known for being sweet-natured, sociable and eager to please. Braccos form strong bonds with their families and are typically gentle with children and other dogs. They can be sensitive and somewhat stubborn, so they respond best to patient, consistent handling rather than harsh correction.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet appropriate for a large, active breed, divided into two meals a day rather than one large serving. Portion size should match your dog's age, weight and activity level, with growing puppies fed a large-breed puppy formula to support steady growth.

As a deep-chested breed, the Bracco may be at risk of bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus), so avoid vigorous exercise immediately before and after meals. Keep fresh water available and monitor body condition, since extra weight strains the joints. Consult your veterinarian for guidance tailored to your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

Bred to range over the field for hours, the Bracco Italiano has considerable stamina and needs substantial daily exercise. Long walks, free running in safe areas, scent games and ideally some form of fieldwork help keep this dog physically and mentally satisfied. An under-exercised Bracco can become bored and restless.

Training should start early and lean on positive, reward-based methods. These dogs are intelligent and willing but sensitive, so calm, encouraging sessions work far better than pressure. Early socialisation builds a confident, well-mannered adult.

## Grooming & care

The short coat is low-maintenance and benefits from a weekly brush to remove loose hair and distribute natural oils, with baths only as needed. The breed's long, floppy ears trap moisture and debris, so check and clean them regularly to reduce the chance of ear infections.

Routine care also includes nail trimming, dental hygiene and wiping the facial folds and lips, which can collect food and saliva. Keep an eye on the eyes, as the loose lower lids can be prone to irritation.

## Health & lifespan

The Bracco Italiano is generally a hardy breed with a typical lifespan of roughly 11 to 14 years. Like many large breeds, it can be predisposed to hip and elbow dysplasia, and its deep chest carries a risk of bloat. Ear and eye issues linked to its conformation may also occur.

Buying from a responsible breeder who screens breeding stock, plus regular veterinary check-ups, supports a long, healthy life. Always seek professional veterinary advice for any health concern.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Bracco Italiano a good family dog?**
Yes. The breed is affectionate, gentle and patient, usually getting along well with children and other dogs when properly socialised. It thrives on companionship and prefers to be involved in family life.

**How much exercise does a Bracco Italiano need?**
A lot. As a working gundog, it needs at least one to two hours of varied daily activity, including walks, off-lead running and mental stimulation, to stay content and well-behaved.

## Conclusion

The Bracco Italiano is a handsome, affectionate gundog that rewards active owners with loyalty and a calm home presence. Given plenty of exercise, gentle training and attentive care of its ears and joints, this ancient Italian breed makes a wonderful, devoted companion.
