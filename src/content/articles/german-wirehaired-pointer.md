The German Wirehaired Pointer (GWP) is a rugged, all-purpose hunting dog bred in Germany to point, retrieve and work in tough terrain and cold water. Distinguished by its harsh, weather-resistant coat and bushy facial furnishings, the GWP is an energetic, loyal companion for active households.

## Appearance & temperament

The GWP is a medium-to-large, muscular dog standing roughly 56–68 cm at the shoulder. Its signature feature is a functional double coat: a dense undercoat beneath a harsh, wiry outer coat that repels water and brush. A bearded muzzle and pronounced eyebrows give the breed its distinctive expression. Coats are typically liver and white, often ticked or roan.

Bred as a versatile gundog, the GWP is intelligent, determined and devoted to its family. The breed tends to bond strongly with its people and can be aloof or reserved with strangers. GWPs are affectionate at home but need a job to do; without enough activity they can become restless or stubborn.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet appropriate for an active large-breed dog, divided into two daily meals rather than one large portion. Adjust quantities to body condition, age and activity level, since hard-working dogs need more calories than couch companions. As a deep-chested breed, the GWP can be prone to bloat, so avoid heavy exercise right before and after meals. Keep fresh water available at all times, and consult your veterinarian before changing diets.

## Exercise & training

This is a high-energy athlete that needs a great deal of daily exercise — well over an hour of running, swimming, retrieving or dog sports. A short neighborhood walk is rarely enough. GWPs excel at field work, agility and tracking, and they thrive when their bodies and minds are both engaged.

Highly intelligent and eager, the breed responds best to consistent, positive, reward-based training started early. Early socialization helps temper their natural wariness of strangers. A bored or under-exercised GWP may dig, bark or chew, so channel that drive into structured activity.

## Grooming & care

The wiry coat is fairly low-maintenance but benefits from weekly brushing to remove loose hair and debris. Many owners hand-strip the coat periodically to preserve its harsh texture rather than clipping it. Check and dry the beard after meals and water work, and inspect the drop ears regularly to help prevent infections. Routine nail trimming and dental care round out a simple grooming schedule.

## Health & lifespan

The GWP is generally a robust, athletic breed with a typical lifespan of around 12–14 years. Like many large, deep-chested dogs, it can be susceptible to hip dysplasia, gastric bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus), ear infections and certain eye and thyroid conditions. Choosing a responsible breeder who health-tests their lines, keeping your dog at a healthy weight and maintaining regular veterinary checkups all support a long, active life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are German Wirehaired Pointers good family dogs?**
Yes, for active families who can meet their substantial exercise needs. They are loyal and affectionate but may be reserved with strangers.

**Do German Wirehaired Pointers shed a lot?**
They shed moderately. Their wiry coat needs weekly brushing and occasional hand-stripping rather than heavy grooming.

## Conclusion

The German Wirehaired Pointer is a hardy, intelligent and devoted gundog built for work and the outdoors. Give this breed plenty of exercise, early training and companionship, and it will reward you with a versatile, loyal partner for years to come.
