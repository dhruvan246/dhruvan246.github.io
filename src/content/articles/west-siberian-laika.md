The West Siberian Laika is a rugged spitz-type hunting dog developed in the forests and taiga of the Ural and West Siberian regions of Russia. Bred to track and bay large game and tree small fur-bearing animals, it remains a working dog at heart: independent, energetic, and deeply tied to outdoor activity.

## Overview

| Trait | Detail |
|---|---|

## Appearance & temperament

The West Siberian Laika has a classic spitz outline: erect triangular ears, a wedge-shaped head, almond-shaped eyes, and a curled or sickle tail carried over the back. The harsh outer coat and soft, thick undercoat come in shades of grey, white, red, black, and various combinations, often with patches or speckling.

Temperamentally, this breed is alert, confident, and intelligent, with a strong independent streak typical of hunting spitz. They tend to bond closely with their family and can be reserved or watchful toward strangers, making them natural watchdogs. Their pronounced prey drive means small pets and free-roaming situations require care.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet appropriate for an active medium-sized dog. Because these dogs are working hunters with high energy needs, portion sizes should reflect actual activity levels, which may rise sharply during hunting or training seasons and fall during quieter periods. Divide daily food into two meals to support steady energy and reduce the risk of bloat. Always provide fresh water, and adjust intake to keep the dog lean and well-muscled rather than heavy. Consult your veterinarian for guidance tailored to age and workload.

## Exercise & training

This is not a low-maintenance breed. The West Siberian Laika needs substantial daily exercise: long walks, running, hiking, and stimulating activity that engages both body and mind. A bored, under-exercised Laika can become vocal, restless, or destructive.

Training should start early and rely on consistency, patience, and positive reinforcement. They are smart and capable but can be stubborn and self-directed, a legacy of working at a distance from hunters. Reliable recall is challenging given their prey drive, so secure fencing and leashed walks in unfenced areas are wise.

## Grooming & care

The double coat is largely self-maintaining but sheds heavily, especially during seasonal coat blows in spring and autumn, when daily brushing helps manage loose hair. Outside those periods, weekly brushing is usually sufficient. Bathe only as needed, since the coat carries natural weather resistance. Routine care includes nail trimming, dental hygiene, and regular checks of the ears, particularly in dogs that spend time in the field.

## Health & lifespan

The West Siberian Laika is generally a hardy, robust breed with few widespread hereditary problems, partly because it was shaped by working function rather than show fashion. As with many active dogs, owners should stay alert to joint conditions such as hip dysplasia, eye issues, and ear infections. Keeping the dog at a healthy weight, providing ample exercise, and maintaining routine veterinary checkups support a typical lifespan of around 12 to 14 years.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the West Siberian Laika a good family dog?**
They can be loyal, affectionate family companions, especially in active households. Their high energy, strong prey drive, and independent nature suit owners who can provide consistent training and plenty of outdoor activity.

**Do West Siberian Laikas bark a lot?**
They are naturally alert and vocal, often barking to signal activity or unfamiliar people. Adequate exercise, mental stimulation, and early training help keep barking at a manageable level.

## Conclusion

The West Siberian Laika is a hardy, intelligent working dog best suited to active owners who can meet its considerable exercise and engagement needs. Given purpose, space, and consistent guidance, it rewards its family with loyalty, vigilance, and tireless companionship in the outdoors.
