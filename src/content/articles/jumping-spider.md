Jumping spiders (family Salticidae) have become beloved invertebrate pets thanks to their large forward-facing eyes, alert personalities, and apparent curiosity toward their keepers. Species like the bold jumper (*Phidippus audax*) and regal jumper (*Phidippus regius*) are popular because they are hardy and easy to observe. This guide covers the essentials of keeping one healthy.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Size | 0.4–2.2 cm body length, depending on species |
| Lifespan | About 6 months to 2 years (females longer) |
| Housing | Small ventilated vertical enclosure |
| Temperament | Curious, alert, generally non-aggressive |
| Care level | Beginner to intermediate |

## Appearance & behaviour

Jumping spiders are compact, fuzzy arachnids with eight eyes arranged so that a large central pair gives them excellent forward vision. This sharp eyesight lets them stalk and pounce on prey rather than rely on a web. They are diurnal and active during the day, frequently turning to track movement. Unlike web-building spiders, they hunt by leaping, anchoring themselves with a silk dragline as a safety tether. Many keepers find them expressive because they visibly orient toward stimuli.

## Housing & environment

A small enclosure of roughly 1–4 litres is appropriate; bigger is not better, as it makes finding food harder. Choose a vertical (taller than wide) container with plenty of cross-ventilation, since jumping spiders build silken retreats high up. Add fake or live plants, twigs, and cork bark for climbing and anchoring. Maintain room temperature between about 20–26°C and provide light cross-ventilation to prevent stale, humid air. Most species do well at moderate humidity; a light misting on one wall every few days offers drinking droplets without soaking the enclosure.

## Diet & feeding

Jumping spiders eat live, appropriately sized insects. Common feeders include flightless fruit flies for spiderlings and small crickets, mealworms, roaches, or house flies for adults. A good rule is to offer prey no larger than the spider's body. Feed adults every 2–4 days and slings more often; a well-fed spider has a plump abdomen. Remove uneaten prey, as crickets can stress or injure a molting spider. Always provide a few water droplets rather than a deep dish, which poses a drowning risk.

## Handling & care

Jumping spiders can become accustomed to gentle interaction, often walking calmly onto a hand. Keep sessions low and over a soft surface, since a fall can be fatal. Move slowly and never grab or restrain them. Bites are rare, minor, and comparable to a small sting for most people. Routine care is simple: spot-clean prey remains, refresh water droplets, and observe daily for normal activity and appetite.

## Health

The most delicate period is molting, when the spider sheds its exoskeleton to grow. It will often seal itself in a silk retreat, stop eating, and appear motionless for days—do not disturb it or offer food during this time. A failed molt, dehydration, or injury from oversized prey are the main risks. Signs of trouble include a shrunken abdomen, lethargy, or curled legs. Keep humidity adequate and prey appropriately sized to prevent most problems.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are jumping spiders dangerous to keep?** No. They are not medically significant to humans; bites are uncommon and typically cause only mild, temporary irritation similar to a minor insect sting.

**Is it legal to keep a jumping spider?** Many regions allow native species, but laws on collecting wildlife or importing exotic species vary widely. Always check your local regulations before acquiring one.

## Conclusion

Jumping spiders are rewarding, low-cost pets that reward attentive keepers with engaging behaviour and surprising personality. With a well-ventilated enclosure, correctly sized live prey, occasional misting, and respect for their molting cycle, these tiny hunters can thrive in captivity for their full natural lifespan.
