Developed in the 1940s by Czech breeder Frantisek Horak, the Cesky Terrier (also called the Bohemian Terrier) was created by crossing Sealyham and Scottish Terriers to produce a leaner, more cooperative hunting dog. The result is a calm, good-natured terrier that is noticeably more laid-back than many of its scrappier cousins, making it a pleasant companion for families and apartment dwellers alike.

## Appearance & temperament

The Cesky Terrier is a small, sturdy dog with short legs and a long body, typically weighing 14–24 pounds (6–11 kg). Its hallmark is a soft, silky, slightly wavy coat that comes in shades of grey-blue or, less commonly, light coffee brown; puppies are usually born black and lighten as they mature. The coat is traditionally clipped short on the body while leaving longer furnishings on the legs, belly, face and characteristic beard and eyebrows.

In temperament the breed stands apart from typical terriers. Ceskys tend to be reserved, patient and even-tempered, less prone to barking and digging than many terrier relatives. They are loyal and affectionate with their families, somewhat reserved with strangers, and generally tolerant of children and other dogs.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food appropriate to your Cesky's age, size and activity level. Because the breed has a stocky build and a tendency to gain weight, measure portions carefully and limit treats. Most adults do well on two measured meals per day. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian about ideal body condition and any dietary adjustments for puppies, seniors or less active dogs.

## Exercise & training

The Cesky Terrier has moderate energy needs that are easily met with a couple of daily walks plus some play or sniffing time. They enjoy securely fenced romps and activities such as earthdog trials, tracking and gentle agility. Their calmer disposition and eagerness to please make them more trainable than most terriers, though a streak of independence remains. Use positive, reward-based methods, keep sessions short and engaging, and prioritise early socialisation to encourage confident, friendly adults.

## Grooming & care

Unlike many terriers, the Cesky's soft coat is clipped rather than hand-stripped, which keeps grooming relatively straightforward. Brush two or three times a week to prevent matting, especially in the longer leg and belly furnishings, and clip the coat every few weeks to maintain its classic outline. Routine care also includes regular nail trims, dental brushing, and checking and cleaning the ears to avoid moisture build-up and infection.

## Health & lifespan

The Cesky Terrier is generally a healthy breed with a typical lifespan of around 12 to 15 years. A condition sometimes seen in the breed is "Scottie cramp," a usually harmless movement disorder that affects gait during excitement or exercise. As with many breeds, eye conditions, patellar luxation and obesity-related issues can occur. Choosing a responsible breeder who screens breeding stock, plus regular veterinary checkups, supports a long and comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Cesky Terriers good apartment dogs?**
Yes. Their small size, moderate exercise needs and relatively quiet, calm nature make them well suited to apartments, provided they receive daily walks and companionship.

**Do Cesky Terriers shed a lot?**
No. Their soft, clipped coat sheds minimally, but it does require regular brushing and periodic clipping to stay neat and free of mats.

## Conclusion

The Cesky Terrier offers the spirit and durability of a working terrier in a notably calmer, more agreeable package. With modest exercise, regular coat clipping and consistent positive training, this loyal Czech breed makes a devoted, adaptable companion for a wide range of homes.
