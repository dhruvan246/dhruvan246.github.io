The Leghorn is a lightweight Mediterranean chicken breed prized for laying an exceptional number of large white eggs. Originally from the port of Livorno in Italy, it became a backyard and commercial favorite worldwide. Active, hardy, and economical to feed, the Leghorn suits keepers who want maximum egg production from a relatively small, low-maintenance bird.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Size | Small to medium; hens ~2–2.5 kg (4.5–5.5 lb), roosters ~2.7–3.4 kg (6–7.5 lb) |
| Lifespan | Typically 4–8 years |
| Space needs | Min. 0.4 m² (4 sq ft) coop space per bird, plus generous run |
| Temperament | Active, alert, flighty, independent, rarely broody |
| Care level | Easy to moderate |

## Appearance & temperament

Leghorns are slim, upright birds with a long, flowing tail and a large single comb that flops to one side in hens. Single-comb white Leghorns are the most familiar, but the breed also comes in brown, buff, black, and other colors, with both single-comb and rose-comb varieties. Bantam Leghorns exist for those with limited space.

Temperamentally, they are energetic foragers and excellent fliers, often clearing low fences with ease. They tend to be alert and somewhat wary rather than cuddly, so they are not the best lap-chicken choice for young children. Their watchful nature does make them efficient at finding food and avoiding predators in a well-managed free-range setting.

## Housing & space

Provide a draft-free but well-ventilated coop with sturdy roosting bars, as Leghorns prefer to roost high. Allow at least 0.4 m² (4 sq ft) of indoor floor space per bird and a minimum of about 1 m² (10 sq ft) in the run, though more is always better for this active breed. Because they are strong fliers, fence runs at least 1.8 m (6 ft) high or add netting and clip a wing if necessary.

Include one nest box per three to four hens. Their large combs are prone to frostbite, so in cold climates ensure good insulation and dry bedding, and apply petroleum jelly to combs during hard freezes.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete layer ration containing roughly 16–18% protein once hens begin laying; growing chicks need a higher-protein starter or grower feed. Offer crushed oyster shell or a calcium source free-choice to support strong eggshells, plus insoluble grit if birds eat anything other than commercial pellets.

Fresh, clean water must always be available, as a single laying hen drinks substantially each day. Leghorns are enthusiastic foragers, so supervised free-ranging supplies natural insects and greens; treats like scratch grains should stay under about 10% of the diet to avoid diluting nutrition.

## Care & handling

Handle Leghorns calmly and consistently from a young age to reduce their natural flightiness; sudden movements startle them. Support the body with one hand under the breast when lifting. Routine care includes checking for external parasites such as mites and lice, trimming nails if needed, and keeping the coop clean and dry to prevent disease.

Because they rarely go broody, you will usually need an incubator or a broody hen of another breed to hatch eggs.

## Health & lifespan

Leghorns are robust and generally long-lived for laying hens, often reaching 4–8 years, though intensive laying can shorten productive years. Common poultry concerns include respiratory infections, internal and external parasites, and reproductive issues like egg binding in heavy layers. Their large combs make them heat-tolerant but frostbite-prone.

Keep vaccinations and biosecurity current, provide a balanced diet, and consult a poultry-savvy veterinarian for illness, as medication needs vary. Quarantine new birds before introducing them to the flock.

## Frequently asked questions

**How many eggs do Leghorns lay?** Productive white Leghorn hens commonly lay around 250–300 large white eggs per year, making them one of the most prolific laying breeds.

**Are Leghorns good pets for families?** They are hardy and rewarding but tend to be active and flighty rather than affectionate, so they suit egg-focused keepers more than households wanting a docile lap chicken.

## Conclusion

The Leghorn rewards keepers with outstanding egg output, hardiness, and low feeding costs. Give it secure, tall fencing, a clean coop, balanced layer feed, and room to forage, and this lively Mediterranean breed will be a productive cornerstone of any backyard flock.
