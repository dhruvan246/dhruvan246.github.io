The Xoloitzcuintli (pronounced "show-low-eats-QUEENT-lee"), often shortened to "Xolo," is one of the world's oldest and rarest dog breeds. Native to Mexico, this ancient companion is best known for its hairless variety, though a coated version exists as well. Calm, loyal, and dignified, the Xolo has been treasured for thousands of years and remains a distinctive, low-maintenance companion today.

## Appearance & temperament

The Xoloitzcuintli comes in three sizes—toy, miniature, and standard—with the standard reaching roughly 18 to 23 inches at the shoulder and 30 to 55 pounds. The hairless variety has smooth, tough skin that may carry a few coarse hairs on the head, while the coated variety wears a short, close-fitting coat. Colors range from black and slate gray to bronze, red, and brindle.

Temperamentally, Xolos are calm, alert, and devoted. They bond strongly with their families and can be reserved or watchful around strangers, which makes them attentive watchdogs. Early socialization helps channel this natural wariness into confident, well-mannered behavior. Indoors they are typically quiet and composed.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed your Xolo a high-quality, balanced dog food suited to its age, size, and activity level. Because the breed has a lean, athletic build, it is important to avoid overfeeding—excess weight strains the joints and reduces longevity. Measure meals rather than free-feeding, and divide the daily amount into two servings.

Hairless Xolos can be prone to dental issues, including missing or irregular teeth, so dental-friendly food and chews can help. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian before adding supplements or making major dietary changes.

## Exercise & training

Xolos have moderate energy and enjoy daily walks plus some active play. A couple of brisk walks and a play session usually satisfy their needs, though they also appreciate a securely fenced yard to explore. They are athletic and can excel at agility and other dog sports.

This is an intelligent, sensitive breed that responds best to patient, positive reinforcement. Harsh corrections undermine their trust. Consistency, early socialization, and reward-based methods produce a well-adjusted dog. Mental stimulation through training games helps prevent boredom.

## Grooming & care

Grooming needs differ by variety. The hairless Xolo requires regular skin care: gentle bathing, occasional moisturizing, and protection from sunburn in strong sun and from cold in winter—a dog coat or sweater is useful in chilly weather. Avoid heavy oils or products that can clog pores and cause blemishes.

The coated variety needs only routine brushing. For both types, keep nails trimmed, ears clean, and teeth brushed regularly. Their minimal shedding makes them appealing to those seeking a tidy housemate.

## Health & lifespan

The Xoloitzcuintli is generally a hardy, long-lived breed, often reaching 13 to 18 years. The hairless gene is linked to dental abnormalities, so missing or crowded teeth are common and worth monitoring. Skin care is the main ongoing concern for hairless dogs, including occasional acne, dryness, and sun sensitivity.

Responsible breeders screen for hip and joint health and other heritable conditions. Routine veterinary checkups, dental care, weight management, and attentive skin care support a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Xoloitzcuintlis hypoallergenic?**
No dog is truly hypoallergenic, but hairless Xolos shed very little and produce minimal dander, which some allergy sufferers tolerate better. Spending time with the breed before adopting is the best test.

**Do Xolos need special skin care?**
Yes. Hairless Xolos need regular gentle bathing, sun protection, and warmth in cold weather. Their skin can develop dryness or minor acne, so a simple, consistent routine keeps it healthy.

## Conclusion

The Xoloitzcuintli is a calm, loyal, and remarkably ancient companion that combines a unique appearance with an easygoing temperament. With moderate exercise, attentive skin and dental care, and positive training, this rare Mexican breed thrives as a devoted family member and lives a long, healthy life.
