The Boer goat is a robust, fast-growing meat breed developed in South Africa during the early 1900s. Easily recognized by its white body and distinctive reddish-brown head, it has become one of the most popular meat goats worldwide thanks to its hardiness, docile nature, and strong maternal instincts. While primarily a livestock animal, Boers are also kept as friendly homestead companions.

## Appearance & temperament

Boer goats have a muscular, deep-bodied build with a broad head, pendulous ears, and a Roman nose. The breed standard favors a white coat with a coloured head, though fully red and "paint" patterns also occur. Both sexes typically carry horns that curve back over the head.

Temperamentally, Boers are known for being gentle and easygoing compared with many goat breeds. They are highly social, bond with their herd, and quickly recognize the people who care for them. Because goats are herd animals, you should never keep a single Boer alone; always keep at least two.

## Housing & space

Boers need a dry, draft-free shelter that protects them from rain, wind, and heat. A simple three-sided barn or run-in shed with clean straw bedding works well. Allow roughly 1.5-2 square metres of indoor space per adult goat, plus generous outdoor pasture or paddock.

Fencing is the biggest practical challenge. Goats are escape artists and persistent climbers, so use sturdy woven-wire or stock fencing at least 1.2 metres high, with no footholds. Provide rocks, stumps, or sturdy platforms for climbing and play, which keeps them active and content.

## Diet & nutrition

Goats are natural browsers, preferring shrubs, weeds, and brush over grazed grass. A good Boer diet centres on quality hay and pasture browse, supplemented with a goat-specific mineral mix. Pregnant, lactating, or growing animals may need limited grain to meet higher energy demands.

Always provide clean, fresh water and free-choice loose minerals formulated for goats; cattle minerals can be dangerous because goats need added copper. Avoid sudden feed changes, mouldy hay, and lawn clippings. Never feed sheep feed, as it lacks the copper goats require.

## Care & handling

Daily care includes fresh water, feed checks, and a quick look for signs of illness. Hooves should be trimmed roughly every 6-8 weeks to prevent overgrowth and lameness. Establish a deworming and vaccination schedule with a livestock veterinarian, including the common CDT vaccine.

Boers are easy to handle when accustomed to people. Move calmly, support their body when lifting kids, and use feed to encourage cooperation rather than force.

## Health & lifespan

With good management, Boers commonly live 8-12 years. Common health concerns include internal parasites (a leading cause of illness in goats), foot rot in wet conditions, and bloat from rich feed. Check the inner eyelid colour using the FAMACHA method to monitor for parasite-related anaemia.

Watch for reduced appetite, isolation, limping, scouring, or a hunched posture, all of which warrant veterinary attention. Routine hoof care, parasite monitoring, vaccination, and a balanced diet prevent most problems.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I keep just one Boer goat?** No. Goats are herd animals and become stressed and noisy when alone. Always keep at least two companions.

**Are Boer goats good for beginners?** Yes, their calm temperament makes them beginner-friendly, but fencing, hoof trimming, and parasite control still require real commitment.

## Conclusion

Boer goats are hardy, friendly, and rewarding animals for anyone with land and time to commit. Secure fencing, a browse-based diet, companionship, and routine hoof and parasite care are the keys to keeping them healthy. Check local livestock regulations before getting started.
