The Pekin Duck is the most popular domestic duck breed in North America, prized for its calm nature, fast growth, and hardy constitution. Originally developed in China and refined in the United States, this large white duck is kept for eggs, meat, and increasingly as a backyard companion. With proper space, water, and feed, Pekins are among the easiest poultry for beginners.

## Appearance & temperament

Pekins are unmistakable: bright white feathers, an upright stance, orange-yellow bills, and orange legs and feet. Ducklings start as the classic fluffy yellow before turning white. They are heavy-bodied birds that rarely fly, which makes them easy to contain. Temperament is one of their biggest selling points. Pekins are typically docile, curious, and quick to bond with the people who feed them. They are flock animals and should never be kept singly, as a lone duck becomes stressed and lonely. Drakes (males) have a quiet, raspy call, while hens are noticeably louder with a classic loud quack.

## Housing & space

Provide a predator-proof shelter with solid walls and a secure door closed at night, since ducks are vulnerable to foxes, raccoons, and hawks. Allow at least 4-6 square feet of floor space per duck inside the house and 10 or more square feet in an outdoor run. Use a deep layer of straw or pine shavings for bedding and keep it dry. Ducks do not roost, so they sleep on the ground. Ventilation matters because ducks are messy with water and create humidity. A low, draft-free house with good airflow near the top works best.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a commercial waterfowl or unmedicated poultry feed. Ducklings need a starter ration (often 18-20% protein), then transition to a maintenance or layer feed as adults. Laying hens require added calcium, typically from a layer feed plus free-choice crushed oyster shell. Niacin is important for ducks; if using a standard chick starter, supplement with brewer's yeast to prevent leg problems. Offer leafy greens, peas, and chopped vegetables as treats. Provide grit for digestion. Always serve feed alongside water, as ducks must wet their food to swallow it safely.

## Care & handling

Clean, fresh water is non-negotiable. Pekins need water deep enough to submerge their heads and clean their eyes and nostrils, plus a pool or pond for bathing whenever possible. Change drinking water often, as ducks foul it quickly. When handling, scoop the bird up by supporting the body and tucking the wings, never grab by the legs or neck. Regular gentle interaction keeps them tame. Clean the shelter weekly and refresh bedding to control moisture and odor.

## Health & lifespan

Well-cared-for Pekins live 5-10 years, though heavy production lines may average less. Common issues include bumblefoot (foot infections from rough surfaces), niacin deficiency causing weak legs, and obesity from overfeeding. Their fast growth can strain legs and joints, so avoid excess treats. Keep bedding dry to prevent respiratory and foot problems. Watch for wet feather, lameness, or loss of appetite, and consult an avian or poultry-savvy veterinarian when symptoms appear.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Pekin ducks need a pond?** Not strictly, but they need water deep enough to dunk their heads and ideally a kiddie pool or pond to bathe. Swimming water keeps their feathers, eyes, and overall health in good shape.

**How many eggs do Pekin ducks lay?** A healthy Pekin hen lays roughly 150-200 large white eggs per year, with the best production in her first two laying seasons before it gradually declines.

## Conclusion

Pekin Ducks reward owners with friendly personalities, steady egg production, and easy-going care. Give them a flock for company, a secure shelter, balanced waterfowl feed, and plenty of clean water, and these hardy white ducks will thrive in almost any backyard or small farm setting.
