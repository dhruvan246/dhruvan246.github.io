The Lionhead is a small, friendly rabbit breed famous for the distinctive "mane" of long wool around its head and neck. Affectionate and curious, it makes a popular companion for families and first-time rabbit keepers willing to provide daily attention and gentle grooming.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Size | Small (about 1.1–1.8 kg / 2.5–4 lb) |
| Lifespan | Around 7–10 years with good care |
| Housing | Large pen or rabbit-proofed indoor space with daily exercise |
| Temperament | Gentle, social, curious, generally docile |
| Care level | Moderate (regular grooming and companionship needed) |

## Appearance & temperament

Lionheads are compact rabbits with a rounded body, upright ears, and the signature wool mane encircling the head. Some have a single mane, while others carry a "double mane" with extra wool on the flanks. Coats come in many colours, including chestnut, black, blue, tortoise, and white. They are typically calm, inquisitive, and enjoy interacting with people, though, like all rabbits, individuals can be timid until they learn to trust their owners.

## Housing & environment

Provide as much space as possible. A large enclosure or puppy pen works as a home base, but rabbits need several hours of supervised exercise daily in a rabbit-proofed area. Line the living space with paper-based or hay bedding, and offer a hideaway for security. Rabbits are social animals and often thrive with a neutered, bonded companion. Keep them indoors or in a sheltered space away from extreme heat, damp, and drafts, since they tolerate cold better than high temperatures. A litter tray filled with rabbit-safe litter makes cleaning easier.

## Diet & nutrition

The foundation of a Lionhead's diet is unlimited grass hay such as timothy or meadow hay, which supports digestion and dental wear. Supplement this with a daily portion of fresh leafy greens and a small measured amount of quality rabbit pellets. Avoid sugary treats, and never feed grass clippings. Fresh, clean water should always be available from a bowl or bottle. Introduce any new foods gradually to avoid digestive upset, as rabbits have sensitive stomachs.

## Handling & enrichment

Support a Lionhead's body fully when lifting, with one hand under the chest and the other supporting the hindquarters; never pick a rabbit up by its ears. Many prefer interacting at floor level rather than being held. Offer enrichment such as chew toys, cardboard tunnels, digging boxes, and foraging opportunities to keep them mentally and physically active. The wool mane benefits from regular gentle brushing, especially during moults, to prevent mats and reduce hairballs.

## Health & lifespan

With attentive care, Lionheads commonly live around 7–10 years. Watch for signs of dental disease, since overgrown teeth are common in rabbits, and monitor appetite and droppings closely. A rabbit that stops eating may have gastrointestinal stasis, a serious emergency requiring prompt veterinary attention. Discuss vaccinations (where available), neutering, and parasite control with a rabbit-savvy vet. Long wool around the rear should be checked to prevent soiling and flystrike in warm weather.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Lionhead rabbits need a lot of grooming?**
Yes. Their wool mane and, in double-maned rabbits, side wool, can mat without regular brushing. A weekly groom, increasing during seasonal moults, keeps the coat healthy.

**Are Lionhead rabbits good for beginners?**
They can be, thanks to their friendly nature, but they still need daily care, space, companionship, and grooming. They are a long-term commitment, not a low-maintenance starter pet.

## Conclusion

The Lionhead Rabbit rewards patient owners with an affectionate, characterful companion topped by a charming wool mane. Provide ample space, a hay-based diet, regular grooming, gentle handling, and access to a rabbit-savvy vet, and your Lionhead can enjoy a happy, healthy life as part of the family.
