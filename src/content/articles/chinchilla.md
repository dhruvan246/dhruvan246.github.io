The chinchilla is a small, crepuscular rodent native to the cold, rocky slopes of the Andes Mountains in South America. Prized for its remarkably dense, soft fur, it makes a curious and long-lived companion for owners willing to meet its specialized needs.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| Size | 9-15 in (23-38 cm) body, plus tail; 400-600 g |
| Lifespan | 10-20 years |
| Housing | Tall multi-level wire cage in a cool room |
| Temperament | Active, curious, skittish, social |
| Care level | Intermediate to advanced |

## Appearance & temperament

Chinchillas have rounded bodies, large rounded ears, big dark eyes, and a bushy tail. Their fur is exceptionally dense, with many hairs growing from each follicle, giving a plush, velvety coat that ranges from standard grey to beige, black, white, and violet variations. They are highly active and athletic, capable of impressive leaps. Naturally cautious and quick to startle, most chinchillas dislike being tightly restrained but can become trusting and affectionate with patient, gentle interaction. They are most lively at dawn and dusk.

## Housing & environment

Because chinchillas climb and jump, provide a tall, multi-level wire cage with solid shelves and ledges to protect their feet. Use a safe, dust-free bedding such as kiln-dried pine or paper-based products. Temperature control is critical: chinchillas easily suffer fatal heat stroke above roughly 25 C (77 F), so keep them in a cool, well-ventilated room, ideally 15-21 C (60-70 F), and never in direct sun. Offer chew-safe wooden toys, tunnels, and a solid exercise wheel. A regular dust bath in special chinchilla dust keeps their fur clean; offer it a few times weekly, not constantly, to avoid dry skin.

## Diet & nutrition

A chinchilla's diet should be built around unlimited grass hay, such as timothy or orchard hay, which provides the fibre needed for healthy digestion and continuously growing teeth. Supplement with a measured portion of plain, high-fibre chinchilla pellets daily. Fresh, clean water should always be available, typically from a sipper bottle. Treats should be very limited; sugary or high-fat foods, including most fruit, nuts, seeds, and raisins, can cause digestive upset and obesity. Avoid sudden diet changes, which can trigger dangerous gut problems.

## Handling & enrichment

Approach a chinchilla calmly and let it come to you, supporting its body fully rather than grabbing. Never grasp the fur tightly, as chinchillas can shed a patch of fur to escape, a defense called fur slip. Daily supervised playtime in a chinchilla-proofed area satisfies their need to run and explore; block hazards like cords and gaps. Provide plenty of safe chew items to wear down teeth and prevent boredom. Many chinchillas enjoy the company of a same-sex companion, but introductions must be slow and carefully supervised.

## Health & lifespan

With good care, chinchillas commonly live 10 to 20 years. Common health concerns include overgrown teeth (malocclusion), digestive disorders such as bloat and constipation, heat stroke, and fur issues. Signs of illness include reduced appetite, drooling, fewer droppings, and lethargy, all of which warrant prompt attention from an exotics-experienced veterinarian. Keep the diet high in fibre, the environment cool, and monitor weight to support a long, healthy life. Note that ownership rules for exotic pets vary by region, so check local regulations.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do chinchillas need water baths?** No. Chinchillas should never be bathed in water; their dense fur traps moisture and can lead to skin problems. They clean themselves by rolling in fine chinchilla dust instead.

**Are chinchillas good pets for children?** They can be, but they are delicate, fast, and easily stressed, so they suit gentle older children and adults better than very young kids, and always require adult supervision.

## Conclusion

A chinchilla rewards dedicated owners with years of playful, charming companionship. By providing a cool environment, a hay-based diet, regular dust baths, and patient handling, you can help this Andean rodent thrive as a healthy, long-lived member of your household.
