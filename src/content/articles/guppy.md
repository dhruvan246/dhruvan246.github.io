The guppy (*Poecilia reticulata*) is one of the most popular freshwater aquarium fish in the world. Hardy, colourful, and easy to breed, it is an ideal choice for beginners and experienced hobbyists alike. Native to northeastern South America, guppies adapt well to a wide range of conditions, which is part of why they have become an aquarium staple.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| Adult size | About 1.5–2.5 inches (4–6 cm); females larger than males |
| Lifespan | Roughly 1–3 years |
| Minimum tank size | 5–10 gallons |
| Water type | Freshwater (tropical) |
| Care level | Beginner / easy |

## Appearance & behaviour

Guppies are famous for their vivid colours and flowing tails, especially in males, which are smaller but far more ornate than the plainer, larger females. Decades of selective breeding have produced countless colour strains and tail shapes, from fan-tails to delta-tails. They are peaceful, active, and constantly swimming, making them lively additions to a community tank. Guppies are social and feel most comfortable in groups, so keeping several together encourages natural behaviour.

## Tank setup & water parameters

A tank of at least 5–10 gallons suits a small group, with larger tanks needed as numbers grow. Guppies thrive in stable, well-filtered water. Aim for a temperature of about 72–82°F (22–28°C), a pH around 6.8–7.8, and slightly hard water, which they tend to prefer. Provide a gentle filter, a secure lid (guppies can jump), and some live or artificial plants for cover. Regular partial water changes of around 20–30% weekly help keep ammonia and nitrite at zero and nitrate low. Always cycle a new tank before adding fish.

## Diet & feeding

Guppies are omnivores with hearty appetites. A good-quality flake or micro-pellet food formulated for tropical fish forms a solid staple. Supplement this with occasional treats such as brine shrimp, daphnia, bloodworms (live or frozen), and small amounts of blanched vegetables. Feed small portions once or twice a day, only as much as they finish within a couple of minutes. Overfeeding is a common mistake that fouls the water and harms health, so err on the side of less.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Guppies are peaceful and mix well with other small, calm community fish such as platies, mollies, neon tetras, corydoras catfish, and snails. Avoid housing them with fin-nippers like some barbs, or with large or aggressive fish that may eat them. Note that guppies breed readily, so mixing males and females will quickly produce fry. Keeping only males, or providing dense plants for fry to hide in, helps manage population if breeding is not desired.

## Health & common issues

Guppies are hardy but can suffer from common aquarium ailments when water quality slips. Watch for fin rot, ich (white spot disease), and fungal infections, which often follow stress or poor conditions. Clamped fins, loss of colour, or lethargy are early warning signs. Maintaining clean, stable water is the best prevention. Quarantine new fish before adding them to an established tank, and consult an aquatic specialist or vet for persistent or serious illness rather than guessing at treatments.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do guppies need a heater?** In most homes, yes. Guppies are tropical fish and do best in stable warm water, so a heater is recommended unless your room reliably stays in their preferred range.

**How many guppies can I keep together?** As a rough guide, allow a few gallons per fish and avoid overcrowding. A 10-gallon tank can comfortably house a small group, with more space needed as they breed.

## Conclusion

Guppies are a rewarding, low-maintenance fish that bring colour and movement to any freshwater aquarium. With a properly cycled tank, stable warm water, a varied diet, and peaceful tank mates, they are forgiving of beginner mistakes and quick to thrive. Their easy breeding and friendly temperament make them a lasting favourite in the hobby.
