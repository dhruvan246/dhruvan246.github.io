The Schapendoes, sometimes called the Dutch Sheepdog, is a lively, shaggy herding breed from the Netherlands. Once a common farm dog used to drive flocks, it nearly disappeared during the mid-20th century before dedicated breeders revived it. Today it is prized as an agile, affectionate companion with a famously springy, bounding gait.

## Appearance & temperament

The Schapendoes is a lightly built, medium-sized dog that looks larger than it is because of its abundant coat. A long, harsh-to-soft outer coat covers the body, with a thick undercoat, a notable beard, and hair that often partly veils the eyes. Coats appear in many colors, with blue-grey and black being especially common. The breed moves with an effortless, elastic spring that lets it change direction quickly.

In temperament, the Schapendoes is cheerful, alert, and devoted. It bonds closely with its family, is typically friendly toward children and other dogs, and tends to be intelligent and independent-minded—a legacy of its working past. It is watchful without being aggressive and can be reserved with strangers at first.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food suited to the Schapendoes' age, weight, and activity level. As an active breed, working or sporting individuals may need more calories than couch companions. Divide food into two measured meals a day rather than free-feeding, and use a portion guide as a starting point, adjusting to keep the dog lean. Because the heavy coat can hide weight gain, check body condition by feeling the ribs. Always provide fresh water, and keep treats to a small share of daily calories.

## Exercise & training

This is an energetic, athletic breed that thrives on activity. Aim for at least an hour or more of exercise daily, combining walks with off-leash running, fetch, or play. The Schapendoes excels at dog sports such as agility, flyball, and obedience, and enjoys having a job to do. Mental stimulation through puzzle toys and training games is just as important as physical exercise.

Training should be positive, consistent, and engaging. The breed is clever but can be a touch stubborn or easily bored, so keep sessions short and rewarding. Early socialization and reward-based methods bring out its best, friendliest character.

## Grooming & care

The long double coat needs regular attention. Brush thoroughly at least once or twice a week—more during seasonal shedding—to prevent mats and tangles, paying special attention to the beard, legs, and behind the ears. The coat is meant to look natural and is generally not clipped short. Bathe only as needed. Routine care also includes checking ears, trimming nails, and brushing teeth.

## Health & lifespan

The Schapendoes is generally a hardy, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of about 12 to 15 years. Like many breeds, it can be affected by conditions such as hip dysplasia and certain eye disorders, including progressive retinal atrophy. Responsible breeders screen breeding stock for known issues. Routine veterinary checkups, parasite prevention, dental care, and keeping the dog at a healthy weight all support a long, active life. Consult your veterinarian about any specific health concerns.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Schapendoes good family dogs?**
Yes. They are typically affectionate, playful, and good with children and other pets, making them well suited to active families who can meet their exercise needs.

**Do Schapendoes shed a lot?**
They shed moderately, with heavier seasonal shedding. Regular brushing manages loose hair and prevents the long coat from matting.

## Conclusion

The Schapendoes is a joyful, energetic Dutch herding dog that rewards an active household with loyalty and fun. With daily exercise, consistent positive training, and regular coat care, this spirited companion makes a charming and devoted family member.
