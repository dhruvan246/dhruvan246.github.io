The German Spitz is a bright, alert companion dog known for its fox-like face, pricked ears, and luxurious double coat. One of the oldest dog types in central Europe, it comes in several size varieties, with the Klein (small) and Mittel (medium) being the most familiar in homes today. Devoted and watchful, the German Spitz makes a charming family pet for owners who enjoy an active, vocal little dog.

## Overview

| Trait | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Size | Small (Klein variety roughly 9–11.5 in / 23–29 cm at the shoulder) |
| Lifespan | 13–15 years |
| Coat | Long, harsh outer coat over a soft, dense undercoat |
| Energy | Moderate to high |
| Good with kids | Yes, with supervision and early socialization |

## Appearance & temperament

The German Spitz has a compact, almost square build with a plumed tail that curls over the back and a thick ruff around the neck. The wedge-shaped head, dark almond eyes, and small triangular ears give it a foxy, attentive expression. Coats appear in many colors including white, black, brown, orange, cream, and wolf-grey.

Temperamentally, this breed is lively, confident, and deeply attached to its people. German Spitz dogs are naturally alert and tend to bark at unfamiliar sounds, which makes them excellent little watchdogs but means barking should be managed early. They are intelligent and eager to please, though they can show an independent, spirited streak.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality complete dog food formulated for small breeds and matched to your dog's age, weight, and activity level. Because the German Spitz is a small dog, portion control matters: measure meals rather than free-feeding to prevent weight gain. Most adults do well on two measured meals a day.

Treats should make up only a small part of daily calories and are useful for training rewards. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian before changing diets or if your dog gains or loses weight.

## Exercise & training

Despite its small size, the German Spitz is energetic and enjoys daily walks plus active play. Around 30–60 minutes of exercise a day, combined with mental stimulation such as games and puzzle toys, keeps this clever breed happy and prevents boredom-related behaviors.

Training should be positive, consistent, and started young. German Spitz dogs respond well to reward-based methods and enjoy learning tricks. Early socialization with people, other animals, and everyday noises helps curb excessive barking and wariness toward strangers.

## Grooming & care

The dense double coat needs regular attention. Brush at least two to three times a week to remove loose hair and prevent matting, increasing frequency during the heavy seasonal shed when the undercoat blows out. Despite the volume of fur, the coat is relatively self-cleaning and resists tangling when maintained.

Bathe only as needed, and avoid shaving the double coat, as it protects against both heat and cold. Routine care also includes regular nail trims, ear checks, and dental hygiene such as tooth brushing.

## Health & lifespan

The German Spitz is generally a hardy, long-lived breed, often reaching 13 to 15 years. As with many small and spitz-type dogs, owners should be aware of conditions such as patellar luxation (slipping kneecaps), dental disease, and certain eye issues like progressive retinal atrophy.

Routine veterinary checkups, vaccinations, parasite prevention, dental care, and a healthy weight all support a long life. Buying from a responsible breeder who screens breeding dogs can reduce the risk of inherited problems.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the German Spitz the same as a Pomeranian?** They are closely related. The Pomeranian (sometimes called the Zwergspitz or Toy Spitz) descends from larger German Spitz dogs but is now a separate, smaller variety with its own standard.

**Do German Spitz dogs bark a lot?** They are naturally alert and vocal, so they can bark readily at noises and visitors. Early training and socialization help keep barking at a manageable level.

## Conclusion

The German Spitz is a spirited, affectionate small dog that rewards attentive owners with loyalty and lively companionship. With consistent training, regular coat care, daily exercise, and good veterinary support, this hardy breed can thrive as a long-lived family member for well over a decade.
