The Tiger Salamander (*Ambystoma tigrinum*) is one of the largest land-dwelling salamanders in North America and a popular choice for amphibian keepers. Hardy, long-lived, and full of personality, it rewards a stable, low-stress setup with years of fascinating behaviour.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Size | 6–8 in typical (up to ~13 in) |
| Lifespan | 10–15 years (sometimes longer) |
| Enclosure | 20+ gallon terrarium with deep substrate |
| Temperament | Docile, shy, mostly nocturnal |
| Care level | Beginner to intermediate |

## Appearance & behaviour

Tiger Salamanders are stocky, broad-headed amphibians with smooth, moist skin marked by yellow or olive blotches and bars over a dark brown or black background, a pattern that gives them their name. Markings vary widely between regional populations. They are primarily fossorial, spending much of their time burrowed underground, and become most active at night or after rain. In a terrarium they often learn to recognise their keeper at feeding time, surfacing readily when food appears.

## Habitat & water

Provide a horizontally oriented terrarium with at least 4–6 inches of moist (not soggy) substrate such as coconut fibre, organic topsoil, or a soil-and-leaf-litter mix that allows burrowing. Cool temperatures are essential: aim for around 60–70°F (15–21°C) and avoid letting the enclosure exceed the mid-70s, as these salamanders are intolerant of heat. No special lighting or heating is usually needed indoors; ambient room temperature often suffices. Keep humidity high and the substrate consistently damp. Include a shallow, easily exited water dish for soaking, and use dechlorinated water only, since chlorine and chloramine harm amphibian skin. Add cork bark, hides, and a layer of leaf litter for security.

## Diet & feeding

Tiger Salamanders are enthusiastic carnivores with hearty appetites. Offer a varied diet of earthworms (an excellent staple), crickets, dubia roaches, and the occasional waxworm or nightcrawler. Larger adults may take pinky mice infrequently, though invertebrates should form the bulk of the diet to prevent obesity. Dust feeder insects with a calcium and vitamin supplement a couple of times per week. Feed juveniles every one to two days and adults two to three times weekly, adjusting portions to maintain a rounded but not bloated body. Remove uneaten prey to keep the enclosure clean.

## Handling & health

Like all amphibians, Tiger Salamanders absorb substances through their permeable skin, so handling should be minimal and only with clean, wet, fragrance-free hands. Oils, lotions, and salts on dry skin can injure them. Watch for signs of trouble such as bloating, skin lesions, lethargy, weight loss, or refusal to eat, which may indicate poor water quality, incorrect temperature, or infection. Excessive heat is a common and serious risk. Spot-clean waste promptly and refresh substrate as needed to prevent bacterial and fungal problems. An amphibian-experienced exotics veterinarian should address persistent illness. Legality and collection of wild specimens vary by region, so check local laws and choose captive-bred animals when possible.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Tiger Salamanders need water to swim in?**
No. Adults are terrestrial and only need a shallow soaking dish; deep water can stress or even drown a land-phase adult that cannot easily climb out.

**Can Tiger Salamanders be kept together?**
It is generally safer to house them singly. They may compete for food, become stressed, or even nip each other, and similar-sized tankmates are sometimes mistaken for prey.

## Conclusion

With cool temperatures, deep moist substrate, clean dechlorinated water, and a varied insect diet, a Tiger Salamander makes a rewarding, long-lived amphibian companion. Keep handling to a minimum, watch for heat stress, and you can enjoy this charismatic burrower for well over a decade.
