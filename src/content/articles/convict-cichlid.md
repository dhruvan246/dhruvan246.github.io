The Convict Cichlid (*Amatitlania nigrofasciata*) is one of the most popular beginner cichlids, prized for its bold personality, striking stripes and willingness to breed in almost any aquarium. Native to Central America, it is hardy and undemanding, but its aggression means it must be housed thoughtfully.

## Overview

| Attribute | Typical range |
|---|---|
| Adult size | Around 4-6 in (10-15 cm); females usually smaller |
| Lifespan | Roughly 8-10 years with good care |
| Minimum tank size | About 30 gallons for a single fish or pair |
| Water type | Freshwater |
| Care level | Easy |

## Appearance & behaviour

Convict Cichlids get their name from the vertical black-and-grey "prison stripe" bands running down a pale body. Females often develop orange or pink scaling on the belly, which is the easiest way to sex them. A common pink (leucistic) variety lacks the strong striping.

Behaviourally, they are intelligent, curious and notably aggressive, especially when breeding or guarding territory. They dig substrate, rearrange decor and defend their patch fiercely. Many keepers enjoy watching their interactive, almost dog-like awareness of activity outside the glass.

## Tank setup & water parameters

Provide a tank of at least 30 gallons, with more space for pairs or community setups. Use a soft sand or fine gravel substrate they can dig, plus plenty of rockwork, caves and flowerpots to create defendable territories and line-of-sight breaks that reduce conflict.

Aim for water temperatures of about 75-82°F (24-28°C), a pH near 6.5-8.0, and moderate hardness. They tolerate a wide range but appreciate stability. Strong filtration and regular partial water changes are important because these active fish produce a lot of waste. Hardy live or plastic plants work, though they may uproot delicate species while digging.

## Diet & feeding

Convict Cichlids are omnivores with hearty appetites. A quality cichlid pellet or flake forms a good staple, supplemented with frozen or live foods such as bloodworms, brine shrimp and daphnia, plus occasional blanched vegetables like spinach or peas. Feed small amounts once or twice daily, only what they finish in a couple of minutes, to avoid overfeeding and water-quality problems.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Compatibility is the biggest challenge. A bonded male-female pair forms a strong territory and will attack most tank mates, particularly when spawning. In larger tanks, robust Central American cichlids of similar temperament and size can sometimes coexist, but timid or long-finned fish are poor choices. Many owners keep a single Convict or a pair in a species-only tank. If you do not want constant breeding, keeping a single fish avoids a population explosion.

## Health & common issues

Convicts are robust but susceptible to common freshwater ailments such as ich (white spot), bacterial infections and fin damage from fighting. Most problems trace back to poor water quality, so consistent maintenance is the best prevention. Quarantine new fish, watch for clamped fins, loss of appetite or unusual spots, and consult an aquatics-savvy vet or specialist if illness persists. Aggression injuries may require separating combatants.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Convict Cichlids good for beginners?** Yes. They are hardy, adaptable and easy to feed, making them a classic first cichlid, though their aggression means owners should plan tank mates carefully.

**Do Convict Cichlids breed easily?** Extremely easily. A compatible pair will spawn readily on a flat surface, and both parents guard the eggs and fry, so be prepared to manage the resulting young.

## Conclusion

The Convict Cichlid rewards keepers with bold colour, fascinating behaviour and fuss-free care. Give it space, structure and clean water, manage its territorial nature, and it makes a durable, engaging aquarium resident. Availability and regulations vary by region, so check local rules before purchasing.
