The Betta fish (*Betta splendens*), also called the Siamese fighting fish, is one of the most popular freshwater aquarium pets. Native to the slow-moving waters of Southeast Asia, it is prized for its vivid colours and flowing fins. With the right warm, filtered tank, a betta is hardy and rewarding to keep.

## Overview

| Attribute | Typical range |
|---|---|
| Adult size | Around 2.5–3 inches (6–7.5 cm) |
| Lifespan | Roughly 2–4 years with good care |
| Minimum tank size | 5 gallons (about 19 litres) |
| Water type | Warm freshwater |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & behaviour

Bettas are famous for their long, flowing fins and bright colours, which range from deep reds and blues to metallic and pastel shades. Males are typically more colourful with larger fins, while females are smaller and shorter-finned. They are curious, intelligent fish that often learn to recognise their keeper. Males are territorial and should never be housed together, as they will fight, hence the name "fighting fish." Bettas also have a special labyrinth organ that lets them breathe air from the surface.

## Tank setup & water parameters

Despite myths about keeping bettas in tiny bowls, they thrive in a properly heated, filtered aquarium of at least 5 gallons. Keep the water temperature between about 76–82°F (24–28°C) using an adjustable heater, since they are tropical fish. Aim for a pH near neutral (around 6.5–7.5). Provide gentle filtration, as bettas dislike strong currents. Add live or silk plants, a soft substrate, and hiding spots. A lid is essential because bettas can jump. Perform regular partial water changes to keep ammonia and nitrite at zero.

## Diet & feeding

Bettas are carnivores and need a protein-rich diet. Feed a quality betta-specific pellet as a staple, supplemented with occasional treats such as frozen or live bloodworms, daphnia, or brine shrimp. Feed small amounts once or twice daily, only what they can eat in a couple of minutes. Overfeeding is a common mistake that fouls the water and can cause bloating, so remove any uneaten food. A weekly fasting day can aid digestion.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Male bettas should always be kept singly to avoid fighting. In a larger, well-planted community tank, a betta may coexist with peaceful, non-nippy species such as small snails, shrimp (with some risk), corydoras, or kuhli loaches. Avoid fin-nippers like tiger barbs and brightly coloured or long-finned fish that a betta may mistake for a rival. Always watch new tank mates closely and have a backup plan if aggression appears.

## Health & common issues

Common betta health problems include fin rot, ich (white spot), and swim bladder issues, often linked to poor water quality or overfeeding. Watch for clamped fins, loss of colour, lethargy, or trouble swimming. Most issues are prevented by clean, warm, stable water and a proper diet. If a fish shows persistent signs of illness, consult an aquatic vet or an experienced fishkeeper before adding any treatment, and quarantine sick fish where possible.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can a betta live in a small bowl without a heater?** No. Bettas are tropical fish that need stable, heated water and filtration. An unheated bowl leads to stress, illness, and a shortened lifespan.

**Can two bettas share a tank?** Two males must never share a tank as they will fight. Even females can be territorial, so any sorority setup needs a large, planted tank and careful monitoring.

## Conclusion

With a warm, filtered tank of at least 5 gallons, a protein-rich diet, and clean water, a Betta fish makes a colourful, interactive, and beginner-friendly pet. Meeting its real tropical needs, rather than relying on old bowl myths, helps your betta live a long, healthy life.
