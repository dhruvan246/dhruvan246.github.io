The Gargoyle Gecko (*Rhacodactylus auriculatus*) is a hardy, arboreal lizard native to the forests of southern New Caledonia. Named for the bumpy, horn-like cranial crests above its eyes, it has become a favorite among keepers who want a low-maintenance, room-temperature gecko with bold patterns and an easygoing nature.

## Overview

| Attribute | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 7-9 inches (including tail) |
| Lifespan | 15-20 years in captivity |
| Enclosure size | 18 x 18 x 24 inches (single adult) |
| Temperament | Calm, tolerant of handling |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & temperament

Gargoyle Geckos show some of the most varied coloration in the hobby, ranging from grey and brown to striking red, orange, and white. Patterns include striped, blotched (reticulated), and mottled morphs. Unlike many geckos, this species can regenerate a dropped tail, though a regrown tail rarely matches the original shape. They are generally docile and slow-moving, but they can be territorial, so adults should be housed individually to prevent biting and nipping.

## Habitat & enclosure

Being arboreal, Gargoyle Geckos need vertical space more than floor space. A front-opening glass terrarium of at least 18 x 18 x 24 inches suits one adult. Furnish it with cork bark, branches, vines, and broad-leaved plants (live or artificial) for climbing and hiding. A bioactive substrate or a moisture-retaining mix such as coconut fiber works well; avoid loose substrates that could be swallowed during feeding. Juveniles are often kept in smaller enclosures and moved up as they grow.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

One of this species' biggest appeals is that it thrives at room temperature. Aim for 72-78°F (22-26°C) during the day, with a slight night drop. Temperatures above the low 80s°F are stressful and can be dangerous. Supplemental heat is rarely needed in a typical home. While they are nocturnal, providing a low-level UVB source on a 12-hour cycle supports vitamin D3 synthesis and bone health. Maintain humidity around 50-70% by misting once or twice daily, allowing the enclosure to dry out partially between mistings to prevent mold.

## Diet & feeding

Gargoyle Geckos are omnivores. The simplest, most reliable diet is a commercial powdered crested/gargoyle gecko diet (CGD) mixed with water and offered in shallow dishes several times per week. Supplement this with appropriately sized, gut-loaded insects such as crickets or dubia roaches dusted with calcium, typically once or twice weekly. Insects should be no longer than the space between the gecko's eyes. Always provide fresh, clean water.

## Handling & health

Handle geckos gently and infrequently at first, allowing them to walk hand-over-hand rather than gripping them. Avoid grabbing the tail. Common health issues include metabolic bone disease (from inadequate calcium or D3), retained shed around toes, and impaction from improper substrate. Signs of trouble include weight loss, lethargy, soft jaw, or refusal to eat. An annual checkup with a reptile-experienced veterinarian is recommended.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Gargoyle Geckos good for beginners?** Yes. They tolerate room temperatures, eat a simple prepared diet, and are calm, making them one of the easier pet geckos to keep successfully.

**Can Gargoyle Geckos be housed together?** It is generally not advised. Adults, especially males, can be territorial and may bite, nip tails, or compete for food, so solitary housing is safest.

## Conclusion

With modest space, simple feeding, and stable room temperatures, the Gargoyle Gecko is a rewarding, long-lived reptile for both new and experienced keepers. As with all exotic pets, check that ownership is legal in your area, and source captive-bred animals from reputable breeders for the healthiest start.
