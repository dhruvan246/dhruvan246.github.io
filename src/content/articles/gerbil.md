The gerbil, most commonly the Mongolian gerbil (*Meriones unguiculatus*), is a small desert rodent that has become a popular and rewarding pet. Curious, clean, and active during the day, gerbils are social animals that thrive in company and reward attentive owners with entertaining digging and tunnelling behaviour.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Size | 10-13 cm body, plus a similar-length tufted tail |
| Lifespan | 3-4 years typically |
| Housing | Deep tank or gnaw-proof cage with thick bedding |
| Temperament | Social, inquisitive, rarely aggressive |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & temperament

Gerbils have a slim, agile body, large dark eyes, and a furred tail ending in a small tuft, distinguishing them from rats and mice. The wild "agouti" coat is sandy brown, but selective breeding has produced black, grey, white, and spotted varieties. They are alert, energetic, and naturally curious, often standing upright to survey their surroundings. Unlike hamsters, gerbils are diurnal, so they are awake and entertaining during the day. They are social by nature and should be kept in same-sex pairs or small groups; a solitary gerbil can become stressed and lonely.

## Housing & environment

Because gerbils are dedicated diggers, the most important feature of their home is depth of bedding rather than floor footprint alone. A glass or sturdy plastic "gerbilarium" of at least 75 litres for a pair lets you provide 15-20 cm of substrate for tunnelling. Use paper-based bedding or dust-free aspen mixed with plenty of hay; avoid cedar and pine shavings, which release irritating oils. Provide a sand bath for grooming, a ceramic hideout, and untreated wooden chews to wear down constantly growing teeth. Keep the enclosure out of direct sunlight and away from draughts, at normal room temperature, and provide a solid-surface wheel for exercise.

## Diet & nutrition

A quality commercial gerbil mix or pellet forms the base of the diet, supplying balanced grains and seeds. Because seed-heavy mixes are high in fat, offer them in moderation to prevent obesity. Supplement with small amounts of fresh vegetables such as carrot, broccoli, and leafy greens, plus the occasional sunflower seed or mealworm as a treat. Fresh, clean water should always be available, ideally from a bottle with a metal spout. Avoid sugary, salty, or citrus foods, and introduce any new food gradually to avoid digestive upset.

## Handling & enrichment

Gerbils generally tolerate gentle handling well once they trust you. Let a new gerbil settle for a few days, then offer your hand with a treat so it learns to approach. Scoop the body in cupped hands rather than grabbing, and never pick a gerbil up by its tail, which can be injured easily. Keep handling sessions short and close to a surface. Enrichment is essential: cardboard tubes, untreated wood, seed-scattering for foraging, and rearranged tunnels keep their active minds busy and prevent boredom-related behaviours.

## Health & lifespan

With good care, gerbils usually live 3-4 years. They are hardy but can develop problems such as overgrown teeth, "tyzzer's disease," respiratory infections, and tail-skin injuries from rough handling. A common condition is scent-gland tumours in older animals. Watch for warning signs including weight loss, laboured breathing, a dull coat, lethargy, or reduced appetite, and consult a vet experienced with small mammals if these appear. Clean housing, a balanced diet, and companionship are the best preventive measures.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can gerbils be kept alone?** It is not recommended. Gerbils are highly social and do best in same-sex pairs or small groups; a lone gerbil is prone to stress and boredom.

**Do gerbils smell?** Gerbils are among the least odorous rodents. As desert animals they produce very little urine, so a well-maintained enclosure stays remarkably clean and low-odour.

## Conclusion

Gerbils make engaging, low-maintenance companions for beginners and experienced keepers alike. Give them a deep-bedded home, a suitable companion, a balanced diet, and gentle daily interaction, and these lively little diggers will provide years of fascinating, rewarding behaviour to watch.
