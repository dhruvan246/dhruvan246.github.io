The corn snake (*Pantherophis guttatus*) is one of the most popular pet reptiles in the world, prized for its calm nature, manageable size, and stunning array of colors. Native to the southeastern United States, this non-venomous constrictor is widely regarded as an excellent first snake for beginners.

## Overview

| Trait | Details |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 3 to 5 feet (90-150 cm) |
| Lifespan | 15 to 20 years in captivity |
| Enclosure size | 20-40 gallon for adults |
| Temperament | Docile, calm, easy to handle |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & temperament

Corn snakes have slender bodies with a distinctive pattern of saddle-shaped blotches running down the back. Wild types are orange to brownish with red blotches, but decades of selective breeding have produced hundreds of color and pattern variations called morphs, including snow, amelanistic, and anerythristic types. A belly checkerboard pattern resembling maize kernels gives the species its common name.

Temperament is a major reason for their popularity. Corn snakes are generally docile and tolerate handling well once acclimated. They are active explorers and skilled escape artists, so a secure enclosure is essential.

## Habitat & enclosure

A single adult needs a minimum 20-gallon long tank, though a 40-gallon breeder offers more room. Hatchlings can be kept in smaller containers to help them feel secure. A tightly fitting, lockable lid is critical because corn snakes will push at any gap.

Provide at least two hides, one on the warm side and one on the cool side, plus sturdy branches for climbing and a water bowl large enough for soaking. Suitable substrates include aspen shavings, cypress mulch, or paper towels. Avoid cedar and pine, which release oils harmful to reptiles.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Corn snakes are ectotherms and need a thermal gradient. Aim for a warm basking area around 85 to 88°F (29-31°C) and a cooler zone of 75 to 80°F (24-27°C), with a nighttime drop into the low 70s acceptable. An under-tank heat mat or overhead bulb on a thermostat works well; always use a thermostat to prevent burns.

These snakes do not strictly require UVB to survive, but low-level UVB can support overall health. Maintain moderate humidity around 40 to 50 percent, raising it slightly during shedding to help the skin come off cleanly.

## Diet & feeding

Corn snakes are carnivores that eat appropriately sized rodents. Offer prey roughly as wide as the thickest part of the snake's body. Hatchlings start on pinky mice, graduating to larger mice as they grow.

Feed frozen-thawed prey rather than live, which avoids injury to the snake and is more humane. Hatchlings eat every 5 to 7 days, while adults eat every 7 to 14 days. Always provide fresh water and avoid handling for a day or two after a meal to prevent regurgitation.

## Handling & health

Handle your snake gently, supporting its body rather than gripping. Limit sessions to short periods at first while it adjusts. Avoid handling during shedding or right after feeding.

Common health concerns include incomplete sheds, respiratory infections (often signaling incorrect temperature or humidity), and mites. Signs of trouble include wheezing, refusing food, or retained eye caps. Consult a reptile-experienced veterinarian when problems arise. Legality and permit requirements for keeping corn snakes vary by region, so check local regulations before acquiring one.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are corn snakes good for beginners?** Yes. Their docile temperament, modest size, hardiness, and straightforward care make them one of the best snakes for first-time reptile keepers.

**Do corn snakes bite?** They rarely bite and are non-venomous. A startled hatchling may nip, but bites are minor and become uncommon as the snake grows accustomed to handling.

## Conclusion

With proper heating, a secure enclosure, and a consistent feeding routine, a corn snake can be a rewarding, low-maintenance companion for 15 to 20 years. Their gentle nature and dazzling morphs make them a standout choice for new and experienced keepers alike.
