The swordtail (*Xiphophorus hellerii*) is one of the most popular freshwater aquarium fish, prized for its bright colours, lively nature and easy care. Native to the rivers and streams of Central America, it gets its name from the long, sword-like extension on the lower part of the male's tail fin. Hardy and adaptable, swordtails are an excellent choice for beginners and a colourful addition to most community tanks.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | Around 4–6 in (10–15 cm), including the male's sword |
| Lifespan | Typically 3–5 years with good care |
| Minimum tank size | About 20 gallons (75 litres) |
| Water type | Freshwater |
| Care level | Easy / beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & behaviour

Swordtails come in a wide range of selectively bred colours, including red, orange, green, black and various patterns like wagtail and tuxedo. Males are slimmer and display the distinctive elongated lower tail "sword," while females are larger, rounder and lack the sword. They are active, peaceful swimmers that spend much of their time in the middle and upper levels of the tank. Males can be territorial toward one another, so keeping more females than males helps reduce squabbling and harassment.

## Tank setup & water parameters

Swordtails are active swimmers and appreciate plenty of horizontal swimming space, so a longer tank is preferable. Provide a secure, tight-fitting lid, as they are known to jump. Aim for a temperature of roughly 72–79°F (22–26°C), a pH of about 7.0–8.0, and moderate to hard water. They thrive in clean, well-filtered water with a gentle to moderate current. Live or artificial plants, along with some open swimming areas, create a comfortable environment. Perform regular partial water changes to keep nitrates low and water quality stable.

## Diet & feeding

Swordtails are omnivores with a healthy appetite. A good-quality flake or pellet food forms a solid staple, and they benefit from added plant matter such as blanched vegetables, spirulina or algae-based foods. Supplement their diet with occasional live or frozen treats like bloodworms, daphnia or brine shrimp for variety and added protein. Feed small amounts once or twice a day, offering only what they can finish in a couple of minutes to avoid overfeeding and water-quality problems.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Generally peaceful, swordtails do well in community aquariums with other calm species. Good companions include platies, mollies, tetras, danios, corydoras catfish and peaceful gouramis. Avoid housing them with aggressive or fin-nipping fish. Keep in mind that swordtails are prolific livebearers; mixing males and females will almost certainly result in fry. If you don't want a growing population, keep a single sex or be prepared to manage the offspring.

## Health & common issues

Swordtails are hardy but can be affected by common freshwater ailments such as ich (white spot), fin rot and fungal infections, usually linked to poor water quality or stress. Maintaining stable parameters, a clean tank and a balanced diet is the best prevention. Quarantine new fish before adding them, and watch for warning signs like clamped fins, loss of appetite or unusual spots. If illness appears, address water conditions promptly and seek guidance from an aquatics specialist or aquatic veterinarian rather than self-medicating blindly.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do swordtails need to be kept in groups?** They are social and do best in small groups. A good approach is to keep one male with two or three females to spread out attention and reduce male aggression.

**Are swordtails easy for beginners?** Yes. Their hardiness, tolerance of a range of conditions and straightforward diet make them one of the most beginner-friendly aquarium fish, as long as the tank is cycled and well maintained.

## Conclusion

Swordtails reward aquarists with vivid colour, constant activity and an easygoing temperament, all while being forgiving of beginner mistakes. Give them a spacious, well-filtered tank, stable water parameters, a varied diet and compatible tank mates, and these lively livebearers will remain a healthy, eye-catching centrepiece of your aquarium for years.
