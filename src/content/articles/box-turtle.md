The Eastern Box Turtle (*Terrapene carolina carolina*) is a small, terrestrial North American turtle known for its hinged shell, which lets it close up tightly for protection. They are long-lived, low-key reptiles that reward patient keepers with decades of companionship.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | Around 4.5–6 inches (shell length) |
| Lifespan | Commonly 30–50 years, sometimes longer |
| Enclosure size | Large outdoor pen or roomy indoor tortoise table |
| Temperament | Shy, calm, slow-moving |
| Care level | Intermediate (long-term commitment) |

## Appearance & temperament

Eastern Box Turtles have a high, domed carapace patterned with yellow or orange markings over a dark brown background. The hinged plastron allows them to seal themselves inside their shell. Males often have red eyes and a slightly concave plastron, while females tend to have brown eyes. They are quiet, retiring animals that prefer to forage and burrow rather than interact. With time they may recognize a caretaker, but they are not affectionate in the way mammals are.

## Habitat & enclosure

A secure outdoor pen in a temperate climate is the best home, mimicking their natural woodland environment with shade, sun, plants, and hiding spots. Provide a substrate of moist soil and leaf litter that holds humidity and allows digging. Indoors, use a large tortoise table or similar enclosure rather than a glass aquarium, which restricts airflow. Always include a shallow water dish big enough for the turtle to soak in and drink. Make pens predator-proof with buried walls, as box turtles can dig and climb surprisingly well.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Box turtles need a basking area around 85–90°F with cooler zones in the 70s°F so they can thermoregulate. A nighttime drop into the high 60s°F is fine. Indoors, provide UVB lighting to support calcium metabolism and prevent metabolic bone disease; outdoor turtles get this from natural sunlight. Maintain moderate to high humidity, generally 60–80%, by misting and using damp substrate. In cooler regions, many keepers allow healthy adults a managed winter brumation, but this should only be attempted with research and ideally veterinary guidance.

## Diet & feeding

Eastern Box Turtles are omnivores with an appetite that shifts as they age; juveniles eat more protein, while adults take more plant matter. Offer a varied diet of leafy greens, vegetables, and fruit alongside protein such as earthworms, snails, slugs, crickets, and the occasional bit of cooked lean meat. Dust food with a calcium supplement and provide vitamin sources as advised for captive reptiles. Avoid relying on a single food. Fresh, clean water should always be available for soaking and drinking.

## Handling & health

Handle box turtles minimally, as they are easily stressed and prefer to be left to roam. Always wash your hands before and after contact, since reptiles can carry Salmonella. Watch for warning signs like swollen eyes, runny nose, soft shell, or refusal to eat, which can indicate vitamin deficiency, respiratory infection, or shell problems. Establish care with a reptile-experienced veterinarian. Note that collecting wild box turtles is illegal or restricted in many areas, so legality varies by region and captive-bred animals are strongly preferred.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Eastern Box Turtles make good pets?** They can, for committed keepers. They are hardy and fascinating but live for decades and have specific humidity, diet, and space needs, so they suit patient owners.

**Can box turtles swim?** They are land turtles and only wade or soak in shallow water. They are poor swimmers and can drown in deep water, so keep dishes shallow.

## Conclusion

The Eastern Box Turtle is a rewarding but long-term pet that thrives with a naturalistic outdoor or large indoor setup, correct heating and humidity, a varied omnivorous diet, and minimal handling. Choosing a captive-bred turtle and partnering with a reptile vet will help your shelled companion live a long, healthy life.
