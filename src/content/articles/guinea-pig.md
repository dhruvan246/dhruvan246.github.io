The guinea pig (*Cavia porcellus*), also called a cavy, is a gentle, social rodent domesticated thousands of years ago in the Andes of South America. Vocal, affectionate, and rarely prone to biting, guinea pigs are among the most popular small pets for families, though they need more space and care than many people expect.

## Overview

## Appearance & temperament

Guinea pigs are stout, tailless rodents with short legs and a rounded body. Coat types vary widely by breed: the smooth-coated American, the swirled-rosette Abyssinian, the long-haired Peruvian and Silkie, and the hairless Skinny pig. Colors range from solid white, black, and cream to multicolored tortoiseshell and roan patterns.

They are highly social herd animals and communicate with a rich vocabulary of squeaks, rumbles, purrs, and the excited "wheek" that often greets feeding time. They thrive on companionship and routine, and most are calm and easy to handle once they trust their owner.

## Housing & environment

Provide the largest cage you can: a minimum of about 0.7 m2 (7.5 sq ft) for one guinea pig and considerably more for a pair, with bigger always being better. Choose a cage with a solid floor, as wire bottoms injure their feet. Line it with paper-based bedding or fleece, and avoid cedar or pine shavings, which release irritating oils.

Keep the cage indoors in a draft-free room held around 18-24 C (65-75 C). Guinea pigs are sensitive to heat and humidity and can suffer fatal heatstroke above roughly 27 C. Add hideouts, a hay rack, and a heavy ceramic water bowl or bottle. Spot-clean daily and fully change bedding weekly. In most regions a single guinea pig is considered lonely, so keep at least two compatible companions.

## Diet & nutrition

Unlimited grass hay, such as Timothy or orchard hay, should make up the bulk of the diet. The constant chewing wears down their continuously growing teeth and keeps the gut moving. Supplement with about a cup of fresh leafy vegetables daily and a measured portion of plain, vitamin-C-fortified guinea pig pellets.

Critically, guinea pigs cannot make their own vitamin C and must get it from food. Offer C-rich vegetables like bell pepper, leafy greens, and parsley, and provide fresh water at all times. Avoid iceberg lettuce, sugary fruit in excess, and any rabbit food. Never feed chocolate, onions, or processed human snacks.

## Handling & enrichment

Approach calmly and scoop the guinea pig up with one hand under the chest and the other supporting the hindquarters, since a fall can seriously injure them. Daily floor time in a safe, enclosed area lets them run, popcorn (their joyful jumping), and explore. Tunnels, chew toys, and food puzzles provide mental stimulation, while gentle, regular handling builds trust over time.

## Health & lifespan

With good care, guinea pigs typically live 5 to 7 years. Common health issues include vitamin C deficiency (scurvy), dental overgrowth, respiratory infections, bladder stones, and bumblefoot from poor flooring. Watch for weight loss, reduced appetite, labored breathing, or crusty eyes, and weigh your pet weekly to catch problems early.

Because they are prey animals, guinea pigs hide illness well, so any change in eating or droppings warrants prompt attention from a vet experienced with exotic pets.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do guinea pigs need a friend?** Yes. They are herd animals and generally fare best living with at least one compatible companion of the same species; lone guinea pigs can become stressed and withdrawn.

**Can guinea pigs and rabbits live together?** It is not recommended. Rabbits can injure guinea pigs by kicking, carry bacteria harmful to them, and have very different dietary needs.

## Conclusion

Guinea pigs reward attentive owners with years of charming, vocal companionship. Give them a spacious cage, a steady supply of hay and vitamin C, gentle handling, and at least one friend, and these endearing little cavies will thrive as a delightful addition to the family.
