Koi are ornamental varieties of the common carp, prized for their striking colours and graceful movement. Kept in outdoor ponds for centuries in Japan and China, they are long-lived, hardy fish that can become genuinely tame, rising to greet their keepers at feeding time. They are a serious commitment rather than a casual aquarium fish.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | Typically 30-75 cm (1-2.5 ft) |
| Lifespan | Commonly 20-40+ years |
| Minimum tank size | Pond of around 4,000+ litres (1,000+ gallons) |
| Water type | Freshwater |
| Care level | Intermediate |

## Appearance & behaviour

Koi come in many named colour patterns, including white-and-red Kohaku, the black-marked Showa and Sanke, and metallic Ogon. Their bodies are powerful and torpedo-shaped, with a pair of barbels at the mouth. Koi are peaceful, social shoaling fish that do best in groups. They are curious and food-motivated, and many learn to take food from the hand. Being cold-water fish, they slow down and stop feeding in winter when temperatures drop.

## Tank setup & water parameters

Koi grow large and produce a lot of waste, so a spacious outdoor pond with strong filtration is essential; indoor tanks are unsuitable for the long term. Aim for a depth of at least 1.2-1.5 m to provide stable temperatures and refuge from predators. Provide a large biological filter, good aeration, and regular water changes.

- Temperature: roughly 15-25°C, with seasonal variation tolerated
- pH: around 7.0-8.5
- Ammonia and nitrite: 0 ppm; keep nitrate low through filtration and water changes

A secure pond edge and netting help protect koi from herons, cats, and other predators.

## Diet & feeding

Koi are omnivores with hearty appetites. Feed a quality floating koi pellet as a staple, supplemented with vegetables such as lettuce, peas, and slices of orange, plus occasional treats like earthworms. Feeding floating food lets you watch each fish and check its condition. Adjust feeding to water temperature: feed generously in warm weather but reduce as the water cools, and stop feeding entirely once temperatures fall below roughly 10°C, since their digestion slows dramatically. Avoid overfeeding, which fouls the water.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Koi are best kept with other koi or with goldfish, which share similar cold-water and water-quality needs. Keep them in groups, as they are social. Avoid housing them with small fish that could be eaten or with tropical species that require warmer water. Bear in mind that koi will uproot and nibble pond plants, so delicate aquatic plants may need protection or separate planting baskets.

## Health & common issues

Most koi health problems stem from poor water quality and overcrowding. Common issues include ich (white spot), fin rot, ulcers, and parasites such as flukes and anchor worm. Stress from sudden temperature swings or transport can trigger illness. Quarantine new fish before adding them to an established pond. Watch for clamped fins, listlessness, flashing (rubbing against surfaces), or loss of appetite, and consult a vet experienced with fish if problems arise. Good filtration, stable conditions, and routine water testing prevent most disease.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can koi live indoors in an aquarium?** Young koi can be housed indoors temporarily, but they grow large and need a sizeable outdoor pond to thrive long term. A standard home aquarium is not a permanent home.

**How long do koi live?** With good care, koi commonly live 20 to 40 years or more, and exceptionally long-lived individuals have reportedly reached much greater ages.

## Conclusion

Koi are rewarding, personable fish that can become lifelong companions, but they demand a large, well-filtered pond and consistent maintenance. Provide ample space, clean stable water, and a sensible seasonal diet, and a koi pond can bring decades of colour and calm to your garden.
