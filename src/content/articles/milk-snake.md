The Milk Snake (*Lampropeltis triangulum*) is a non-venomous North and Central American constrictor prized for its bold rings of red, black, and white or yellow. A type of kingsnake, it is hardy, manageable in size, and a popular choice for keepers moving beyond their first reptile.

## Overview

| Attribute | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 2–5 ft (depends on subspecies) |
| Lifespan | 15–20 years |
| Enclosure size | 20–40 gallon for most adults |
| Temperament | Generally docile, may be nervous |
| Care level | Beginner to intermediate |

## Appearance & temperament

Milk snakes display banded patterns that often mimic the venomous coral snake, a defense known as Batesian mimicry. With over 20 recognized subspecies, sizes and colors vary widely, from the small Honduran to the larger black milk snake. Most are slender with smooth, glossy scales. They are typically calm but can be flighty when young, sometimes vibrating their tails or releasing musk if startled. Regular gentle handling helps them settle.

## Habitat & enclosure

A secure, escape-proof enclosure is essential, as milk snakes are strong and persistent at testing lids. A 20-gallon long tank suits most adults, with larger subspecies needing 40 gallons or more. Provide a substrate that holds a slight burrow, such as aspen shavings or cypress mulch. Include at least two hides, one on the warm side and one on the cool side, plus a sturdy water bowl large enough for soaking. Branches and clutter give a sense of security.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Create a thermal gradient using an under-tank heater or overhead bulb controlled by a thermostat. Aim for a warm basking zone of about 85–88°F (29–31°C) and a cooler end near 70–75°F (21–24°C), with a nighttime drop allowed. Milk snakes are largely nocturnal and do not strictly require UVB, though low-level UVB can support overall health. Keep humidity moderate, around 40–60%, and raise it slightly during shedding to ensure clean, complete sheds.

## Diet & feeding

Milk snakes are carnivores that feed on appropriately sized rodents in captivity. Offer prey roughly as wide as the snake's thickest point. Hatchlings eat pinky mice every 5–7 days, while adults take adult mice or small rats every 7–14 days. Frozen-thawed prey is safest, warmed to body temperature before feeding. Always provide fresh water. Because some milk snakes are known to attempt cannibalism, house individuals separately.

## Handling & health

Support the snake's body and avoid sudden grabs; let it move through your hands rather than gripping it. Wait 24–48 hours after feeding before handling to prevent regurgitation. Common health concerns include retained shed, mites, respiratory infections (signs include wheezing or open-mouth breathing), and scale rot from damp conditions. Maintaining correct temperatures, humidity, and cleanliness prevents most problems. Consult a reptile veterinarian if symptoms persist.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are milk snakes venomous?** No. Despite mimicking the venomous coral snake, milk snakes are completely non-venomous and kill prey by constriction. They are harmless to humans, though a startled snake may bite or musk.

**Do milk snakes need a large enclosure?** Not usually. Most adults thrive in a 20-gallon long tank, with larger subspecies needing 40 gallons. Security and a proper thermal gradient matter more than sheer floor space.

## Conclusion

Milk snakes reward keepers with striking color, manageable size, and a long lifespan. With a secure enclosure, a correct thermal gradient, appropriately sized prey, and gentle handling, they make resilient and rewarding pets. As with any exotic reptile, check local and regional laws before acquiring one, since legality varies by location.
