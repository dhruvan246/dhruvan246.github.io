One of the oldest pointing breeds, the Portuguese Pointer (Perdigueiro Português) is a sturdy, medium-sized gundog from Portugal, prized for its keen nose, square head and famously affectionate, people-loving nature.

## Appearance & temperament

The Portuguese Pointer is a compact, well-muscled dog with a distinctive squarish head, a pronounced stop and expressive almond-shaped eyes. The short, close-lying coat is most commonly yellow (in various shades) and may carry white markings. Bred to work closely with hunters, the breed is exceptionally affectionate and craves human company, earning a reputation as a gentle, even-tempered companion. Most are friendly with children and sociable with other dogs, though their close-working heritage means they form strong bonds and dislike being left alone for long.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet suited to the dog's age, size and activity level. Active hunting or sporting individuals may need more calories than dogs kept mainly as companions, so adjust portions to maintain a lean, athletic body condition. Dividing the daily ration into two meals helps with digestion, and fresh water should always be available. Monitor weight regularly and consult a veterinarian before changing diets or if body condition shifts.

## Exercise & training

This is an energetic working breed that needs plenty of daily activity — brisk walks, running and, ideally, the chance to use its nose through scent games or fieldwork. A strong hunting drive means recall training and secure, fenced areas are important, as the breed will follow interesting scents. Portuguese Pointers are intelligent and genuinely eager to please, which makes them responsive to patient, reward-based training. Because they are sensitive and devoted, harsh methods are counterproductive; consistency, early socialisation and mental stimulation bring out their best.

## Grooming & care

Grooming demands are low. The short coat needs only weekly brushing to remove loose hair and keep it healthy, with occasional baths when needed. Check the drop ears regularly and keep them clean and dry, since folded ears can trap moisture. Routine nail trimming, dental care and a quick inspection for ticks, burrs or scrapes after outdoor activity round out a simple care routine.

## Health & lifespan

The Portuguese Pointer is generally a robust, healthy breed, typically living around 12 to 14 years. As with many active dogs, responsible owners watch for hip dysplasia and the ear infections that drop-eared breeds can develop. Choosing breeders who health-test their stock, keeping up with routine veterinary checkups, providing regular exercise and maintaining a healthy weight all support a long and active life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Portuguese Pointers good family dogs?**
Yes. They are notably affectionate and gentle, generally do well with children, and thrive on close family involvement, suiting active households that can include them in daily life.

**Do Portuguese Pointers need a lot of exercise?**
Yes. As a working gundog, the breed needs vigorous daily exercise and mental stimulation; without enough activity, they can become restless or unhappy.

## Conclusion

The Portuguese Pointer is a loving, hardworking gundog that rewards an active, attentive owner with loyalty and warmth. Provide ample exercise, gentle training and simple routine care, and this historic breed makes a devoted, easy-to-groom companion.
