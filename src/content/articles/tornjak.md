The Tornjak is a large livestock guardian dog from the mountainous regions of Bosnia and Herzegovina and Croatia, where it has protected flocks of sheep for centuries. Calm, dignified, and fiercely devoted to its family, this ancient working breed combines imposing size with a surprisingly gentle and steady temperament.

## Appearance & temperament

The Tornjak is a powerfully built, well-proportioned dog with a rectangular body, a broad head, and a thick, bushy tail often carried high. Its most striking feature is the long, abundant double coat, which forms a pronounced mane around the neck and feathering on the legs. Coats are usually parti-coloured, with patches of black, brown, or tan over a white base.

Temperamentally, the Tornjak is calm, self-assured, and highly independent. Bred to make decisions alone while guarding livestock, it is alert and protective without being needlessly aggressive. It bonds deeply with its family, is gentle with children it knows, and is naturally wary of strangers. Early, consistent socialisation is essential to channel its guarding instincts appropriately.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality diet formulated for large breeds, divided into two meals a day to reduce the risk of bloat. Portion sizes should match the dog's age, weight, and activity level, since working Tornjaks burn far more energy than companion dogs. Because the breed grows slowly and matures late, large-breed puppy food with controlled calcium and balanced energy supports healthy joint development. Always provide fresh water, and monitor body condition to avoid excess weight, which strains the joints.

## Exercise & training

Though not hyperactive, the Tornjak needs daily exercise to stay content. Long walks, secure off-lead roaming, and a job to do suit it best. A large, well-fenced yard is strongly recommended, as this breed will patrol and guard its territory.

Training requires patience and respect. The Tornjak is intelligent but independent and will not respond to harsh or repetitive drilling. Calm, consistent, reward-based methods work best, ideally started in puppyhood. Early socialisation with people, other animals, and varied environments helps produce a balanced adult guardian.

## Grooming & care

The dense double coat needs brushing once or twice a week to remove loose hair and prevent matting, increasing to several times weekly during the heavy seasonal shedding periods in spring and autumn. The coat is naturally weather-resistant and largely self-cleaning, so bathing is only needed occasionally. Routinely check and trim the nails, clean the ears, and maintain dental hygiene.

## Health & lifespan

The Tornjak is generally a hardy, robust breed with few inherited problems, partly thanks to its natural working heritage. As with most large dogs, hip and elbow dysplasia can occur, and the deep chest means owners should watch for signs of bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus). Routine veterinary check-ups, parasite control, and weight management support a long life. With good care, most Tornjaks live around 11 to 14 years.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Tornjaks good family pets?**
Yes, for the right home. They are devoted and gentle with their family but need space, early socialisation, and an owner experienced with large guardian breeds.

**Do Tornjaks bark a lot?**
As guardians, they will bark to alert their family to anything unusual. Proper training and adequate stimulation help keep barking purposeful rather than excessive.

## Conclusion

The Tornjak is a majestic, loyal guardian best suited to active owners with space and an appreciation for independent working breeds. Given early socialisation, regular exercise, and consistent care, it rewards its family with calm devotion and steadfast protection for many years.
