Famous as the fastest dog breed, the Greyhound is built for short bursts of remarkable speed yet spends most of its day relaxing. Gentle, quiet, and affectionate, Greyhounds, including retired racers, make calm and easygoing companions.

## Overview

| Trait | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Size | Large (typically 27–40 kg) |
| Lifespan | Around 10–14 years |
| Coat | Short, fine, smooth |
| Energy | Moderate (sprinter, not endurance) |
| Good with kids | Yes — gentle and tolerant |

## Appearance & temperament

The Greyhound is a tall, lean, and aerodynamic sighthound with a deep chest, narrow waist, long legs, and a smooth, close-lying coat that comes in many colors and patterns. Despite their athletic build, they are calm, sensitive, and affectionate at home, often content to lounge for hours. They tend to be quiet and bond closely with their families, though their independent sighthound nature means they can be a little reserved with strangers.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food appropriate for the dog's age and activity level. Greyhounds are naturally lean, so it is normal for ribs and hip bones to be lightly visible; the goal is a trim but well-muscled body rather than a heavy one. Divide food into measured meals, provide constant access to fresh water, and avoid overfeeding or excessive treats. Because deep-chested breeds may be more prone to bloat, many owners avoid heavy exercise right after large meals. Consult your veterinarian for advice tailored to your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

Greyhounds are sprinters rather than long-distance athletes. A couple of daily walks plus an opportunity to run flat out in a safely enclosed area usually keeps them happy and healthy. Between bursts of activity, they are famously relaxed. Like most sighthounds, Greyhounds have a strong prey drive and unreliable recall, so they should be kept on a leash or within secure fencing. They respond best to gentle, reward-based training and consistent, patient handling; harsh methods do not suit their sensitive temperament.

## Grooming & care

The short coat is very low-maintenance and needs only a quick weekly brush and the occasional bath. Routine care includes regular nail trimming, dental brushing, and ear checks. Greyhounds have little body fat and a thin coat, so they feel the cold easily; a dog coat in cold or wet weather and a soft, padded bed to cushion their bony frame are appreciated. Their thin skin can be prone to nicks and tears, so check for minor injuries after vigorous play.

## Health & lifespan

Greyhounds are generally a robust breed with a typical lifespan of roughly 10 to 14 years. Breed-linked considerations owners often discuss with their vet include sensitivity to anesthesia common in sighthounds, the risk of bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus) in deep-chested dogs, dental disease, and skin injuries from their thin skin. Routine veterinary checkups, dental care, and a healthy weight all support a long, comfortable life. Always seek professional veterinary guidance for any health concerns.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Greyhounds hyperactive?**
No. While they can run very fast in short bursts, Greyhounds are calm and quiet indoors and spend much of the day sleeping or lounging.

**Are retired racing Greyhounds good pets?**
Yes. Many retired racers adapt well to home life and become gentle, affectionate companions, though they may need time and patience to adjust to a household routine.

## Conclusion

The Greyhound pairs astonishing speed with a remarkably gentle, laid-back personality. With a daily chance to sprint, warmth in cold weather, a soft bed, and routine care, this affectionate sighthound makes a serene and devoted family companion.
