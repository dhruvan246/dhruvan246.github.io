The Russian Tortoise (*Testudo horsfieldii*), also called the Horsfield's or steppe tortoise, is one of the most popular pet tortoises. Hardy, personable, and compact, it suits keepers who can provide a dry, spacious habitat and a high-fiber plant diet.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 5-8 inches (13-20 cm) shell length |
| Lifespan | 40+ years with good care |
| Enclosure size | 4 x 2 feet minimum; larger is better |
| Temperament | Active, curious, bold; generally tolerant |
| Care level | Beginner to intermediate |

## Appearance & temperament

Russian Tortoises have a rounded, tan-to-olive shell with darker brown blotches and a relatively flat top. They are easily recognized by having only four claws on each foot, unlike most other tortoises. Females are usually slightly larger than males, while males often have longer tails.

These tortoises are famously active and inquisitive. They dig enthusiastically, explore their surroundings, and many learn to recognize their keepers at feeding time. They can also be surprisingly determined escape artists, so secure boundaries are essential.

## Habitat & enclosure

Adult Russian Tortoises do best in a large, low enclosure with an open floor plan. A tortoise table or a custom-built pen of at least 4 x 2 feet is preferable to a standard glass aquarium, which limits airflow. Provide a substrate they can burrow into, such as a mix of topsoil and coconut coir or play sand, several inches deep.

Include a shallow water dish for soaking and drinking, a hide for security, and flat rocks or sturdy plants for enrichment. Because these tortoises burrow, walls should extend below the substrate or be otherwise dig-proof. Outdoor time in a predator-safe, escape-proof pen is excellent when temperatures allow.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Provide a thermal gradient: a basking spot around 90-95°F (32-35°C) at one end and a cooler zone near 70-75°F (21-24°C) at the other. Nighttime temperatures can drop into the mid-60s°F (around 18°C). Use a basking bulb for warmth and a separate UVB tube light, which is essential for vitamin D3 synthesis and proper shell and bone development.

Russian Tortoises come from arid steppe regions, so they require relatively low ambient humidity. Keep the enclosure dry and well-ventilated, providing moisture mainly through water dishes and regular soaks rather than damp substrate.

## Diet & feeding

A Russian Tortoise's diet should be high in fiber and calcium and low in protein, fruit, and sugar. Offer a variety of leafy weeds and greens such as dandelion, clover, plantain weed, endive, collard greens, mustard greens, and edible flowers. Grass hay can supplement the diet.

Avoid feeding fruit (offer only rarely, if at all), iceberg lettuce, spinach in excess, and animal protein. Dust food with a calcium supplement several times a week, and offer a cuttlebone for additional calcium. Fresh water should always be available.

## Handling & health

Russian Tortoises generally tolerate brief, gentle handling, but excessive handling causes stress. Always wash your hands before and after contact, as reptiles can carry *Salmonella*. Support the tortoise's body and keep it low to the ground to prevent injury from falls.

Watch for signs of illness such as runny eyes or nose, wheezing, lethargy, a soft or pyramided shell, or loss of appetite. These can indicate respiratory infection, metabolic bone disease, or poor husbandry. An annual check-up with a reptile-experienced veterinarian helps catch problems early.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Russian Tortoises hibernate?** In the wild they brumate (a reptile dormancy) during cold months. Captive hibernation is possible but should only be attempted with a healthy adult and proper guidance from an experienced keeper or vet.

**Are Russian Tortoises legal to own?** They are widely kept, but legality and any permit or import rules vary by region, so check your local and national wildlife regulations before acquiring one.

## Conclusion

With a spacious dry enclosure, proper heating and UVB, and a fiber-rich weed-based diet, a Russian Tortoise can be a rewarding companion for decades. Their hardy nature and engaging personality make them a favorite, but their long lifespan and specific husbandry needs mean ownership is a serious, long-term commitment.
