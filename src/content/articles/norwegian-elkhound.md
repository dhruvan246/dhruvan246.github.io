One of the ancient Nordic spitz breeds, the Norwegian Elkhound was developed in Norway to track and hold large game such as moose ("elg") and to serve as a hardy farm and watchdog. Sturdy, weatherproof and deeply loyal, it remains a bold and devoted companion today.

## Appearance & temperament

The Norwegian Elkhound is a compact, square-built spitz with a wedge-shaped head, pricked ears and a tightly curled tail carried over the back. Its hallmark is a thick grey double coat with black-tipped guard hairs, a lighter undercoat, and darker shading on the muzzle, ears and tail.

Temperamentally, the breed is bold, friendly and confident without being aggressive. Elkhounds are alert and make excellent watchdogs, often vocal when something is amiss. They bond strongly with their families but can be independent and strong-willed, a legacy of working at a distance from the hunter.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food appropriate to the Elkhound's age, size and activity level. This breed gains weight easily and is prone to obesity, so measure portions, limit treats and avoid free-feeding. Divide the daily ration into two meals and provide constant access to fresh water. Adjust amounts as activity changes, and consult your veterinarian for guidance tailored to your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

Bred for long days afield, the Elkhound has notable stamina and needs daily exercise — brisk walks, hikes, play and securely fenced running. Mental stimulation matters as much as physical activity, so puzzle toys, scent games and training keep this clever breed satisfied.

Elkhounds are intelligent but independent, and they respond best to patient, consistent, reward-based training. Early socialization helps. Because their hunting heritage gives them a strong prey drive and a tendency to roam, a secure yard and a reliable recall are important; many do best on leash in open areas.

## Grooming & care

The dense double coat is relatively odor-free and dirt-shedding but requires regular brushing — typically a few times a week — to manage loose hair. During the heavy seasonal sheds ("blowing coat") in spring and autumn, daily brushing helps remove the loosening undercoat. Never shave the double coat, as it insulates against both cold and heat. Otherwise, care is straightforward: occasional baths, routine nail trims, ear checks and regular dental care.

## Health & lifespan

The Norwegian Elkhound is generally a hardy, long-lived breed, often reaching 12 to 15 years. Like many breeds, it can be predisposed to certain conditions, including hip dysplasia, progressive retinal atrophy and other eye disorders, and a hereditary kidney condition (renal dysplasia) seen in some lines. Obesity is a common avoidable problem. Choosing a breeder who health-tests their stock, keeping your dog at a healthy weight and maintaining routine veterinary care all support a long, comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Norwegian Elkhounds shed a lot?**
Yes. The thick double coat sheds year-round and heavily during seasonal coat changes in spring and autumn, so regular brushing is essential.

**Are Norwegian Elkhounds good family dogs?**
Generally yes — they are loyal, friendly and good with children when well socialized, though their alert, vocal nature and independent streak suit owners who can provide structure and exercise.

## Conclusion

The Norwegian Elkhound is a robust, loyal and spirited companion built for an active home. Give it plenty of exercise, consistent training, regular coat care and sensible feeding, and this ancient Nordic breed will reward you with years of devoted, watchful companionship.
