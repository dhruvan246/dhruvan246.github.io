Stick insects, also called phasmids, are among the easiest and most fascinating invertebrate pets to keep. Their twig-like bodies, slow movements, and undemanding nature make them popular with beginners, children, and classrooms. With a simple enclosure and a steady supply of fresh leaves, these gentle insects thrive in captivity.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Size | 5-30 cm depending on species |
| Lifespan | 1-2 years (often shorter for males) |
| Housing | Tall mesh or glass enclosure |
| Temperament | Calm, slow-moving, non-aggressive |
| Care level | Easy, beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & behaviour

Stick insects are masters of camouflage, with elongated bodies that mimic twigs, stems, or leaves. Colours range from brown and green to mottled grey, and some species develop wings as adults. The Indian (or laboratory) stick insect, *Carausius morosus*, is the most commonly kept and reaches around 8-10 cm. Larger species such as the Australian or Macleay's spectre can be considerably bigger.

Most species are nocturnal, becoming active and feeding at night while staying motionless during the day. When threatened, they may sway gently to imitate a twig in the breeze, drop from their perch, or shed a limb to escape. Many species reproduce by parthenogenesis, meaning females can lay fertile eggs without a male.

## Housing & environment

Choose an enclosure that is at least three times the insect's body length in height, as stick insects need vertical space to hang and shed their skin. Mesh or netting cages provide good ventilation, while glass terrariums with mesh lids also work well. Stand twigs and leafy branches upright so the insect can climb.

Keep humidity moderate by misting the enclosure lightly with water once a day; the insect will drink the droplets. Most common species do well at normal room temperature, roughly 20-25 degrees Celsius, away from direct sun and draughts. Line the floor with paper towel or a thin substrate to make cleaning easy.

## Diet & feeding

Stick insects are herbivores, and each species has preferred food plants. Bramble (blackberry), oak, hawthorn, rose, ivy, and eucalyptus are common favourites depending on species. Always research what your particular species eats before bringing it home.

Provide a fresh supply of leaves and replace them before they wilt. Stand cut branches in a small jar of water to keep them fresh, but cover the opening so the insect cannot fall in and drown. Wash wild-foraged leaves to remove pesticides and parasites.

## Handling & care

Stick insects are delicate and should be handled gently and infrequently. Never pull or grab an insect that is gripping a surface, as this can cause it to lose a leg. Instead, coax it onto your flat hand and let it walk. Always handle over a soft surface in case it falls.

Daily care is minimal: mist the enclosure, check the food, and remove droppings. Clean the substrate regularly to prevent mould.

## Health

The most common problems come from incorrect humidity. Too little moisture can cause a difficult or failed moult, where the insect becomes trapped in its old skin. A failed moult is often fatal and is best prevented by maintaining adequate humidity and vertical space. Mould, mites, and fungal growth from over-misting or stale food can also harm them.

A healthy stick insect feeds well, climbs actively at night, and moults cleanly. If your insect stops eating or struggles to moult, review temperature, humidity, and food freshness first.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do stick insects need a heat lamp?** Most commonly kept species are comfortable at normal room temperature and do not need extra heat. Some tropical species prefer warmer conditions, so check the needs of your specific species.

**Are stick insects legal to keep everywhere?** No. Legality varies by region, and some species are restricted as potential agricultural pests. Check local regulations before keeping or releasing any stick insect, and never release captive insects into the wild.

## Conclusion

Stick insects are quiet, low-maintenance, and endlessly intriguing pets that suit almost any household. Give them a tall, well-ventilated enclosure, a steady supply of fresh leaves, and gentle daily misting, and these living twigs will reward you with months of fascinating behaviour.
