The Neapolitan Mastiff, or Mastino Napoletano, is an ancient Italian guardian breed instantly recognized by its enormous frame, loose folds of skin, and abundant facial wrinkles. Calm, loyal, and intensely devoted to family, the "Mastino" is a serious commitment best suited to experienced owners with space to spare.

## Appearance & temperament

The Neapolitan Mastiff is a heavy, powerful dog standing roughly 24-31 inches at the shoulder, with males notably larger than females. Its hallmarks are the abundant loose skin, pendulous jowls, drooping lower eyelids, and deep wrinkles across the face and neck. The short coat comes in shades of gray (blue), black, mahogany, and tawny, sometimes with brindle markings.

Despite an imposing, almost intimidating look, the breed is typically steady and affectionate with its own people. Mastinos are naturally wary of strangers and protective, making early, consistent socialization essential. They tend to be quiet, dignified, and somewhat independent rather than playful or clingy.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality diet formulated for large or giant breeds, with controlled calcium and balanced calories to support slow, steady growth in puppies. Rapid growth and excess weight strain developing joints, so avoid overfeeding. Adults usually do well split across two measured meals a day rather than free-feeding.

Because of their deep chests, Neapolitan Mastiffs are prone to bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus). Avoid heavy exercise right around mealtimes, and discuss feeding strategies with your veterinarian. Always provide constant access to fresh water, and note that this breed drools heavily, especially after eating or drinking.

## Exercise & training

The Mastino has modest exercise needs for its size. A couple of daily walks and access to a securely fenced yard are usually enough to keep an adult fit. Avoid strenuous activity in heat, as the breed's bulk and short muzzle make it sensitive to overheating. Limit high-impact exercise in puppies whose joints are still maturing.

Training should begin early and rely on calm, consistent, positive methods. These dogs are intelligent but strong-willed, and their size means good manners are non-negotiable. Firm, fair leadership and thorough socialization help produce a confident, well-mannered guardian.

## Grooming & care

The short coat is low-maintenance, needing only weekly brushing to remove loose hair. The real work lies in the skin folds and wrinkles, which must be wiped and dried regularly to prevent moisture, dirt, and infection. Clean the facial folds and around the eyes routinely.

Trim nails, check ears, and keep a drool cloth handy. Provide cool, comfortable resting spots and a supportive bed to protect joints and reduce pressure sores on such a heavy frame.

## Health & lifespan

Neapolitan Mastiffs typically live around 7-9 years, shorter than many smaller breeds. Common concerns include hip and elbow dysplasia, bloat, heart conditions such as cardiomyopathy, and eye issues like cherry eye and entropion related to the loose facial skin. Skin-fold dermatitis is also frequent.

Choosing a reputable breeder who screens breeding stock, maintaining a healthy weight, and scheduling regular veterinary checkups give a Mastino the best chance at a comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Neapolitan Mastiffs good family dogs?** Yes, they are devoted and gentle with their families, including children, but their size and protective nature mean supervision and early socialization are important.

**Do Neapolitan Mastiffs drool a lot?** Yes. Their loose jowls cause heavy drooling, particularly after eating, drinking, or exercise, so owners should expect to wipe up frequently.

## Conclusion

The Neapolitan Mastiff is a majestic, loyal guardian wrapped in wrinkles and devotion. With proper socialization, attentive skin and joint care, weight management, and an awareness of its shorter lifespan, the Mastino rewards committed owners with steadfast companionship and a watchful, loving presence.
