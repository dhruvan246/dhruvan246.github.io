The Canadian Eskimo Dog is one of North America's oldest and rarest working breeds, developed by the Inuit of the Arctic to haul sleds and hunt across frozen terrain. Also known as the Canadian Inuit Dog or Qimmiq, it is a robust, hard-driving spitz built for endurance in extreme cold rather than for the show ring. This is a serious working dog with intense energy and deep pack instincts.

## Appearance & temperament

The Canadian Eskimo Dog is a powerfully built spitz with a deep chest, muscular frame, erect triangular ears, and a bushy tail that curls over the back. Males stand roughly 23-28 inches at the shoulder and are noticeably larger than females. The dense double coat comes in many colors and patterns, including white, black, grey, red, and combinations of these.

Temperamentally, this is an affectionate but intensely energetic and tough breed. Bred to work in cooperative teams, these dogs have strong pack drives, a powerful prey instinct, and a tendency toward dominance. They are loyal and people-oriented but are not couch dogs; they thrive when they have a job to do and can become destructive or vocal if under-stimulated.

## Diet & nutrition

As a hard-working Arctic breed with a fast metabolism, the Canadian Eskimo Dog needs a high-quality, protein-rich diet that supports its activity level. Active and working dogs may require considerably more calories than a typical large breed, while companion dogs need careful portioning to avoid weight gain. Feed measured meals two or three times a day rather than free-feeding, and adjust amounts to the individual dog's workload and condition. Always provide constant access to fresh water, especially after vigorous exercise. Consult your veterinarian for a feeding plan suited to your dog's age and activity.

## Exercise & training

Few breeds need as much exercise as this one. Bred to pull sleds over long distances, the Canadian Eskimo Dog requires vigorous daily activity, ideally hours of running, hiking, pulling, or other strenuous work. Without sufficient outlet, these dogs become frustrated, noisy, and destructive.

Training requires firm, consistent, and patient leadership. These intelligent dogs respond best to positive, reward-based methods but can be independent and strong-willed. Early socialization is essential, particularly given their high prey drive toward smaller animals. They should be kept securely contained, as they are skilled diggers and escape artists.

## Grooming & care

The dense double coat sheds heavily, especially during seasonal "blowouts" when the soft undercoat is shed in clumps. Brush several times a week year-round and daily during heavy shedding periods to manage loose fur and prevent matting. Bathing is rarely needed beyond a few times a year. This breed tolerates cold superbly but struggles in heat, so provide shade, water, and avoid strenuous activity during hot weather. Routine nail trims, ear checks, and dental care round out basic maintenance.

## Health & lifespan

The Canadian Eskimo Dog is generally a hardy, naturally bred dog with a typical lifespan of around 10 to 13 years. As a large, deep-chested breed it can be prone to bloat (gastric torsion), and hip dysplasia and eye conditions may occur. Heat sensitivity is an important practical concern given the dense coat. Buying from a responsible breeder who screens breeding stock, plus routine veterinary checkups, helps support long-term health.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Canadian Eskimo Dogs good for first-time owners?**
Generally no. Their extreme exercise needs, independent temperament, strong prey drive, and pack-oriented behavior make them a demanding breed best suited to experienced, active owners.

**Can they live in warm climates?**
They are built for the Arctic and cope poorly with heat. They can live in milder regions with careful management, shade, and avoidance of exercise during hot parts of the day, but extreme heat is dangerous for them.

## Conclusion

The Canadian Eskimo Dog is a rare, rugged, and deeply historic working breed that rewards the right owner with loyalty and remarkable stamina. It demands serious exercise, firm guidance, and a cool environment, making it a poor fit for casual pet homes but an outstanding partner for active people who can meet its considerable needs.
