The Red-Eyed Tree Frog (*Agalychnis callidryas*) is one of the most recognisable amphibians in the world, prized for its vivid green body, blue-and-orange flanks, orange feet, and brilliant red eyes. Native to the rainforests of Central America, it makes a rewarding display animal for keepers who can provide a warm, humid, planted home and a steady supply of live insects.

## Overview

| Attribute | Typical range |
|-----------|---------------|

| Temperament | Shy, nocturnal, not a handling pet |

## Appearance & behaviour

Red-Eyed Tree Frogs are slender, arboreal frogs with large adhesive toe pads for climbing. Their famous red eyes and flashes of colour are thought to startle predators, a defence known as deimatic display. By day they tuck their limbs in and rest on the undersides of leaves, masking their bright colours and appearing as a plain green leaf. They become active at night, climbing and hunting. These frogs are social enough to be kept in small groups of similar size, but they are strictly look-don't-touch animals.

## Habitat & water

Provide a tall vivarium with plenty of vertical climbing space: sturdy branches, cork bark, and broad-leaved live or artificial plants such as pothos or bromeliads. Maintain daytime temperatures of about 75–82°F (24–28°C), dropping a few degrees at night, and keep humidity high at 70–90% through daily misting. Use a low-wattage UVB light on a 12-hour cycle and a reliable temperature gradient. Substrate should hold moisture without staying soggy; coconut fibre or sphagnum moss works well. Always include a shallow dish of clean, dechlorinated water, as these frogs absorb water and toxins through their permeable skin. Never use tap water that has not been treated.

## Diet & feeding

Red-Eyed Tree Frogs are insectivores. Offer appropriately sized live crickets as a staple, supplemented with other feeders such as roaches, flightless fruit flies, and the occasional waxworm as a treat. Feed in the evening when the frog is active. Adults are typically fed every two to three days, while juveniles need food more often to support growth. Dust insects with a calcium supplement at most feedings and a multivitamin with vitamin D3 once or twice a week to prevent metabolic bone disease. Gut-load feeder insects with nutritious vegetables before offering them.

## Handling & health

These frogs should rarely be handled. Their skin is delicate and absorbs oils, lotions, and residues from human hands, which can harm them. If you must move a frog, wear clean, damp, powder-free gloves and keep contact brief. Watch for signs of trouble such as weight loss, lethargy, cloudy or sunken eyes, bloating, or red, irritated skin, which can indicate poor husbandry or infection. An exotic-animal veterinarian should handle any illness. Good hygiene, stable humidity, and clean water prevent most common problems.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Red-Eyed Tree Frogs good for beginners?**
They are best for keepers with some experience, because they need precise humidity, temperature, and live-food husbandry. A committed first-time keeper can succeed with research and proper equipment.

**Is it legal to keep one?**
*Agalychnis callidryas* is widely available in the pet trade, but laws on owning, importing, or collecting amphibians vary by country and region. Always check local regulations and buy captive-bred animals.

## Conclusion

The Red-Eyed Tree Frog is a stunning, low-handling display amphibian that thrives when its rainforest needs are met. With a tall planted vivarium, warm humid conditions, clean dechlorinated water, and a varied dusted insect diet, these frogs can live healthy lives and reward keepers with nightly activity and unforgettable colour.
