The German Longhaired Pointer (GLP) is a versatile continental gundog bred to point, retrieve and work both on land and in water. Calm and affectionate at home yet tireless in the field, it suits active owners who want an even-tempered, hardworking companion.

## Overview

| Trait | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Size | Large (around 25–32 kg) |
| Lifespan | 12–14 years |
| Coat | Medium-length, wavy, feathered |
| Energy | Very high |
| Good with kids | Good |

## Appearance & temperament

The GLP is a strongly built, athletic dog with a moderately long, slightly wavy coat that carries feathering on the ears, legs, tail and underside. The coat is most commonly brown (often called liver) or brown roan, sometimes with white markings. The expression is kind and intelligent.

Temperamentally, the breed is calm, friendly and people-oriented. GLPs form close bonds with their family and are generally good with children and other dogs when well socialised. They are eager to please and biddable, but they need a job to do and dislike being left alone for long stretches.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet appropriate to the dog's age, weight and high activity level. Working or very active GLPs may need more calories during hunting season and less in quieter periods. Divide the daily ration into two meals rather than one large feeding, and avoid heavy exercise right after eating, as deep-chested breeds can be prone to bloat. Keep fresh water available and monitor body condition to prevent excess weight.

## Exercise & training

This is a high-energy sporting breed that needs substantial daily exercise — well beyond a stroll around the block. Running, swimming, fetch, scent work and dog sports all help burn energy and satisfy the breed's strong working drive. A bored, under-exercised GLP may become restless or destructive.

GLPs are intelligent and respond best to positive, consistent, reward-based training. Early socialisation and recall training are valuable given the breed's hunting instincts. Their willingness to work makes them rewarding to train for owners who stay engaged.

## Grooming & care

The medium-length coat is fairly low-maintenance. Weekly brushing keeps it clean and tangle-free, with extra attention to the feathered areas, which can pick up burrs and debris after outdoor work. Check and clean the ears regularly, as drop ears can trap moisture, and keep nails trimmed and teeth cared for. Bathe only as needed.

## Health & lifespan

The German Longhaired Pointer is generally a hardy, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of around 12 to 14 years. As with many large, deep-chested sporting dogs, responsible owners stay alert to hip dysplasia, ear infections, and bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus). Choosing puppies from health-screened parents, keeping the dog at a healthy weight, and scheduling routine veterinary checkups all support long-term wellbeing. Always consult your veterinarian about any health concerns.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the German Longhaired Pointer a good family dog?**
Yes, for active households. The breed is gentle, affectionate and generally good with children, provided it gets the exercise and companionship it needs.

**How much exercise does a German Longhaired Pointer need?**
A lot — this is a working gundog that thrives on vigorous daily activity such as running, swimming and scent games, ideally totalling well over an hour.

## Conclusion

The German Longhaired Pointer is a calm, devoted and highly capable sporting dog that rewards an active owner with loyalty and versatility. Meet its needs for exercise, mental stimulation and companionship, and the GLP makes a steady, affectionate family companion.
