The Pudelpointer is a versatile German hunting dog created in the late 19th century by crossing the Poodle with the English Pointer. The aim was a single dog that could point, retrieve and work in water across field and forest. Today it remains an uncommon but highly capable gundog, valued for its trainability, even temperament and weather-resistant coat.

## Appearance & temperament

The Pudelpointer is a balanced, athletic dog of medium-to-large build, with a strong frame suited to a full day in the field. Its hallmark is a harsh, wiry outer coat over a softer undercoat, usually seen in shades of brown, liver or chestnut, sometimes with small white markings. Many have a distinctive beard and bushy eyebrows.

In temperament, the breed is calm, intelligent and eager to please. Pudelpointers tend to bond closely with their families and are generally friendly and even-tempered. They are alert without being excessively noisy, and their strong working drive means they are happiest when given a job to do.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food appropriate for an active large breed and the dog's life stage. Because the Pudelpointer is energetic, working individuals may need more calories during hunting season and fewer in the off-season. Divide the daily amount into two meals and adjust portions to keep the dog at a healthy weight, since extra pounds strain the joints. Always provide fresh water, especially after exercise. Ask your veterinarian for guidance tailored to your dog.

## Exercise & training

This is a high-energy breed that needs substantial daily activity, ideally an hour or more of vigorous exercise such as running, swimming, fetch or fieldwork. Without enough outlet for their stamina and intelligence, Pudelpointers can become bored and restless. Mental challenges like scent games and training drills are as important as physical exercise.

Fortunately, they are very trainable. Their Poodle heritage gives them quick minds, and they respond best to consistent, reward-based methods. Early socialization and basic obedience set a strong foundation for these willing learners.

## Grooming & care

The wiry coat is relatively low-maintenance but does benefit from regular care. Brush weekly to remove loose hair and debris, and consider occasional hand-stripping or trimming to maintain the harsh texture, particularly on working dogs. Bathe only as needed. Check and clean the ears regularly, as drop ears can trap moisture, and trim nails and brush teeth on a routine schedule.

## Health & lifespan

The Pudelpointer is generally a hardy, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of around 13 to 14 years. As with many active large dogs, hip dysplasia is a consideration, and responsible breeders screen their stock. Ear infections can occur because of the floppy ears, so routine ear care helps. Regular veterinary checkups, parasite prevention and a healthy weight support a long, active life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Pudelpointers good family pets?** Yes. They are typically calm, affectionate and loyal companions at home, provided their high exercise needs are met. Supervise interactions with young children as you would with any dog.

**Do Pudelpointers shed a lot?** They are generally moderate shedders. Their wiry coat sheds less heavily than many breeds, and weekly brushing keeps loose hair under control.

## Conclusion

The Pudelpointer is a smart, athletic and affectionate breed that thrives with an active owner. Bred for versatile hunting work, it brings stamina, trainability and a friendly disposition to the home. Give it ample exercise, consistent training and routine grooming, and the Pudelpointer will be a devoted and capable companion for many years.
