The Western Hognose Snake (*Heterodon nasicus*) is a small, stout, upturned-snouted colubrid from the grasslands and prairies of central North America. Its quirky personality, manageable size, and dramatic bluffing displays have made it one of the most popular pet snakes for beginners and experienced keepers alike.

## Overview

| Trait | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Adult size | Females 24-36 in (60-90 cm); males 14-24 in (35-60 cm) |
| Lifespan | 10-18 years in captivity |
| Enclosure size | 20-40 gallon for an adult |
| Temperament | Docile, curious, prone to bluffing rather than biting |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & temperament

Western Hognose Snakes have a heavy, keeled-scaled body and a distinctive upturned "pig-like" snout used for digging in loose soil. Coloration is typically tan, sand, or olive with darker dorsal blotches, though many captive-bred morphs (albino, anaconda, axanthic) now exist. They are famous for their theatrical defense: flattening the neck like a cobra, hissing, and "playing dead" by rolling belly-up. They are mildly rear-fanged with a weak saliva that is harmless to most people but can cause localized swelling in rare cases.

## Habitat & enclosure

A single adult does well in a 20-40 gallon terrarium with a secure, ventilated lid. These are fossorial snakes that love to burrow, so provide 2-4 inches of a loose, dig-friendly substrate such as aspen shavings or a soil-sand blend. Avoid dusty or aromatic substrates like cedar. Include at least two hides (one on each end), a sturdy water bowl, and a few decor items. Keep enclosures separate, as hognoses should always be housed individually.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Use a thermal gradient with a basking spot of 88-92°F (31-33°C) at one end and an ambient cool side of 75-80°F (24-27°C), dropping a few degrees at night. An under-tank heat mat or low-wattage overhead heater on a thermostat works well. While they are not strictly UVB-dependent, low-level UVB lighting supports overall health. Keep humidity moderate at around 30-50%; a slightly more humid hide aids shedding. Always monitor temperatures with a digital thermometer.

## Diet & feeding

In the wild, hognoses favor amphibians, but captive snakes thrive on appropriately sized frozen-thawed rodents about as wide as the snake's body. Hatchlings eat pinky mice every 5-7 days, while adults take an adult mouse every 7-14 days. Some individuals, especially males, can be picky feeders; scenting prey or offering it in the evening often helps. Always provide fresh water and avoid handling for 24-48 hours after a meal to prevent regurgitation.

## Handling & health

Hognoses are generally gentle and tolerate regular, calm handling, which helps them stay tame. Support the body and avoid sudden movements. Watch for common health issues such as respiratory infections (wheezing, bubbling), retained shed, mites, and refusal to eat. Maintaining correct temperatures, humidity, and cleanliness prevents most problems. Schedule a checkup with a reptile-experienced veterinarian if symptoms persist.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Western Hognose Snakes venomous?** They are technically mildly venomous rear-fanged snakes, but their toxin is weak and they almost never bite humans, instead preferring to bluff. Reactions, if any, are usually minor localized swelling.

**Is it legal to own a Western Hognose Snake?** In many areas yes, but laws vary by country, state, and city, and some places require permits. Always check local regulations before acquiring one.

## Conclusion

The Western Hognose Snake combines a compact size, a charming personality, and straightforward care, making it an excellent choice for keepers seeking a low-maintenance yet endearing reptile. With a proper burrowing setup, accurate temperatures, a consistent feeding routine, and gentle handling, your hognose can stay healthy and entertaining for well over a decade.
