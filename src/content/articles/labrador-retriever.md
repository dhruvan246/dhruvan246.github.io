Friendly, eager to please and endlessly adaptable, the Labrador Retriever is one of the world's most popular dogs. Originally bred to retrieve game and help fishermen, the Lab today thrives as a family companion, assistance dog and active outdoor partner.

## Appearance & temperament

Labradors are solidly built, athletic dogs with a broad head, kind expression and a distinctive thick "otter" tail. The short double coat comes in three recognised colours: yellow, black and chocolate. Temperamentally, Labs are outgoing, gentle and people-oriented. They tend to be sociable with strangers and other animals, which makes them poor guard dogs but wonderful family pets. Their patience and tolerance are a large part of why they are so widely chosen for homes with children.

## Diet & nutrition

Labradors are famously food-motivated and prone to overeating, so portion control is essential to prevent obesity. Feed a complete, balanced diet appropriate to the dog's age, size and activity level, and measure meals rather than free-feeding. Treats should be counted as part of the daily total. During growth, a large-breed puppy formula supports steady, healthy development. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian if you are unsure about the right amount to feed.

## Exercise & training

This is an energetic working breed that generally needs at least an hour or more of activity each day. Walks, games of fetch and swimming all suit the Lab well, as most love water. Without enough physical and mental stimulation, they can become bored and develop unwanted chewing or digging habits, especially as adolescents. The good news is that Labradors are highly intelligent and respond beautifully to positive, reward-based training. Early socialisation and consistent, gentle handling bring out their best.

## Grooming & care

The short coat is fairly low-maintenance but does shed, often heavily during seasonal coat changes. Weekly brushing helps manage loose hair, increasing to several times a week during heavy sheds. Bathe only as needed. Check and clean the ears regularly, as the floppy shape can trap moisture, and keep up routine nail trimming and dental care.

## Health & lifespan

Labradors are generally robust but, like many large breeds, can be predisposed to certain conditions. These commonly include hip and elbow dysplasia, obesity, ear infections and some inherited eye conditions. As a deep-chested breed they can also be at risk of bloat. Choosing a breeder who health-tests their dogs, maintaining a healthy weight and scheduling regular veterinary check-ups all support a long, comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Labrador Retrievers good family dogs?**
Yes. Their friendly, patient and tolerant nature makes them one of the most popular family breeds, though they need daily exercise and training to thrive.

**Do Labradors shed a lot?**
Yes. They shed year-round and more heavily during seasonal coat changes, so regular brushing helps keep loose hair under control.

## Conclusion

The Labrador Retriever combines an affectionate temperament with intelligence and versatility, earning its place as a beloved companion. Provide plenty of exercise, careful portion control and consistent training, and a Lab will reward you with years of loyal, good-natured friendship.
