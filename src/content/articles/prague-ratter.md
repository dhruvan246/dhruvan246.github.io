The Prague Ratter, known in its homeland as the Pražský Krysařík, is one of the smallest dog breeds in the world. Originating in the Czech lands, this diminutive companion was historically valued for catching rats and is now cherished as a devoted, alert, and surprisingly hardy family dog.

## Appearance & temperament

The Prague Ratter is a tiny, square-built dog that typically stands around 8-9 inches (20-23 cm) at the shoulder and weighs only about 4-6 pounds (1.5-2.6 kg). It has a fine-boned yet elegant frame, a pear-shaped head, large erect ears, and lively dark eyes. The most common coloring is black and tan, though brown, blue, and other shades occur. The smooth coat lies close to the body and gives the breed a sleek, refined look.

Despite its size, the Prague Ratter is bold, curious, and affectionate. These dogs bond closely with their families and tend to be playful and alert, often acting as quick, vocal little watchdogs. They are intelligent and people-oriented, but their small stature means they can be wary of strangers and easily overwhelmed by rough handling.

## Diet & nutrition

Because of its tiny body, the Prague Ratter needs a high-quality diet formulated for small or toy breeds, with appropriately sized kibble. Portions are small, and meals are usually best divided into two or three feedings a day to help maintain steady energy and prevent low blood sugar, which toy breeds can be prone to. Measure food carefully, as even slight overfeeding can lead to weight gain on such a small frame. Always provide fresh water, and use treats sparingly during training.

## Exercise & training

Though small, the Prague Ratter is energetic and enjoys daily activity. A couple of short walks plus indoor play usually satisfy its needs, and the breed adapts well to apartment living. These dogs also enjoy games and gentle agility-style activities that engage their quick minds.

The Prague Ratter is intelligent and eager to please, which makes training rewarding when done with positive, reward-based methods. Early socialization helps curb excessive wariness and barking. Gentle, consistent handling builds confidence, while harsh corrections can make this sensitive breed timid.

## Grooming & care

Grooming is straightforward thanks to the short, smooth coat. A weekly brush with a soft brush or grooming mitt removes loose hair and keeps the coat glossy. Bathe only occasionally, when needed. Because the breed is so small and lean, it feels the cold easily, so a sweater or coat is helpful in chilly weather. Routine care should include regular nail trims, ear checks, and dental brushing, as toy breeds are especially prone to dental crowding and tartar buildup.

## Health & lifespan

The Prague Ratter is generally a healthy, long-lived breed, often reaching 12-14 years or more. As with many toy dogs, owners should watch for dental disease, patellar luxation (slipping kneecaps), and the fragility that comes with a delicate skeleton, so jumps from heights should be discouraged. Responsible breeding and routine veterinary checkups help keep these dogs thriving. Regular dental care and weight management are among the most important steps for a long, comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Prague Ratter the same as a Chihuahua?**
No. Although both are tiny toy breeds, the Prague Ratter is a distinct breed from the Czech lands with its own standard, typically more leggy and fine-boned in appearance.

**Are Prague Ratters good apartment dogs?**
Yes. Their small size and moderate exercise needs make them well suited to apartments, provided they get daily walks, play, and companionship.

## Conclusion

The Prague Ratter is a tiny but spirited companion that packs bold personality and devotion into a very small package. With gentle handling, sensible nutrition, dental care, and protection from cold and rough play, this Czech breed makes an affectionate, low-maintenance friend that can thrive happily for many years.
