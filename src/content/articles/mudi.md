The Mudi (pronounced "moody") is a versatile Hungarian herding breed prized by shepherds for its agility, courage, and quick mind. Still relatively rare outside its homeland, the Mudi is a compact, athletic working dog that thrives when given a job to do and a close bond with its people.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Size | Medium (18–29 lbs / 8–13 kg) |
| Lifespan | 12–14 years |

| Energy | High |
| Good with kids | Yes, with early socialization |

## Appearance & temperament

The Mudi is a medium-sized, wedge-shaped dog standing roughly 15–19 inches at the shoulder. Its hallmark feature is the distinctive coat: smooth and short on the head and front of the legs, but dense, wavy, or curly across the body. Coats come in several colors, including black, brown, gray, white, fawn, and a marbled "merle" pattern. The ears are erect and alert, giving the breed a bright, attentive expression.

Temperamentally, the Mudi is energetic, intelligent, and devoted. Bred to think independently while working livestock, it is quick to learn and eager to please its family. Mudis tend to be reserved with strangers and make alert, vocal watchdogs. With proper socialization they are affectionate companions that bond closely with their household.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed your Mudi a complete, balanced diet formulated for an active medium breed. Because this is a high-energy working dog, portion size should match its actual activity level to maintain a lean, athletic build. Divide daily food into two meals to support steady energy.

Choose a food with quality animal protein as the main ingredient. Always provide fresh water, and adjust amounts as your dog ages or its workload changes. Consult your veterinarian for guidance tailored to your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

The Mudi has a strong work drive and needs substantial daily exercise—well beyond a casual walk. Brisk runs, hiking, fetch, and herding or agility activities help channel its energy. Mentally, the breed shines at dog sports such as agility, obedience, flyball, and herding trials.

Training is generally a pleasure, as Mudis are sharp and responsive to positive, reward-based methods. Early socialization is important to soften their natural wariness of strangers and to curb excessive barking. A bored or under-exercised Mudi may become restless or noisy, so consistency and mental stimulation are key.

## Grooming & care

One of the Mudi's practical advantages is its low-maintenance coat. Weekly brushing is usually enough to keep the wavy fur healthy and remove loose hair, with more frequent brushing during seasonal shedding periods. The coat is naturally somewhat weather-resistant and self-cleaning.

Bathe only as needed. Routine care also includes regular nail trims, ear checks, and dental hygiene through tooth brushing to support long-term health.

## Health & lifespan

The Mudi is generally a hardy, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of around 12 to 14 years. As with many breeds, responsible breeders screen for conditions such as hip dysplasia and certain eye disorders. Maintaining a healthy weight, providing regular exercise, and scheduling routine veterinary checkups all help your Mudi live a long, active life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Mudis good apartment dogs?**
They can adapt to apartment life only if given vigorous daily exercise and mental work. Without enough activity, a Mudi's energy and tendency to bark can become difficult to manage in close quarters.

**Do Mudis shed a lot?**
Mudis are moderate shedders. Weekly brushing keeps loose hair under control, with heavier shedding during seasonal coat changes a couple of times a year.

## Conclusion

The Mudi is an intelligent, athletic, and loyal companion best suited to active owners who can meet its considerable exercise and mental needs. Reward its work ethic with training, sport, and companionship, and this rare Hungarian herder will be a devoted and capable partner for years to come.
