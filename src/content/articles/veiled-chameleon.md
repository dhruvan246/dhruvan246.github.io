The veiled chameleon (*Chamaeleo calyptratus*) is one of the most widely kept pet chameleons, recognized by the tall helmet-like casque on its head. Native to Yemen and Saudi Arabia, it is hardier than many chameleon species but still demands a carefully controlled environment and a hands-off approach.

## Overview

| Trait | Details |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 14–24 inches (males larger, females smaller) |
| Lifespan | 5–8 years (females often shorter) |
| Enclosure size | At least 24 x 24 x 48 inches, taller is better |
| Temperament | Solitary, territorial, easily stressed |
| Care level | Intermediate to advanced |

## Appearance & temperament

Veiled chameleons display green bodies banded with yellow, blue, and brown, and can shift coloration with mood, temperature, and breeding state. Males are larger, carry a more pronounced casque, and have a small spur on each hind heel. These are not social animals. They must be housed individually, as cohabitation leads to stress, fighting, and injury. Dark colors, gaping, and hissing signal that a chameleon feels threatened, so they are best enjoyed as a display animal rather than a hands-on pet.

## Habitat & enclosure

Because chameleons are arboreal, vertical space matters more than floor area. A well-ventilated screen enclosure is strongly preferred over glass, which traps stale air. Fill the space with sturdy branches, vines, and live or artificial foliage to create climbing routes and hiding spots. Live plants such as pothos or ficus help hold humidity and provide cover. Avoid loose particulate substrate, which poses an impaction risk if swallowed during feeding.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Provide a thermal gradient with a basking spot of about 85–95°F at the top and cooler areas around 72–80°F lower down, dropping a few degrees at night. A linear UVB tube (such as a 5.0 or 6.0) is essential for vitamin D3 synthesis and calcium absorption, preventing metabolic bone disease; replace the bulb every 6–12 months even if it still lights up. Keep ambient humidity around 50–60%. Chameleons do not recognize standing water, so mist the enclosure several times daily or use a dripper to provide droplets they can drink.

## Diet & feeding

Veiled chameleons are primarily insectivores, eating crickets, dubia roaches, locusts, hornworms, and silkworms. Uniquely among many chameleons, they also nibble plant matter, so offer occasional leafy greens. Gut-load feeder insects with nutritious produce before offering them, and dust insects with calcium powder; use a vitamin and D3 supplement on a more limited schedule as guided by your exotics vet. Juveniles eat daily, while adults typically feed every other day to avoid obesity.

## Handling & health

Handling should be minimal because it stresses these animals. Let the chameleon walk onto your hand rather than grabbing it. Watch for common health concerns such as metabolic bone disease, dehydration, mouth rot, and egg binding in females, which can lay infertile eggs even without a male. Sunken eyes, lethargy, and refusal to eat warrant a visit to a reptile-experienced veterinarian.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do veiled chameleons need a UVB light?** Yes. Without proper UVB, they cannot process calcium and develop metabolic bone disease, a serious and often fatal condition.

**Can two veiled chameleons live together?** No. They are solitary and territorial, and housing them together causes chronic stress and fighting. Each animal needs its own enclosure.

## Conclusion

The veiled chameleon rewards careful keepers with striking color and fascinating behavior, but it is a specialist that needs vertical space, UVB, controlled humidity, and minimal handling. Note that exotic pet legality varies by region, so check local rules before acquiring one. With the right setup and a good exotics vet, a healthy veiled chameleon makes a captivating display reptile.
