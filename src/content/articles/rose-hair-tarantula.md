The Rose Hair Tarantula (*Grammostola rosea*), native to the dry scrublands of Chile, is one of the most popular pet tarantulas in the world. Hardy, slow-moving and undemanding, it is widely recommended as a first tarantula for keepers willing to respect its needs and its venom.

## Appearance & behaviour

This is a stocky, terrestrial tarantula with a tan to grey-brown body and a covering of fine hairs that can take on a pinkish or rose-coloured sheen, giving the species its name. Some individuals show a brighter red-pink form. Females are larger and far longer-lived than males.

Rose Hairs are famously calm and inactive, often sitting in one spot for days or even weeks. They can have unpredictable moods, occasionally fasting for months or moving restlessly. Like other New World tarantulas, they defend themselves by kicking urticating (irritating) hairs from the abdomen rather than biting readily.

## Housing & environment

A single adult does well in a 5-10 gallon terrarium with more floor space than height, since a fall can rupture the abdomen. Provide 2-4 inches of dry substrate such as coconut fibre or peat, plus a cork bark hide and a shallow water dish.

Keep temperatures around 70-80°F (21-27°C); normal room temperature is usually fine. This is a dry-climate species, so humidity should stay low to moderate (around 50-60%) with good ventilation. A secure, escape-proof lid is essential, as tarantulas can squeeze through small gaps.

## Diet & feeding

Rose Hair Tarantulas are insectivores. Feed appropriately sized live prey such as crickets, roaches or mealworms, generally two to four items every week or two for adults, and slightly more often for growing spiderlings.

Offer prey no larger than the spider's body. Remove uneaten insects within a day, as they can stress or even injure a moulting tarantula. Do not be alarmed by long fasts, which are normal for this species, especially before a moult. Always keep fresh water available.

## Handling & care

Handling is best kept to a minimum. Although docile, a fall from even a short height can be fatal, and the kicked hairs cause itching, while a bite delivers venom comparable to a bee sting for most people, though reactions vary. Wash hands and avoid touching your eyes or face after contact.

Routine care is simple: spot-clean waste, refill the water dish and watch for moulting, when the spider flips onto its back. Never disturb a moulting tarantula, and wait several days afterward before offering food so the new fangs can harden.

## Health

Healthy Rose Hairs are robust. The most common problems are husbandry-related: dehydration from a dry, waterless setup, injuries from falls, and difficult moults often linked to incorrect conditions. A wrinkled, shrunken abdomen suggests dehydration.

Mites and fungal issues can appear in overly damp, dirty enclosures, so keep the habitat clean and well-ventilated. Exotic invertebrate-savvy vets are uncommon, making prevention your best tool.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Rose Hair Tarantulas dangerous?** They are not considered medically dangerous to healthy adults; their bite is mild and they rarely bite. The main hazards are irritating hairs and allergic reactions, so handle cautiously and seek medical advice if you react badly.

**Is owning one legal?** In many places yes, but exotic-pet and invertebrate laws vary widely by country, state and city. Check local regulations before acquiring one.

## Conclusion

With its long lifespan, calm nature and simple dry-habitat requirements, the Rose Hair Tarantula rewards patient, hands-off keepers. Provide a secure terrarium, steady warmth, fresh water and occasional feedings, and this gentle desert spider can be a fascinating companion for many years.
