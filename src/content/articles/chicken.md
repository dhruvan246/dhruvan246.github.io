## Overview

The domestic chicken (*Gallus gallus domesticus*) is one of the most popular and rewarding farm pets, prized for fresh eggs, pest control, and friendly personalities. With hundreds of breeds available, chickens suit everyone from rural homesteaders to suburban hobbyists, though local zoning laws should always be checked first.

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|

## Appearance & temperament

Chickens come in a remarkable range of sizes, colors, and feather patterns. Adults have a fleshy comb and wattles, with roosters typically displaying larger combs, longer tail feathers, and brighter plumage than hens. Breeds vary from compact bantams to broad, heavy birds like Orpingtons.

Chickens are highly social flock animals and should never be kept alone; a group of at least three is ideal. They establish a clear "pecking order" and communicate through a wide range of clucks and calls. Most breeds are calm and tolerant of gentle handling, especially when raised from chicks.

## Housing & space

A secure, well-ventilated coop is essential. Provide at least 0.3–0.4 m² of indoor floor space per standard hen, plus an outdoor run offering around 1 m² or more per bird. Crowding leads to stress, feather-pecking, and disease.

Each coop needs roosting bars set above the floor, since chickens instinctively perch at night, and one nest box for every three to four hens. Use dry bedding such as pine shavings or straw, and clean regularly. Predator-proofing is critical: bury hardware cloth around the base, lock the coop at dusk, and cover the run to deter hawks, foxes, and raccoons.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a commercially balanced poultry feed appropriate to the birds' age: chick starter, grower, then layer feed (around 16% protein) for laying hens. Layer feed supplies the extra calcium needed for strong eggshells; offer crushed oyster shell separately as a supplement.

Provide constant access to clean, fresh water. Chickens also need insoluble grit to grind food in their gizzard, especially if they eat anything beyond commercial pellets. Kitchen scraps and treats like leafy greens, mealworms, and scratch grains are fine in moderation but should stay under 10% of the diet. Avoid avocado, chocolate, raw dried beans, and anything moldy or salty.

## Care & handling

Chickens are low-maintenance but need daily attention. Refill food and water, collect eggs, and do a quick health check each morning. Hens enjoy dust-bathing, so a dry patch of soil or sand helps them keep parasites at bay naturally.

To handle a chicken, approach calmly and support the body with both hands, gently securing the wings to prevent flapping. Regular, gentle interaction builds trust. Trim overgrown nails or beak tips only if necessary, and watch for signs of bullying within the flock.

## Health & lifespan

With good care, chickens live 5–8 years, and some hens reach a decade or more, though egg production declines with age. Common issues include external parasites (mites and lice), intestinal worms, respiratory infections, and egg-binding in laying hens. Routine coop cleaning and parasite checks prevent most problems.

Watch for warning signs such as lethargy, ruffled feathers, pale comb, reduced appetite, or abnormal droppings. Quarantine new or sick birds and consult an avian or poultry-experienced veterinarian when illness appears. Vaccination programs vary by region and flock size.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do you need a rooster for hens to lay eggs?**
No. Hens lay eggs without a rooster; a rooster is only required if you want fertilized eggs for hatching chicks.

**How many eggs does a hen lay?**
A productive laying breed can produce around 4–6 eggs per week during peak years, with output declining as the hen ages or during winter and molting.

## Conclusion

Chickens are friendly, productive, and surprisingly easy farm pets that reward owners with fresh eggs and entertaining personalities. With a predator-proof coop, a balanced diet, companions, and routine health checks, a small flock can thrive for many years. Always confirm local regulations before bringing chickens home.
