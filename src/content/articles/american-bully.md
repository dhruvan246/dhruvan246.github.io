The American Bully is a muscular, blocky companion breed developed in the United States in the 1990s, largely from the American Pit Bull Terrier and American Staffordshire Terrier with influence from other bully-type dogs. Despite its powerful, intimidating appearance, the breed was bred specifically for a gentle, people-focused temperament, and it has become a popular family companion.

## Appearance & temperament

The American Bully is instantly recognizable by its broad chest, heavy bone, wide-set stance and large, blocky head with strong cheek muscles. The breed comes in several recognized varieties — Pocket, Standard, Classic and XL — which differ mainly in height and overall mass rather than build. Coats are short, stiff and come in nearly every color and pattern.

Temperament is the defining feature of a well-bred American Bully. The breed is known for being confident, stable and deeply affectionate toward its family, including children. Good-natured and eager to please, these dogs thrive on human companionship and often dislike being left alone for long stretches.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet formulated for the dog's life stage and size. Because of their muscular build, American Bullies need adequate quality protein, but they also gain weight easily, so portions should be measured and treats limited. Divide daily food into two meals and keep fresh water available. Puppies of larger XL lines benefit from controlled, steady growth, so avoid overfeeding. Consult your veterinarian to set an ideal weight and feeding amount for your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

American Bullies have moderate exercise needs — a couple of brisk daily walks plus some play or structured activity usually keeps them fit and content. They are not endurance athletes, and short-muzzled individuals can overheat, so avoid strenuous exercise in hot, humid weather.

The breed is intelligent and highly food-motivated, which makes positive-reinforcement training effective. Early socialization with people, dogs and new environments is important. Because of their strength, teaching reliable leash manners and basic obedience from puppyhood is essential.

## Grooming & care

Grooming is minimal. The short coat needs only weekly brushing with a rubber curry or bristle brush to remove loose hair and distribute skin oils. Bathe occasionally as needed. Pay attention to any skin folds, keeping them clean and dry to prevent irritation. Trim nails regularly, brush teeth, and check the ears for wax or odor.

## Health & lifespan

A typical American Bully lives around 8 to 13 years. As a relatively new breed with bully-type ancestry, it can be prone to hip and elbow issues, skin allergies, and heart conditions. Dogs with shorter muzzles may have some breathing limitations, and certain heavily built lines can face joint strain. Buying from breeders who health-test their stock, maintaining a healthy weight and keeping up with routine veterinary care all support a longer, healthier life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the American Bully an aggressive breed?**
No. The American Bully was specifically bred for a friendly, stable temperament toward people. Well-bred, properly socialized dogs are typically affectionate companions, though responsible ownership and training remain important.

**How big does an American Bully get?**
It depends on the variety. Pocket types are the smallest, while XL types are the largest and heaviest. Most fall into a medium size range with a stocky, muscular build.

## Conclusion

The American Bully pairs an imposing, muscular look with a famously gentle, affection-loving personality. With moderate exercise, simple grooming, a measured diet and consistent early training, it makes a devoted family companion for owners prepared to meet its social and physical needs.
