Corydoras catfish, often called "corys," are small, armored bottom-dwelling fish from South America. Beloved for their peaceful nature, comical scavenging, and active group behaviour, they are among the most popular community aquarium fish for beginners and experts alike.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 1–3 inches (2.5–7.5 cm), depending on species |
| Lifespan | 5–10 years with good care |
| Minimum tank size | 20 gallons (75 litres) |
| Water type | Freshwater |
| Care level | Easy |

## Appearance & behaviour

Corydoras have stout, rounded bodies covered in two rows of bony plates (scutes) rather than scales, giving them an armored appearance. They display three pairs of barbels around the mouth used to sense food in the substrate. Coloration varies widely by species, from the bronze and albino corys to spotted, striped, and metallic green patterns.

These are highly social, shoaling fish that should always be kept in groups of six or more. In numbers they are confident and active, constantly foraging across the bottom. A charming quirk is their habit of darting to the surface to gulp air, which they absorb through their intestine, an adaptation to oxygen-poor waters.

## Tank setup & water parameters

Provide a soft sand or smooth, rounded gravel substrate. Corys are constantly digging with their delicate barbels, and sharp gravel can wear these down and cause infection. Include plenty of plants, driftwood, and caves for shade and security, as corys appreciate dim, sheltered areas.

Aim for these typical parameters, though preferences vary by species:

- Temperature: 72–78°F (22–26°C)
- pH: 6.5–7.5
- Hardness: soft to moderately hard
- Filtration: gentle to moderate flow with stable, well-oxygenated water

Always cycle the tank before adding fish and perform regular partial water changes of around 25% weekly to keep nitrates low.

## Diet & feeding

Corydoras are omnivorous scavengers but should not be expected to survive on leftovers alone. Feed a varied diet of sinking pellets or wafers specifically formulated for bottom-feeders. Supplement with frozen or live foods such as bloodworms, daphnia, and brine shrimp, plus the occasional blanched vegetable.

Feed small amounts once or twice daily, only what they can consume in a few minutes, to avoid uneaten food fouling the water.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Corys are exceptionally peaceful and make ideal community members. They pair well with other calm species such as tetras, rasboras, guppies, mollies, and dwarf gouramis. Avoid large, aggressive, or territorial fish that may bully or outcompete them.

Because they occupy the bottom of the tank, they rarely conflict with mid- and top-dwelling species. Keep them with their own kind in good numbers for the best behaviour.

## Health & common issues

The most common problems stem from poor water quality and unsuitable substrate. Sharp gravel frequently causes eroded or infected barbels, reducing their ability to find food. High nitrate levels and ammonia spikes can stress corys and lead to fungal or bacterial infections.

Corys can also fall victim to "red blotch disease" and fin rot when conditions decline. Maintaining clean water, a gentle substrate, and a stress-free environment prevents most illnesses. Quarantine new fish before adding them to an established tank.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q: Can I keep just one or two Corydoras catfish?**
No. Corys are shoaling fish that become stressed and withdrawn when kept alone. Always keep a group of at least six of the same species for healthy, natural behaviour.

**Q: Why do my Corydoras swim to the surface and gulp air?**
This is normal. Corys can breathe atmospheric air through their intestine. Occasional gulping is healthy, but very frequent trips may signal poor oxygen levels or water quality worth checking.

## Conclusion

Corydoras catfish are hardy, endearing, and peaceful, making them a rewarding choice for almost any freshwater community aquarium. Give them a group of companions, a soft substrate, clean water, and a varied sinking diet, and these busy little scavengers will thrive for years.
