Instantly recognisable by its deep facial wrinkles, "hippopotamus" muzzle and blue-black tongue, the Chinese Shar Pei is an ancient breed once used for hunting, herding and guarding. Calm, dignified and devoted to its family, it makes a loyal and watchful companion for experienced owners.

## Appearance & temperament

The Shar Pei is a compact, square-built medium dog standing around 46–51 cm at the shoulder. Its most famous features are the loose, wrinkled skin (most pronounced in puppies), a broad, padded muzzle, small sunken eyes, tiny triangular ears and a high-set, curled tail. The distinctive bluish-black tongue is shared with only a few breeds. The harsh, prickly coat is short and comes in many solid colours, including fawn, cream, red, black and blue.

In temperament, Shar Pei are calm, confident and intensely loyal. They are devoted and affectionate with their own family but naturally aloof and reserved with strangers, which makes them alert watchdogs. Early, consistent socialisation is essential to ensure this independent breed grows into a well-mannered, even-tempered adult.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, good-quality dog food suited to the Shar Pei's age, size and activity level, divided into measured meals rather than free-feeding. The breed can be prone to weight gain and some individuals have food sensitivities, so monitor body condition and watch for skin or digestive reactions. Provide fresh water at all times and make any diet changes gradually. Ask your veterinarian if you suspect a food allergy or need tailored advice.

## Exercise & training

Shar Pei have moderate energy needs and are generally content with a couple of daily walks plus some play. They are not built for extreme endurance, and their flat-faced muzzle means they can overheat easily, so avoid strenuous exercise in hot, humid weather.

Training calls for patience and a calm, consistent hand. Intelligent but independent and strong-willed, Shar Pei respond best to short, positive, reward-based sessions begun early. Thorough socialisation with people and other animals from puppyhood helps temper their natural wariness and guarding instincts.

## Grooming & care

The short coat is low-maintenance and only needs a weekly brush to remove loose hair, plus occasional baths. The real care priority is the skin folds: wrinkles around the face and body should be kept clean and thoroughly dry to prevent moisture, irritation and infection. Routine nail trimming, dental care and regular ear cleaning round out their grooming needs.

## Health & lifespan

Shar Pei typically live 8 to 12 years. The breed is predisposed to several conditions, including a hereditary inflammatory disorder known as Familial Shar Pei Fever, skin and fold infections, eye problems such as entropion (in-rolling eyelids), and ear issues linked to their narrow ear canals. Choosing a responsible breeder who health-tests, along with routine veterinary check-ups, vaccinations, parasite control and diligent skin care, gives a Shar Pei the best chance at a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Why does the Shar Pei have a blue-black tongue?**
It is a natural breed trait. Shar Pei are born with pink tongues that darken to bluish-black as puppies; a spotted or lavender tongue can also occur and is considered normal.

**Are Shar Pei good for first-time owners?**
Not usually. Their independent, strong-willed nature and specific skin-care needs make them better suited to experienced owners committed to early socialisation and consistent training.

## Conclusion

The Shar Pei is a calm, loyal and strikingly unusual companion for an owner prepared to meet its grooming and training needs. Provide attentive skin care, sensible exercise, patient socialisation and good veterinary support, and this dignified ancient breed will reward you with steadfast devotion.
