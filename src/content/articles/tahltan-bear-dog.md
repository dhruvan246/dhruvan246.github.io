The Tahltan Bear Dog was a small, hardy spitz-type breed developed by the Tahltan people of northwestern British Columbia and the Yukon. Bred to hunt big game, it is widely considered extinct, with the last known dogs recorded in the 1970s and 1980s. This guide describes the breed as it was historically documented.

## Appearance & temperament

The Tahltan Bear Dog was a compact, foxy-faced spitz standing roughly 12 to 18 inches at the shoulder and weighing about 10 to 18 pounds. Its most distinctive feature was a short, bushy tail carried erect, often likened to a shaving brush or whisk. The coat was short, dense, and weatherproof, commonly black or black-and-white, with large erect ears and a pointed muzzle.

Despite its small size, the breed was renowned for courage and stamina. Accounts describe it as alert, energetic, and devoted to its people, yet bold enough to confront bears and lynx in deep snow. With its own family it was affectionate and lively, while remaining wary and quick to bark at strangers.

## Diet & nutrition

Historically these dogs were fed a sparse, protein-rich diet of meat, fish, and game scraps suited to a harsh subarctic environment, and they were famous for thriving on very little. A modern small breed of similar build would do best on a complete, balanced dog food formulated for small, active dogs, portioned to maintain a lean body condition. Fresh water should always be available, and treats kept modest to avoid weight gain in such a small frame.

## Exercise & training

This was a working hunting dog with high energy and remarkable endurance for its size. It was suited to long days afield, tracking and bringing game to bay over rugged terrain. A comparable dog today would need vigorous daily exercise, brisk walks, off-leash running in safe areas, and stimulating activities such as scent games.

The breed was intelligent and independent, traits typical of primitive northern dogs. Training should rely on patience, consistency, and positive reinforcement, as these dogs respond poorly to harsh handling. Early socialization helps temper their natural wariness toward strangers and other animals.

## Grooming & care

The short double coat was low-maintenance and largely self-cleaning. Weekly brushing would remove loose hair and distribute skin oils, with heavier shedding expected during seasonal coat changes. Bathing was rarely needed. Routine care includes regular nail trimming, dental brushing, and checking the ears for debris or moisture, especially in active outdoor dogs.

## Health & lifespan

As an extinct breed, no formal health data exists, but the Tahltan Bear Dog was known historically for hardiness and resilience in extreme cold. Small spitz-type dogs of similar build generally lived around 10 to 14 years. Owners of comparable breeds should watch for common small-dog concerns such as dental disease, luxating patellas, and weight management, and maintain regular veterinary checkups, vaccinations, and parasite prevention.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Tahltan Bear Dog extinct?** Yes. The breed is widely regarded as extinct, with the last documented individuals recorded in the 1970s and 1980s. Declining isolation of the Tahltan people, disease, and crossbreeding contributed to its disappearance.

**Why was it called a "bear dog"?** The Tahltan people used these small, fearless dogs to track and harry large game, including bears, by darting in and barking to hold the animal at bay until hunters arrived.

## Conclusion

The Tahltan Bear Dog stands as a remarkable example of an Indigenous working breed perfectly adapted to its environment and purpose. Though no longer with us, its legacy as a small but exceptionally brave hunting companion endures in the history of North American dogs.
