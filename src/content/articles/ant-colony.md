## Overview

Keeping an ant colony, or formicarium hobby, lets you observe a complex social insect society up close. A single fertile queen and her growing workforce can become a fascinating, low-cost living display.

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Size | Workers 2-15 mm; queens up to 20-25 mm depending on species |
| Lifespan | Workers months to a few years; queens can live 5-20+ years |
| Housing | Test tube setup for founding, then a formicarium with outworld |
| Temperament | Non-handleable; observe-only, peaceful in their enclosure |
| Care level | Beginner to intermediate |

## Appearance & behaviour

Ants are eusocial insects with distinct castes: a single (sometimes multiple) egg-laying queen, sterile female workers, and seasonal winged males and new queens. Workers handle foraging, brood care, and nest maintenance, while the queen focuses solely on reproduction. Common beginner species include *Lasius niger* (black garden ant), *Camponotus* (carpenter ants), and *Messor* (harvester ants). Colonies communicate through pheromone trails and antennal contact, and you will see brood organised into eggs, larvae, and pupae. Watching workers cooperate, defend, and tend young is the main appeal of the hobby.

## Housing & environment

New colonies are typically founded in a simple test tube with a water reservoir plugged by cotton, which provides the humidity the queen needs. Once a dozen or more workers appear, the colony is moved to a formicarium, a nest of plaster, ytong, acrylic, or 3D-printed material with chambers and tunnels, connected to an "outworld" where ants forage and dispose of waste.

Maintain a humidity gradient so ants can choose damp and dry areas, and keep most species at roughly 22-26 C. Cover the nest with a red filter or dark cloth, as ants prefer darkness. Prevent escapes with a barrier such as fluon (PTFE) or talc around the outworld rim. Avoid direct sunlight and sudden temperature swings.

## Diet & feeding

Ants need two food groups: carbohydrates for energy and protein for raising brood. Offer sugar sources like diluted honey, sugar water, or specialist ant nectar, plus protein from small feeder insects such as fruit flies, crickets, or mealworms. Harvester ants (*Messor*) also eat seeds. Feed small amounts a few times per week and remove uneaten food promptly to prevent mould and mites. Always provide a constant water source through a hydrated test tube or water tower.

## Handling & care

Ant colonies are observe-only pets and should never be handled directly, as this stresses the colony and risks bites or stings. Routine care is minimal: refill water reservoirs, replace fouled test tubes, clean the outworld of debris, and watch for mould. Many founding queens enter a slow "claustral" phase and need to be left undisturbed in the dark until the first workers emerge. Some species hibernate (diapause) over winter at cooler temperatures, which is normal and healthy.

## Health

A healthy colony shows steady brood and active foraging. Common problems include mites, mould from excess food, dehydration from a dry nest, and starvation if protein is lacking. Keep enclosures clean and humidity correct to avoid most issues. A queen who stops laying or workers dying off rapidly may signal stress, poor conditions, or an unmated queen. Never use household insecticides near the formicarium.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do I need to buy a queen to start a colony?** Yes. A colony cannot grow without a fertile, mated queen. Hobbyists either catch queens during a nuptial flight or buy founded colonies from reputable suppliers.

**Is keeping ants legal everywhere?** No. Laws vary by region, and many countries restrict keeping or importing non-native species to protect ecosystems. Always keep local species and check local regulations first.

## Conclusion

A pet ant colony is a rewarding, educational window into insect society that needs little space and modest cost. With the right humidity, a balanced diet of sugars and protein, and patience during founding, you can watch a single queen build a thriving community over years. Keep only legal, local species and enjoy observing nature at work.
