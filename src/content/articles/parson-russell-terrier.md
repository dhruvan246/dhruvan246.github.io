The Parson Russell Terrier is a small, square-built working terrier originally bred in 19th-century England to run alongside foxhounds and bolt foxes from their dens. Named after the Reverend John "Jack" Russell, this breed combines plucky courage with an affectionate, lively nature, making it a spirited companion for active households.

## Appearance & temperament

The Parson Russell Terrier stands taller and squarer than its close relative the Jack (Russell) Terrier, with longer legs suited for running with the hunt. It typically reaches 12 to 15 inches at the shoulder and weighs 13 to 17 pounds. The predominantly white coat may be smooth or broken (rough), with tan, black, or tricolor markings, especially around the head. The dense, harsh outer coat protects against weather and brush.

Bold, clever, and tireless, this terrier is friendly and outgoing with its family. It is alert, vocal, and naturally inclined to dig and chase, reflecting its hunting heritage. Without enough stimulation, it can become bored and mischievous, so it thrives best with owners who appreciate a busy, independent-minded dog.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality, complete dog food formulated for small, active breeds. Because Parson Russell Terriers burn plenty of energy, they need nutrient-dense meals, but they can gain weight if overfed or under-exercised. Divide the daily amount into two measured meals rather than free-feeding. Use treats sparingly, particularly during training, and keep fresh water available at all times. Adjust portions based on age, activity level, and body condition, and consult your veterinarian about the best diet for your individual dog.

## Exercise & training

This is a genuinely high-energy breed that needs substantial daily exercise, ideally an hour or more of walks, running, and active play. Mental challenges such as scent games, puzzle toys, and trick training help channel its sharp intelligence. Always supervise off-lead time and use secure fencing, as the strong prey drive can send a Parson Russell after small animals in an instant.

Training works best with positive, reward-based methods and consistency. These terriers are smart but independent, so short, engaging sessions and early socialization pay off. Teaching a reliable recall is especially important.

## Grooming & care

Grooming needs are modest. Brush the coat weekly to remove loose hair; broken-coated dogs benefit from occasional hand-stripping to maintain texture. Bathe only as needed. Check and clean the ears regularly, trim nails every few weeks, and brush the teeth several times a week to support dental health. Routine grooming sessions are also a good chance to inspect the skin and coat.

## Health & lifespan

The Parson Russell Terrier is generally hardy, with a typical lifespan of 13 to 15 years. Like many breeds, it can be prone to certain inherited conditions, including patellar luxation, lens luxation and other eye disorders, and deafness in some lines. Choosing a breeder who screens for hereditary issues, along with regular veterinary checkups, vaccinations, parasite prevention, and dental care, helps keep this active terrier healthy throughout its life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Parson Russell Terrier the same as a Jack Russell Terrier?**
They share ancestry, but they are recognized as distinct breeds. The Parson Russell is taller and more square in build, while the Jack Russell is generally smaller and shorter-legged.

**Are Parson Russell Terriers good apartment dogs?**
They can adapt to smaller spaces if given enough daily exercise and mental stimulation, but their high energy and tendency to bark make a home with an active owner and secure yard a better fit.

## Conclusion

The Parson Russell Terrier is a bright, brave, and energetic little dog that rewards owners willing to meet its considerable exercise and engagement needs. With consistent training, plenty of activity, and routine care, this charismatic terrier makes a loyal and entertaining companion for years to come.
