The Border Terrier is a small, hardy working dog originally bred along the border country between England and Scotland to hunt foxes alongside hunt horses. Tough yet affectionate, it remains one of the most even-tempered of the terriers and a popular family companion.

## Overview

| Trait | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Size | Small (11.5–15.5 lb / 5–7 kg) |
| Lifespan | 12–15 years |
| Coat | Wiry, weather-resistant double coat |
| Energy | High |
| Good with kids | Yes, generally very good |

## Appearance & temperament

Borders are instantly recognized by their distinctive "otter-shaped" head, dark expressive eyes, and small drop ears. They stand roughly 10–11 inches at the shoulder, with a narrow, flexible body built to follow quarry to ground. Coat colors include grizzle and tan, blue and tan, red, and wheaten.

Temperamentally, the breed is plucky and game when working but notably mild-mannered and people-oriented at home. Borders tend to be friendlier with other dogs and strangers than many terriers, though their strong prey drive means small pets like rodents may not be safe companions. They are intelligent, eager to please, and adaptable.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food formulated for small or active breeds. Most adult Border Terriers do well on roughly a half to one cup of quality dry food per day, split into two meals, though exact amounts vary with age, weight, and activity. This breed can be prone to weight gain, so measure portions and limit treats. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian before major diet changes or for puppy and senior feeding plans.

## Exercise & training

Despite their small size, Borders are energetic and need substantial daily activity—typically an hour or more of walks, play, and off-lead running in safe areas. They excel at dog sports such as agility, earthdog trials, and obedience, which channel their working drive. A bored Border may dig or bark, so mental stimulation matters as much as physical exercise.

Training should be consistent, reward-based, and started early. Borders respond well to positive methods and food motivation. Because of their hunting instinct, reliable recall is difficult around small animals, so secure fencing and leashed walks in unfenced areas are wise. Note that Borders are agile jumpers and climbers.

## Grooming & care

The wiry double coat is low-shedding but benefits from weekly brushing. Traditionally the coat is hand-stripped (plucking dead hairs) two or three times a year to maintain its proper texture and weather resistance; clipping is an alternative but softens the coat over time. Bathe only as needed to preserve natural oils. Routine care includes regular nail trims, ear checks, and dental brushing.

## Health & lifespan

Border Terriers are generally robust and long-lived, often reaching 12–15 years. Like all breeds they can be predisposed to certain conditions, including hip dysplasia, patellar luxation, heart issues, and a rare breed-associated neurological condition sometimes called canine epileptoid cramping syndrome. Responsible breeders screen breeding stock. Regular veterinary checkups, parasite prevention, dental care, and weight management support a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Border Terriers good for first-time owners?**
Yes, they are often a good choice thanks to their friendly, trainable nature, though their high energy and prey drive require commitment to exercise and secure containment.

**Do Border Terriers shed a lot?**
No, they shed relatively little. Their wiry coat sheds minimally, especially when hand-stripped, making them lower-maintenance than many double-coated breeds for shedding.

## Conclusion

The Border Terrier blends terrier grit with an affectionate, adaptable personality, making it a versatile companion for active individuals and families. With daily exercise, consistent training, occasional coat stripping, and routine veterinary care, this rugged little dog can thrive as a loyal household member for many years.
