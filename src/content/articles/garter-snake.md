The garter snake (genus *Thamnophis*) is one of North America's most familiar and widespread snakes, and it has become a popular beginner reptile. These small, active, day-loving snakes are non-venomous, hardy, and far more inquisitive than many other pet serpents, which makes watching them genuinely entertaining.

## Overview

| Trait | Typical Range |
|-------|---------------|
| Adult size | 18–30 inches (some females larger) |
| Lifespan | 6–10 years in captivity |
| Enclosure size | 20-gallon long minimum for one adult |
| Temperament | Active, curious, generally docile |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & temperament

Garter snakes are slender, with a typical pattern of three light stripes running down a darker body, though colors vary widely between species and locales. They are diurnal and constantly on the move, exploring their enclosure throughout the day. Most tolerate handling well, but when startled they may musk (release a smelly fluid) or release a mild, harmless saliva. With regular gentle contact they usually calm down and become reliably tame.

## Habitat & enclosure

A single adult does well in a 20-gallon long tank with a secure, escape-proof lid, as garters are skilled at squeezing through small gaps. Provide a substrate such as aspen, cypress mulch, or coconut fiber that allows some burrowing. Include at least two hides (one on each side of the temperature gradient), climbing branches, and a sturdy water bowl large enough for the snake to soak. Because they are semi-aquatic in the wild, easy access to clean water is important.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Set up a thermal gradient with a warm basking area around 85–90°F (29–32°C) and a cooler end near 70–75°F (21–24°C). Use an under-tank heater or overhead bulb controlled by a thermostat, and always confirm temperatures with a reliable thermometer. As diurnal snakes, garters benefit from a low-level UVB bulb and a regular day-night light cycle. Keep humidity moderate, roughly 40–60%, with a light misting if the air is very dry, especially during shedding.

## Diet & feeding

In the wild, garter snakes eat fish, amphibians, worms, and small prey. In captivity, earthworms (nightcrawlers) are an excellent staple, along with appropriately sized feeder fish. Avoid feeding goldfish and rosy reds as a regular diet, since they contain thiaminase that can cause a vitamin B1 deficiency over time. Many keepers also offer thawed pinky mice. Dust meals occasionally with a reptile calcium and vitamin supplement. Feed juveniles every few days and adults roughly once a week, adjusting to body condition.

## Handling & health

Support the snake's body and avoid grabbing it from above, which can trigger a defensive musk response. Keep handling sessions short and skip them for a day or two after feeding. Watch for common issues such as retained shed, mites, mouth rot, and respiratory infections signaled by wheezing or excess mucus. Maintaining correct temperatures and clean water prevents most problems. A reptile-experienced veterinarian should evaluate any persistent symptoms.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are garter snakes dangerous to keep?** No. They are non-venomous and produce only a very mild saliva that is harmless to people, causing at most slight irritation in rare sensitive cases. Their main defense is musking.

**Do I need a permit to own a garter snake?** It depends on where you live. Some regions protect native wildlife or restrict collection, so always buy captive-bred animals and check your local and state regulations before acquiring one.

## Conclusion

Garter snakes reward beginners with an active, engaging pet that is easy to house and feed. Give them a secure enclosure, a proper thermal gradient, clean water, and a varied worm-and-fish diet, and these resilient little snakes will thrive for many years.
