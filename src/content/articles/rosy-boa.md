The Rosy Boa (*Lichanura trivirgata*) is a small, slow-moving snake native to the deserts and rocky scrublands of the southwestern United States and northwestern Mexico. Its docile nature, modest size, and simple care needs make it one of the most beginner-friendly boas in the hobby.

## Overview

| Trait | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 2–3.5 feet (60–110 cm) |
| Lifespan | 18–25+ years in captivity |
| Enclosure size | 20-gallon long for an adult |
| Temperament | Calm, docile, rarely defensive |
| Care level | Easy — good for beginners |

## Appearance & temperament

Rosy Boas are stout, heavy-bodied for their length, and come in several regional color forms. Most display three lengthwise stripes against a cream, gray, or tan background, with stripe colors ranging from rosy-orange and brown to slate. They are among the calmest pet snakes, seldom biting and tending to move slowly and deliberately. When startled they typically curl into a ball rather than strike, which makes them easy to handle.

## Habitat & enclosure

A single adult does well in a 20-gallon long terrarium or a similarly sized enclosure with a secure, tight-fitting lid — these boas are strong and persistent escape artists. Provide an aspen, sand-soil mix, or paper-based substrate a couple of inches deep so they can burrow. Include at least two hides (one on the warm side, one on the cool side) and some sturdy rocks or cork bark to climb over. Avoid loose, dusty substrates that can be accidentally ingested during feeding.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Create a thermal gradient with a warm basking area around 88–92°F (31–33°C) and a cooler side near 75–80°F (24–27°C); a nighttime drop into the upper 60s°F is fine and natural. Under-tank heaters or low-wattage overhead heat sources controlled by a thermostat work well, and a thermometer at each end lets you monitor the gradient. As a desert species, the Rosy Boa needs low humidity — roughly 30–50% — so a small water bowl is usually enough. While they don't strictly require UVB, low-level UVB lighting can support overall health. During winter, many keepers provide a cooler brumation period to mimic natural cycles, especially before breeding.

## Diet & feeding

Rosy Boas are carnivores that eat appropriately sized rodents. Choose prey roughly the width of the snake's thickest part: pinky or fuzzy mice for juveniles, graduating to adult mice for grown boas. Hatchlings and young snakes eat about every 5–7 days, while adults do well on one meal every 10–14 days. Thawed frozen prey is safest and convenient. These boas are prone to obesity, so avoid overfeeding and watch body condition. Always keep clean, fresh water available, though they drink relatively little.

## Handling & health

Wait a day or two after a meal before handling to prevent regurgitation, then handle gently and support the body. With regular calm interaction, most Rosy Boas tame readily. Common health issues include retained shed (often from incorrect humidity), mites, respiratory infections from overly damp or cold conditions, and obesity from overfeeding. Provide a slightly more humid hide during shedding to help the skin come off in one piece. Any wheezing, prolonged appetite loss, or unusual lumps warrants a visit to a reptile veterinarian.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Rosy Boas good for beginners?** Yes. Their small size, calm temperament, simple desert setup, and infrequent feeding make them one of the easiest snakes for first-time keepers.

**Is it legal to keep a Rosy Boa?** Captive-bred Rosy Boas are widely kept, but reptile laws vary by region and some areas restrict native or wild-collected specimens. Always check local and state regulations and buy captive-bred animals.

## Conclusion

With a secure enclosure, a proper warm-to-cool gradient, low humidity, and sensibly spaced rodent meals, a Rosy Boa is a hardy, long-lived, and remarkably gentle companion. Its easygoing nature and decades-long lifespan reward attentive, consistent care.
