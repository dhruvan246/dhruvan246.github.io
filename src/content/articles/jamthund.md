The Jamthund, also known as the Swedish Elkhound, is a large, ancient spitz breed from the Jämtland region of northern Sweden. Bred to hunt moose and other big game in harsh forest terrain, it is a hardy, courageous, and independent working dog with deep loyalty to its family.

## Appearance & temperament

The Jamthund is a substantial, well-balanced spitz with a wolf-like appearance. Males typically stand around 23-26 inches at the shoulder and females slightly smaller, with weights generally ranging from about 60 to 75 pounds. The breed has erect, pointed ears, a tightly curled tail carried over the back, and an alert, dignified expression. The coat is usually grey with lighter and darker shadings and distinctive light markings on the muzzle, cheeks, and underside.

Temperamentally, the Jamthund is calm and steady at home but brave and tenacious when working. It is intelligent and independent, traits that helped it track and hold large game alone. These dogs tend to be affectionate and devoted to their people while remaining reserved or watchful with strangers, making them natural watchdogs.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, high-quality diet formulated for large, active dogs. Because the Jamthund is a working breed with significant energy demands, calorie needs rise with activity level, especially during cold weather or hunting season. Split daily food into two meals to support steady energy and reduce the risk of bloat associated with deep-chested breeds.

Monitor body condition closely; you should be able to feel the ribs without excess fat covering them. Provide constant access to fresh water and adjust portions as the dog ages or activity decreases. Consult your veterinarian about appropriate amounts and any breed-specific nutritional considerations.

## Exercise & training

This is a high-energy breed that needs substantial daily exercise, ideally an hour or more of vigorous activity such as long walks, hiking, running, or off-leash exploration in safe areas. Without enough physical and mental stimulation, the Jamthund can become bored and restless.

Its independence and strong hunting drive make consistent, patient training important from puppyhood. Use positive, reward-based methods and start socialization early. A reliable recall is challenging given the breed's prey drive, so secure fencing and leashed walks in unfenced areas are recommended.

## Grooming & care

The thick double coat is relatively low-maintenance most of the year, needing brushing roughly once or twice a week to remove loose hair and prevent matting. During seasonal shedding periods in spring and autumn, the undercoat blows out heavily and daily brushing helps manage the loose fur.

Bathe only as needed, since the coat has natural weather resistance. Routine care also includes regular nail trimming, dental brushing, and checking the ears for dirt or irritation.

## Health & lifespan

The Jamthund is generally a robust, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of around 12 to 14 years. As with many large dogs, hip and elbow issues can occur, and responsible breeders screen for joint health. Keeping the dog at a healthy weight, providing regular exercise, and scheduling routine veterinary checkups all support long-term wellbeing. Always discuss any health concerns with your veterinarian.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Jamthund a good family dog?**
Yes. Jamthunds are loyal and affectionate with their families and can do well with children when properly socialized, though their size and energy mean young kids should be supervised.

**Does the Jamthund need a lot of exercise?**
Yes. As an active hunting breed, it requires plenty of daily physical activity and mental engagement to stay content and well-behaved.

## Conclusion

The Jamthund is a hardy, intelligent, and devoted spitz suited to active owners who can meet its considerable exercise and engagement needs. With early training, regular grooming during shedding seasons, and a loving home, this Swedish Elkhound makes a loyal companion and capable working partner.
