The Red-Eared Slider (*Trachemys scripta elegans*) is one of the most widely kept pet turtles in the world. Named for the red stripe behind each eye, this hardy aquatic species is rewarding but long-lived and demands a large, well-filtered habitat. Many owners underestimate how big these turtles grow and how much space they truly need.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 6-12 inches (shell), females larger |
| Lifespan | 20-30+ years in captivity |
| Enclosure size | 75-125+ gallon aquatic tank for an adult |
| Temperament | Active, alert, semi-social |
| Care level | Intermediate |

## Appearance & temperament

Red-Eared Sliders have an olive-to-dark-green carapace marked with yellow stripes, and the signature red patch behind each eye. Hatchlings are bright green and roughly the size of a coin, but they grow quickly. Females typically reach a larger size than males, while males develop longer front claws and tails. These turtles are diurnal and curious, spending their days swimming, foraging, and basking. With routine they often learn to recognize the person who feeds them, though they remain reptiles that prefer observation over handling.

## Habitat & enclosure

Sliders are highly aquatic and need a spacious tank with deep, clean water for swimming. A common guideline is about 10 gallons of water per inch of shell length, so a full-grown adult needs a 75-125+ gallon setup. Water should be deep enough for the turtle to fully submerge and right itself. Every tank also requires a dry basking platform or dock where the turtle can climb completely out of the water and dry off. Because sliders are messy eaters and produce heavy waste, a powerful canister or external filter rated well above the tank volume is essential to keep water clean.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Provide a basking spot of about 88-95°F (31-35°C) using a heat lamp positioned over the dock. Keep water temperature around 72-78°F (22-26°C) with a submersible heater, slightly warmer for hatchlings. Crucially, sliders need a UVB light source running 10-12 hours a day; UVB lets them synthesize vitamin D3 and absorb calcium, preventing metabolic bone disease and shell deformities. Replace UVB bulbs every 6-12 months as their output fades even when the bulb still glows. As an aquatic species, ambient humidity is less of a concern than maintaining proper water and basking temperatures.

## Diet & feeding

Red-Eared Sliders are omnivores whose diet shifts with age. Juveniles are more carnivorous and need frequent protein, while adults eat more plant matter. Offer a quality commercial turtle pellet as a base, supplemented with leafy greens such as collard, dandelion, and aquatic plants. Protein sources can include earthworms, insects, and occasional feeder items. Many keepers feed juveniles daily and adults every 2-3 days, offering only what the turtle finishes in a few minutes. A cuttlebone provides extra calcium. Avoid overfeeding, which causes obesity and rapid shell growth.

## Handling & health

Sliders tolerate brief handling but can become stressed, so limit it and support the body securely. Always wash your hands afterward, since turtles can carry *Salmonella* bacteria. Watch for warning signs like a soft or pyramiding shell, swollen eyes, loss of appetite, wheezing, or floating lopsided, which can indicate respiratory infection or nutritional deficiency. Good water quality, correct temperatures, and UVB prevent most common illnesses. A reptile-experienced veterinarian should handle any persistent health problems.

## Frequently asked questions

**How big do Red-Eared Sliders get?** Most adults reach 6-12 inches in shell length, with females generally larger than males. They grow steadily for several years, so plan for a large tank from the start.

**Are Red-Eared Sliders legal to own?** Legality varies by region. Many places restrict the sale of hatchlings under 4 inches, and some areas treat sliders as an invasive species, so check local laws before acquiring one.

## Conclusion

Red-Eared Sliders are engaging, long-lived companions that thrive with a big filtered tank, a warm basking dock, and steady UVB. With proper water quality, a balanced omnivorous diet, and routine care, a slider can stay healthy for decades, making it a true long-term commitment rather than a casual pet.
