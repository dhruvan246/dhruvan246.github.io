The Neon Tetra (*Paracheirodon innesi*) is one of the most popular freshwater aquarium fish in the world, beloved for its glowing blue-and-red stripe and calm, schooling nature. Native to the soft, slow-moving blackwater streams of the upper Amazon basin, it is hardy enough for beginners yet rewards careful keepers with brilliant colour.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| Adult size | About 3–4 cm (1.2–1.5 in) |
| Lifespan | Roughly 5–8 years with good care |
| Minimum tank size | 40 litres (about 10 gallons) |
| Water type | Freshwater, soft and slightly acidic |
| Care level | Beginner to easy |

## Appearance & behaviour

Neon Tetras have a slim, torpedo-shaped body marked by an iridescent blue-green stripe running from nose to tail, with a vivid red band along the rear half. The colours can dim at night or under stress and brighten again when the fish is healthy and relaxed. They are peaceful, active swimmers that feel safe only in groups. Kept alone or in pairs they become nervous and faded, so a shoal of at least six (ideally more) is essential for natural behaviour and confidence.

## Tank setup & water parameters

Provide a planted aquarium with subdued lighting, gentle filtration, and plenty of open mid-water swimming space. Dark substrate and floating plants help replicate their shaded forest streams and make their colours pop. Aim for a temperature of about 22–26°C (72–78°F), a pH of roughly 6.0–7.0, and soft to moderately soft water. Neon Tetras are sensitive to poor water quality, so cycle the tank fully before adding them and perform regular partial water changes to keep ammonia and nitrite at zero and nitrate low.

## Diet & feeding

Neon Tetras are omnivores with small mouths and modest appetites. A good staple is high-quality micro pellets or crushed flake food, supplemented with frozen or live treats such as daphnia, brine shrimp, and bloodworms. Variety supports their colour and health. Feed small amounts once or twice a day, only what they finish within a couple of minutes, and remove any uneaten food. Overfeeding quickly fouls the water and is a common beginner mistake.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Their peaceful temperament suits a community of similarly small, calm species. Good companions include other small tetras, rasboras, corydoras catfish, otocinclus, dwarf gouramis, and freshwater shrimp. Avoid large, aggressive, or fin-nipping fish, as well as anything big enough to view a Neon Tetra as food, such as angelfish or larger cichlids. Always keep them in a sizeable shoal so they stay bold rather than hiding.

## Health & common issues

With clean, stable water Neon Tetras are generally robust. The best-known threat is "neon tetra disease," a parasitic condition that causes fading colour, lumps, and difficulty swimming; it has no reliable cure, so prevention through good husbandry and quarantining new fish is key. They are also prone to stress from sudden parameter swings and to fin or fungal infections in dirty tanks. If a fish looks unwell, isolate it and consult an aquatics specialist or aquatic veterinarian rather than guessing at treatments.

## Frequently asked questions

**How many Neon Tetras should I keep together?** Keep a minimum of six, and preferably ten or more. Larger groups feel secure, school tightly, and display their brightest, most natural colours.

**Are Neon Tetras good for beginners?** Yes, once the tank is fully cycled and stable. They are inexpensive and hardy, but they need clean, soft, slightly acidic water and should never be added to a brand-new, uncycled aquarium.

## Conclusion

Neon Tetras are a stunning, low-maintenance choice for a peaceful community aquarium. Give them a properly cycled tank, soft warm water, a varied diet, and a generous shoal of their own kind, and they will reward you for years with shimmering colour and lively, graceful schooling.
