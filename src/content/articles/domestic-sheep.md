Domestic sheep (*Ovis aries*) are gentle, social grazing animals kept worldwide for wool, meat, milk, and increasingly as friendly smallholding companions. They are hardy and low-cost to feed, but they are not solitary pets: a sheep needs the company of its own kind and enough land to graze.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| Size | 45–160 kg, depending heavily on breed |
| Lifespan | 10–12 years (some reach 15+) |
| Space needs | Roughly 2–5 sheep per acre of good pasture |
| Temperament | Calm, gregarious, timid; bond strongly to a flock |
| Care level | Moderate; daily checks and seasonal husbandry |

## Appearance & temperament

Sheep vary enormously across the 1,000-plus breeds. Most have a thick fleece, cloven hooves, and a narrow muzzle suited to close grazing. Some breeds are naturally polled (hornless) while others, often rams, grow curved horns. Hair breeds such as the Katahdin shed naturally and do not need shearing.

Temperamentally, sheep are prey animals: easily startled, they flee toward the flock rather than fight. They are intelligent, recognise human and sheep faces, and can become tame with gentle, consistent handling. A lone sheep becomes stressed, so always keep at least two.

## Housing & space

Sheep are pasture animals first. Provide securely fenced grazing, ideally rotated between paddocks to control parasites and let grass recover. Fencing must be sheep-proof and, in many regions, predator-resistant against dogs, coyotes, or foxes.

They tolerate cold well thanks to their fleece but need a three-sided shelter or barn for shade, wind, and driving rain. Ensure good drainage, as standing in mud promotes foot problems. Always supply clean, fresh water and a mineral supplement.

## Diet & nutrition

Grass and forage form the bulk of a healthy sheep's diet. On good pasture, adult sheep often need little else. When grazing is poor or in winter, supplement with good-quality hay. Grain should be limited and introduced slowly, as sudden rich feeding can cause dangerous digestive upsets like acidosis or bloat.

Provide a sheep-specific mineral lick. Critically, never feed sheep mineral mixes or licks formulated for other livestock, because added copper that suits cattle or goats can be toxic to sheep. Fresh water must always be available.

## Care & handling

Wool breeds need shearing at least once a year, usually in spring, to prevent overheating, matting, and flystrike. Hooves should be checked and trimmed regularly to avoid lameness. Routine parasite management, both internal worms and external pests, is essential and best planned with a vet to avoid resistance.

Handle sheep calmly and from the side; sudden movements panic them. Moving them works best by using their flocking instinct rather than chasing individuals.

## Health & lifespan

With good care, sheep live around 10–12 years. Common concerns include internal parasites, footrot, flystrike, bloat, and pregnancy complications in ewes. Vaccination schedules, such as for clostridial diseases, vary by region and should follow local veterinary advice.

Watch daily for signs of illness: separation from the flock, limping, off-feed behaviour, or laboured breathing. Because sheep hide weakness, early observation matters. Establish a relationship with a livestock vet before problems arise.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I keep just one sheep as a pet?** No. Sheep are flock animals and a single sheep suffers chronic stress and loneliness. Always keep a minimum of two, and ideally three or more.

**Do all sheep need shearing?** No. Traditional wool breeds must be shorn yearly, but hair or self-shedding breeds like Katahdin and Dorper drop their coats naturally and need little to no shearing.

## Conclusion

Domestic sheep are rewarding, even-tempered animals for anyone with suitable land and a willingness to learn basic husbandry. Give them secure pasture, flock company, the right minerals, and seasonal shearing and hoof care, and they thrive as productive, charming additions to a smallholding.
