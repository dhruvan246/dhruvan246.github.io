The capybara is the world's largest living rodent, a semi-aquatic herbivore native to South America. Famously gentle and social, it has become a sought-after exotic pet, but its size, water needs, and need for companionship make it one of the most demanding animals on this list.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Size | 39-51 in (100-130 cm) long; 75-150 lb (35-66 kg) |
| Lifespan | 8-10 years in captivity (up to 12 with excellent care) |
| Housing | Large secure outdoor enclosure with pool access |
| Temperament | Calm, social, gentle, highly companion-dependent |
| Care level | Advanced (not for beginners) |

## Appearance & temperament

Capybaras have a barrel-shaped body, short legs, a blunt muzzle, and coarse reddish-brown to grey fur. Their feet are partially webbed for swimming, and their eyes, ears, and nostrils sit high on the head so they can stay mostly submerged. Adults typically weigh 75-150 pounds.

They are remarkably docile and rarely aggressive, but they are intensely social herd animals. A lone capybara often becomes stressed, depressed, or ill, so keeping at least two together is widely considered essential to their welfare.

## Housing & environment

Capybaras need a large, secure outdoor space, ideally a fenced yard of several hundred square feet or more, with sturdy fencing they cannot dig under. Because they are semi-aquatic, access to water for swimming and soaking is not optional: a pool or pond they can fully submerge in helps regulate body temperature and supports skin and digestive health.

They are sensitive to cold and cannot tolerate freezing conditions, so a heated, insulated shelter is required in cooler climates. Provide shade and clean water at all times, since they overheat easily and have limited ability to sweat.

## Diet & nutrition

Capybaras are grazing herbivores. The bulk of the diet should be grass and grass hay, which also helps wear down their continuously growing teeth. Supplement with leafy greens and limited vegetables, plus a vitamin C source, as capybaras (like guinea pigs) cannot synthesize their own vitamin C and can develop scurvy without it.

Like other rodents, they practice coprophagy, re-ingesting certain droppings to extract additional nutrients, which is normal and important for digestion. Avoid sugary fruits, bread, and processed foods.

## Handling & enrichment

Hand-raised, well-socialized capybaras can be calm and affectionate, enjoying gentle scratches and human company. Still, they are large, powerful animals with sharp incisors, so respectful handling is important and they are not suited to small children.

Enrichment comes largely from companionship, water access, and grazing. Provide pool time, things to forage and chew, and the steady company of other capybaras or compatible animals to keep them mentally and physically healthy.

## Health, legality & ethics

Common health concerns include dental overgrowth, skin and foot problems from inadequate water access, vitamin C deficiency, and stress-related illness from isolation. A veterinarian experienced in exotics or large rodents is essential, and these can be difficult to find.

Legality varies widely by region: many states, countries, and municipalities restrict or ban private ownership, or require permits. Always confirm local laws first. Ethically, consider whether you can meet their space, water, social, and lifespan needs before acquiring one.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can you keep just one capybara?** It is strongly discouraged. Capybaras are herd animals and typically need at least one companion to avoid serious stress and health decline.

**Do capybaras really need a pool?** Yes. As semi-aquatic animals they rely on water to regulate temperature, care for their skin, and stay healthy, so swim-deep water access is a core requirement.

## Conclusion

Capybaras are gentle, fascinating animals, but they are a major commitment requiring large secure space, year-round water access, companionship, a specialized diet, and exotic veterinary care. For owners who can truly meet those needs, and where ownership is legal, they can be rewarding companions.
