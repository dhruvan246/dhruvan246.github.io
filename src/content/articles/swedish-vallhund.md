The Swedish Vallhund is a small, long-bodied herding dog with deep roots in Scandinavia, where it worked Viking farms driving cattle and guarding the homestead. Often nicknamed the "Viking dog," this spirited spitz combines a powerful, low-slung frame with a bold, friendly personality.

## Appearance & temperament

The Swedish Vallhund has a distinctive long, low silhouette reminiscent of a corgi, though the two breeds are separate. It stands around 11.5 to 13.5 inches at the shoulder and typically weighs 20 to 35 pounds, with males usually larger than females. The breed comes in shades of grey through red and sable, marked by a characteristic "harness" pattern of lighter hair over the shoulders. Tails vary naturally from full-length to bobtail.

Temperamentally, the Vallhund is alert, confident, and affectionate. It bonds closely with its family, stays eager to please, and tends to be watchful and vocal, making it a capable little watchdog. Bright and sociable, it usually gets along well with children and other pets when properly socialized.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality complete dog food formulated for the breed's life stage and activity level. Because the Vallhund has a sturdy build and a hearty appetite, it can gain weight easily, and excess weight strains its long back. Measure portions, limit treats to a small share of daily calories, and keep an eye on body condition. Divide food into two meals a day and always provide fresh water. Consult your veterinarian for tailored guidance.

## Exercise & training

Despite its short legs, the Swedish Vallhund is athletic and energetic, needing a good amount of daily activity. Aim for at least an hour of exercise through brisk walks, play, and off-leash romps in safe areas. This is a working breed that thrives on a job, excelling at agility, herding, obedience, and tricks.

Mentally sharp and quick to learn, the Vallhund responds best to positive, reward-based training. Early socialization and consistent, engaging sessions channel its energy and curb the herding instinct to nip at heels. Without enough stimulation, it may become bored and overly vocal.

## Grooming & care

The double coat is relatively low-maintenance. Weekly brushing keeps it healthy and removes loose hair, increasing to more frequent sessions during the twice-yearly shedding seasons when the undercoat blows out heavily. Bathe only as needed. Routine care includes regular nail trims, dental brushing, and checking the ears for debris or signs of irritation.

## Health & lifespan

The Swedish Vallhund is generally a hardy, long-lived breed, typically reaching 12 to 15 years. Like other long-backed dogs, it can be prone to back and joint issues, and the breed is associated with a form of inherited retinopathy affecting the eyes. Hip evaluation and eye screening from reputable breeders help reduce risk. Keeping the dog at a healthy weight and providing regular veterinary checkups supports a long, active life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Swedish Vallhunds related to corgis?**
They share a similar long, low body shape and may have crossed paths historically, but they are distinct breeds with separate origins. The Vallhund is a Scandinavian spitz, while corgis come from Wales.

**Do Swedish Vallhunds bark a lot?**
They can be quite vocal, as alertness and "talking" are part of their herding heritage. Consistent training and enough mental and physical stimulation help keep barking under control.

## Conclusion

The Swedish Vallhund is a hardy, intelligent, and affectionate companion that rewards active owners with loyalty and plenty of personality. With regular exercise, mental challenges, and basic coat care, this ancient Viking herder makes a lively and devoted family dog.
