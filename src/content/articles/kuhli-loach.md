The Kuhli Loach (*Pangio kuhlii*) is a slender, eel-like freshwater fish from the streams and peat-laden waters of Southeast Asia. Peaceful, nocturnal and endlessly entertaining once settled, it is a popular bottom-dweller for community aquariums, though its shy nature means it is often heard about more than it is seen.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 3-4 inches (7.5-10 cm) |
| Lifespan | 7-10 years |
| Minimum tank size | 20 gallons (75 litres) |
| Water type | Warm, soft, slightly acidic freshwater |
| Care level | Easy to moderate |

## Appearance & behaviour

Kuhli Loaches have a long, snake-like body marked with alternating dark brown or black bands over a pinkish-yellow base, giving them a banded, worm-like look. Four pairs of barbels around the mouth help them sense food in the substrate. They lack prominent scales on the head and have a small spine beneath each eye.

These fish are primarily nocturnal and burrowing, often disappearing into substrate or decor during the day and becoming active at dusk. They are highly social and far bolder when kept in groups, where they will pile together in caves or wriggle around the tank in the evening.

## Tank setup & water parameters

A soft, sandy substrate is essential, as Kuhli Loaches love to burrow and sharp gravel can injure their delicate bodies and barbels. Provide plenty of hiding spots: caves, driftwood, dense planting and leaf litter all help them feel secure.

Aim for these parameters:

- Temperature: 75-86 F (24-30 C)
- pH: 5.5-7.0
- Hardness: soft, around 3-8 dGH
- A gentle filter and secure lid (they can squeeze through small gaps)

Because they often hide in filter intakes, a sponge guard over the intake is wise. Keep water clean with regular partial water changes, as loaches are sensitive to poor water quality.

## Diet & feeding

Kuhli Loaches are omnivorous scavengers that forage along the bottom. Offer sinking pellets or wafers as a staple, supplemented with frozen or live foods such as bloodworms, brine shrimp, daphnia and tubifex. They will also nibble at biofilm and leftover food.

Feed in the evening or after lights-out when they are most active, so faster mid-water fish do not eat everything first. Feed small amounts they can finish to avoid fouling the water.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Always keep Kuhli Loaches in groups of at least five or six; single fish become stressed and reclusive. They are gentle and non-aggressive, making them excellent community fish.

Good companions include other peaceful species such as tetras, rasboras, gouramis, Corydoras and small peaceful barbs. Avoid large or aggressive fish that may bully or eat them, and avoid very small shrimp fry, which may be eaten opportunistically.

## Health & common issues

Their thin scales and barbels make Kuhli Loaches more sensitive to medications, especially those containing copper, so dose with care and consider quarantine tanks. They are prone to ich (white spot) and skin infections if water quality drops.

Watch for damaged barbels caused by rough substrate, and signs of stress such as constant hiding even at night or refusal to eat. Stable, clean, well-oxygenated water and a peaceful tank are the best preventives. Quarantine new arrivals to reduce disease risk.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Kuhli Loaches hard to keep?** Not especially. They are hardy once their tank is mature and stable, but they need soft substrate, warm soft water, hiding places and companions of their own kind to thrive.

**Why do my Kuhli Loaches keep hiding?** This is normal behaviour. They are nocturnal and shy, especially in small groups or sparse tanks. Adding more loaches, more cover and dim lighting usually makes them far more visible and active.

## Conclusion

The Kuhli Loach is a charming, peaceful oddball that rewards patient keepers with quirky nocturnal antics. Give it a warm, soft-water tank with sandy substrate, plenty of hiding spots and a group of its own kind, and this gentle eel-like loach will be a long-lived, fascinating member of your community aquarium.
