The Olde English Bulldogge is a muscular, athletic dog developed in the United States during the 1970s by breeder David Leavitt, who aimed to recreate the look of the 18th-century bulldog while producing a healthier, more functional animal than the modern English Bulldog. The result is a sturdy, confident companion with a longer muzzle and fewer breathing problems than its flatter-faced relatives.

## Appearance & temperament

The Olde English Bulldogge is a thickset, powerfully built dog standing roughly 16-20 inches at the shoulder. It carries a broad head, well-defined jaw, and a muzzle that is noticeably longer than the modern Bulldog's, which improves airflow and athleticism. The short coat comes in colors such as brindle, fawn, red, black, and various combinations with white.

Temperamentally, the breed is known for being calm, friendly, and devoted to its family. These dogs are generally confident without being aggressive, and they tend to be good-natured with children. They retain protective instincts and can be reserved with strangers, making early socialization important. Despite their imposing build, most are easygoing house companions.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality diet formulated for large, muscular breeds, with adequate protein to support lean muscle mass. Because the Olde English Bulldogge can gain weight easily and carry a heavy frame, portion control is essential to avoid putting strain on the joints. Most adults do well on two measured meals per day rather than free-feeding.

Always provide fresh water, especially after exercise, since the breed can overheat. Treats should be limited and factored into daily calories. Consult your veterinarian about appropriate amounts based on age, activity level, and body condition.

## Exercise & training

This is a moderate-energy breed that benefits from daily activity such as brisk walks and play sessions, but it is not built for extreme endurance or distance running. Aim for around 30-60 minutes of exercise per day, split into shorter sessions. Avoid strenuous activity in hot or humid weather, as the breed tolerates heat poorly.

Training tends to go smoothly with consistent, positive, reward-based methods. The Olde English Bulldogge is intelligent and eager to please but can show a stubborn streak, so patience and early obedience work pay off. Socialization from puppyhood helps produce a well-mannered adult.

## Grooming & care

The short coat is low-maintenance and needs only weekly brushing to manage shedding and keep it healthy. Bathe occasionally as needed. Pay attention to any facial folds, keeping them clean and dry to prevent irritation, though this breed generally has fewer wrinkles than the English Bulldog.

Routine care includes regular nail trimming, ear cleaning, and dental hygiene. Check the ears for moisture or odor, and brush the teeth several times a week to support long-term oral health.

## Health & lifespan

Selectively bred for improved health, the Olde English Bulldogge typically lives 9-14 years and generally has better respiratory function and mobility than flatter-faced bulldogs. Even so, owners should watch for hip and elbow dysplasia, joint issues, allergies, and some risk of heat sensitivity. Maintaining a healthy weight greatly reduces strain on the joints.

Choosing a responsible breeder who screens for hip, eye, and cardiac conditions improves the odds of a sound, long-lived dog. Regular veterinary checkups help catch issues early.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Olde English Bulldogge the same as an English Bulldog?**
No. It is a separate, more athletic breed created in the 1970s to resemble the older bulldog type while reducing the health and breathing problems common in the modern English Bulldog.

**Are Olde English Bulldogges good family dogs?**
Yes. They are typically affectionate, calm, and tolerant with children, and they bond closely with their families, though early socialization is recommended.

## Conclusion

The Olde English Bulldogge offers the classic bulldog look paired with greater athleticism and a generally sounder constitution. With moderate exercise, careful weight management, simple grooming, and attention to heat, this loyal and good-natured breed makes a rewarding companion for many households.
