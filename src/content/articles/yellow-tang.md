The Yellow Tang (*Zebrasoma flavescens*) is one of the most recognizable saltwater aquarium fish, prized for its brilliant lemon-yellow body and lively, grazing personality. Native to the reefs of the Pacific, it has long been a centerpiece of reef tanks. While hardy once established, it needs a large, well-oxygenated system and a vegetable-rich diet to truly thrive.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 6-8 inches (15-20 cm) |
| Lifespan | 10-20+ years |

## Appearance & behaviour

The Yellow Tang has an oval, disc-shaped body that is uniformly bright yellow, fading to a paler shade at night. Like all tangs (surgeonfish), it carries a sharp, retractable spine near the base of its tail that can deliver a nasty cut, so handle it with care. These fish are active, constant swimmers that spend the day grazing on surfaces. They can be peaceful but become territorial toward other tangs, especially those of similar shape or color.

## Tank setup & water parameters

Because Yellow Tangs swim continuously and need open horizontal space, a tank of at least 100 gallons with a long footprint is recommended. Provide plenty of live rock for grazing, hiding, and establishing territory. Strong water flow and efficient filtration with a quality protein skimmer help keep oxygen high and waste low.

- Temperature: 72-78 degrees F (22-26 degrees C)
- pH: 8.1-8.4
- Salinity: 1.020-1.025 specific gravity
- Ammonia and nitrite: 0 ppm; nitrate kept low

Always add a Yellow Tang to a fully cycled, mature aquarium, as it is sensitive to unstable conditions.

## Diet & feeding

In the wild, Yellow Tangs are herbivores that graze on algae across the reef. In captivity their diet should be primarily plant-based. Offer dried marine algae or nori sheets clipped in the tank daily, along with spirulina-based flakes or pellets. Occasional meaty foods like brine or mysis shrimp can be given in moderation, but vegetable matter must dominate. Feed small amounts several times a day. A steady supply of greens keeps their color vivid and supports immune health, while a meat-heavy diet can lead to malnutrition.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Yellow Tangs are reef-safe and generally peaceful with non-tang species such as clownfish, gobies, wrasses, and many corals. Aggression is mostly directed at other tangs and surgeonfish. If keeping more than one tang, do so only in very large systems, add them simultaneously, and avoid pairing species with similar body shapes. Adding the Yellow Tang last, after calmer fish are settled, helps reduce territorial disputes.

## Health & common issues

Tangs are especially prone to marine ich (*Cryptocaryon*) and marine velvet, often triggered by stress or poor water quality. A strict quarantine period for all new arrivals is one of the best preventive measures. Head and lateral line erosion (HLLE) is another common problem, frequently linked to poor nutrition and water conditions; a varied, algae-rich diet helps prevent it. Maintain pristine water, stable parameters, and low stress. Consult an aquatic veterinarian or specialist for persistent disease.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q: How big a tank does a Yellow Tang really need?**
A: Plan for at least 100 gallons with a long, open swimming area. These active grazers cover a lot of ground daily, and cramped tanks lead to stress, aggression, and disease.

**Q: Can I keep two Yellow Tangs together?**
A: It is risky. They can be territorial toward their own kind. Only attempt it in a very large tank, introduce both at the same time, and watch closely for bullying.

## Conclusion

The Yellow Tang is a rewarding, long-lived marine fish that brings constant color and motion to a reef aquarium. Success comes down to space, water quality, and a steady vegetable-based diet. Note that collection and trade rules for marine species vary by region, so check local regulations. Give this fish room to swim and graze, and it can flourish for well over a decade.
