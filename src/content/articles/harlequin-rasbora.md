The Harlequin Rasbora (*Trigonostigma heteromorpha*) is one of the most popular community aquarium fish, beloved for its warm copper-orange body and the bold black triangular patch on its rear half. Hardy, peaceful and active, it is an excellent choice for beginners and planted-tank enthusiasts alike.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | Around 1.5–2 in (4–5 cm) |
| Lifespan | Typically 4–6 years with good care |
| Minimum tank size | About 10–15 gallons (38–57 L) |
| Water type | Freshwater |
| Care level | Easy / beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & behaviour

Harlequin Rasboras have a deep, laterally compressed body in shades of pink, copper or orange, with a distinctive black wedge-shaped marking covering the back half of the body. This triangle gives the fish its common name. Males tend to be slightly slimmer and more intensely coloured, while females are rounder.

They are peaceful, social shoaling fish that should always be kept in groups. Maintained in numbers, they form tight, fast-moving schools and display their best colours. Lone or small groups become shy, stressed and faded.

## Tank setup & water parameters

A group of six or more does best in a tank of at least 10–15 gallons. These fish originate from soft, slightly acidic blackwater streams in Southeast Asia, so they appreciate gentle filtration, subdued lighting and plenty of live plants. Dark substrate and floating plants help bring out their colour and make them feel secure.

Aim for these approximate parameters:

- Temperature: 73–82°F (23–28°C)
- pH: slightly acidic to neutral, roughly 6.0–7.5
- Hardness: soft to moderately soft

Provide open swimming space along with planted areas. Always cycle the aquarium before adding fish, and perform regular partial water changes to keep nitrates low.

## Diet & feeding

Harlequin Rasboras are easy-going omnivores. In the wild they eat small insects, larvae and zooplankton. In the aquarium, offer a quality micro-pellet or flake food as a staple, supplemented with frozen or live foods such as daphnia, bloodworms and brine shrimp for variety and condition. Their small mouths need appropriately sized food. Feed small amounts once or twice daily, only what they finish within a couple of minutes, to avoid overfeeding and water-quality problems.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Their calm temperament makes them ideal community fish. Good companions include other small, peaceful species such as tetras, corydoras catfish, otocinclus, dwarf gouramis, kuhli loaches and snails or dwarf shrimp. Avoid large or aggressive fish and known fin-nippers, which may bully or eat them. Keeping a sizeable shoal reduces stress and discourages skittish behaviour.

## Health & common issues

Harlequin Rasboras are generally robust, but like most aquarium fish they are vulnerable to ich (white spot), fin rot and fungal infections, usually triggered by poor water quality, stress or sudden temperature swings. Stable, clean water and a varied diet are the best preventatives. Quarantine new arrivals before adding them to an established tank, and watch for clamped fins, faded colour or loss of appetite as early warning signs.

## Frequently asked questions

**How many Harlequin Rasboras should I keep together?** Keep at least six, and ideally more. As shoaling fish they feel secure and look their best in larger groups, becoming bolder and more colourful.

**Are Harlequin Rasboras good for beginners?** Yes. They are hardy, peaceful and adaptable, tolerating a range of conditions as long as the water is clean and stable, making them an excellent first community fish.

## Conclusion

The Harlequin Rasbora packs striking colour and lively schooling behaviour into a small, low-maintenance package. Given a planted tank, stable soft water, a varied diet and the company of its own kind, it rewards keepers with years of vibrant, active display, an ideal centrepiece for any peaceful community aquarium.
