The wallaby is a small to mid-sized marsupial native to Australia and nearby islands. Several species, most commonly the Bennett's (red-necked) and dama (tammar) wallaby, are sometimes kept by experienced exotic keepers. They are grazing, hopping animals that need wide outdoor space and are best suited to dedicated hobbyists rather than first-time pet owners.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Size | 2-6 ft tall; 13-50 lb depending on species |
| Lifespan | 12-15 years in captivity |

| Temperament | Shy, skittish, prey-driven, can be tamed with patience |

## Appearance & temperament

Wallabies resemble small kangaroos, with powerful hind legs, a long muscular tail used for balance, short forelimbs, and large upright ears. Coat color ranges from grey-brown to reddish depending on species. Females carry a forward-facing pouch where young (joeys) develop.

By nature wallabies are prey animals, so they are easily startled and may bolt or thump when frightened. Hand-raised individuals can become calm and accepting of their keeper, but they generally do not crave handling the way a dog or cat does. Sudden movements, loud noises, and unfamiliar people can cause panic and injury.

## Housing & environment

Wallabies require a spacious, securely fenced outdoor enclosure. A general guideline is at least a quarter-acre for one or two animals, with smooth fencing roughly 5-6 feet high to prevent jumping and injury. Fencing should be solid or visually obvious, since a panicked wallaby can run headlong into wire.

Provide a dry, draft-free shelter or barn for protection from rain, wind, and extreme heat or cold. Soft grassy ground reduces foot and leg injuries. Remove hazards, toxic plants, and anything they might collide with. Wallabies are social, so keeping at least a pair is recommended where space allows.

## Diet & nutrition

In the wild, wallabies are grazers and browsers. Captive diets center on free-choice grass hay and pasture grazing, supplemented with a formulated macropod or herbivore pellet. Small amounts of leafy greens, vegetables, and root vegetables can be offered as variety.

Constant access to clean fresh water is essential. Avoid sudden diet changes, high-sugar fruits, bread, and other processed human foods, which can upset their specialized digestive system. A vet familiar with macropods can help fine-tune the diet to prevent issues like lumpy jaw and metabolic bone disease.

## Handling & enrichment

Bonding starts young, ideally with hand-raised joeys that imprint on their keeper. Move slowly, speak softly, and let the animal approach on its own terms. Never grab a wallaby by the tail or chase it. Adults should be handled minimally and only when necessary.

Enrichment comes mainly from space to hop, graze, and explore. A varied environment with natural ground cover, gentle slopes, and a stable social companion supports their wellbeing far more than toys.

## Health, legality & ethics

Common health concerns include lumpy jaw (a serious oral bacterial infection), metabolic bone disease from poor diet, parasites, and stress-related trauma from collisions. Routine care requires an exotics or wildlife veterinarian, who can be hard to find in many areas.

Legality varies widely by region. Many places require permits, ban private ownership outright, or have specific enclosure standards. Always verify local, state, and national laws before acquiring one. Ethically, consider whether you can realistically meet a wallaby's lifelong space, social, and specialized care needs.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are wallabies good house pets?** No. Wallabies are not house animals. They need large outdoor space and are easily stressed indoors. They suit dedicated keepers with proper land and facilities.

**Do wallabies need a companion?** Yes, where possible. Wallabies are social and generally do better kept in pairs or small groups rather than alone.

## Conclusion

Wallabies are fascinating, gentle marsupials, but they are demanding exotic animals rather than casual pets. With ample secure outdoor space, a correct grass-based diet, minimal stress, and access to a knowledgeable exotics vet, a committed keeper can provide a good life. Always confirm legality and honestly weigh the long-term commitment first.
