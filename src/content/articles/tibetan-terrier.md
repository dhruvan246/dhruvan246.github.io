Despite the name, the Tibetan Terrier is not a terrier at all. This shaggy, medium-sized companion was bred centuries ago by monks in the monasteries and villages of Tibet, where it was kept as a watchdog, herding helper and treasured friend. Sure-footed and adaptable, it remains an affectionate, lively dog that bonds closely with its family.

## Appearance & temperament

The Tibetan Terrier is a squarely built, compact dog of medium size with a distinctive long double coat that falls over the eyes and covers the body. The coat comes in many colours, including white, gold, cream, grey, black and various combinations. A notable breed feature is the large, flat, round "snowshoe" feet, which helped the dog move across snow and rough mountain terrain.

In temperament, Tibetan Terriers are intelligent, loyal and sensitive. They are typically reserved with strangers but devoted to their families, making them alert watchdogs. Playful and good-humoured, they thrive on companionship and may become anxious if left alone for long periods.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food appropriate to your Tibetan Terrier's age, size and activity level. Most adults do well on two measured meals a day. Because the breed has a moderate, easily maintained build, watch portion sizes carefully, as excess weight puts strain on the joints. Use treats in moderation, especially during training, and always provide access to fresh water. If you are unsure about quantities or a specific diet, consult your veterinarian.

## Exercise & training

This is an active, agile breed that enjoys daily walks, play sessions and the chance to explore. Around 30–60 minutes of exercise a day usually keeps a Tibetan Terrier content, along with mental stimulation through games and puzzle toys. Their athleticism makes them well suited to activities such as agility and rally.

Tibetan Terriers are clever but can be independent and a little stubborn, so consistent, reward-based training works best. Early socialisation with people, other dogs and new situations helps temper their natural wariness toward strangers and builds a confident, well-mannered adult.

## Grooming & care

The long double coat needs regular attention. Brush several times a week, ideally every day or two, to prevent mats and tangles, paying special attention to the areas behind the ears and under the legs. Some owners choose to keep the coat trimmed shorter for easier maintenance. Bathe as needed, and routinely check and clean the ears, trim the nails and brush the teeth. Keep the hair around the eyes and feet tidy.

## Health & lifespan

Tibetan Terriers are generally hardy and often live 12–15 years. Like many breeds, they can be prone to certain inherited conditions, including hip dysplasia, eye disorders such as progressive retinal atrophy and lens luxation, and a rare neurological condition. Responsible breeders screen their stock for hip and eye problems. Routine veterinary check-ups, parasite prevention, dental care and weight management all help support a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Tibetan Terrier hypoallergenic?**
No dog is truly hypoallergenic, but the Tibetan Terrier's coat sheds relatively little and some allergy sufferers tolerate it better than heavier-shedding breeds. Spending time with the breed before committing is wise.

**Are Tibetan Terriers good apartment dogs?**
Yes. Their moderate size and adaptable nature make them suitable for apartments, provided they receive daily exercise, mental stimulation and plenty of companionship.

## Conclusion

The Tibetan Terrier is a charming, intelligent companion with a rich heritage and a striking shaggy coat. With regular grooming, daily activity, early socialisation and routine veterinary care, this loyal dog rewards its owners with years of affectionate, lively company.
