Nicknamed the "Grey Ghost" for its sleek silver-grey coat and pale eyes, the Weimaraner is an athletic German hunting dog bred to work all day in the field. Devoted, intelligent and tireless, it suits an active owner who can keep both its body and mind busy.

## Overview

| Trait | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Size | Large (25–40 kg) |
| Lifespan | 10–13 years |
| Coat | Short, smooth, silver-grey |
| Energy | Very high |
| Good with kids | Yes, with supervision and exercise |

## Appearance & temperament

Weimaraners are elegant and muscular, standing roughly 57–70 cm at the shoulder. Their hallmark is the short grey coat — ranging from silver to mouse-grey — paired with amber or blue-grey eyes. A less common long-haired variety also exists. Temperamentally they are bold, alert and highly affectionate, forming intense bonds with their families. This devotion has a flip side: they dislike being left alone and are well known for separation anxiety.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, good-quality diet appropriate to the dog's age and high activity level, following the portion guidance on the food and your vet's advice. Because Weimaraners are a deep-chested breed at risk of bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus), it is sensible to split the daily ration into two or more smaller meals and avoid vigorous exercise immediately before and after eating. Keep fresh water available and monitor body condition rather than overfeeding.

## Exercise & training

- Need **extensive daily exercise** — long runs, hikes, fetch, swimming or dog sports — plus mental challenges to prevent boredom and destructive behaviour.
- Highly intelligent and trainable but strong-willed; early socialisation and consistent, reward-based training work best.
- A strong prey drive means recall and secure fencing deserve attention.

## Grooming & care

- Grooming is simple: the short coat needs only occasional brushing and the breed sheds modestly.
- Bathe only as needed, and check the ears, nails and teeth regularly.
- With low body fat and a thin coat, Weimaraners feel the cold and appreciate a coat in chilly weather.

## Health & lifespan

Weimaraners typically live around 10–13 years. The most important breed-linked concern is bloat (GDV), a sudden, life-threatening emergency; owners should learn its warning signs. Other conditions to be aware of include hip dysplasia and certain hereditary issues. Buying from breeders who health-test their dogs, alongside routine veterinary care, helps support a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Weimaraners good for first-time owners?**
They are a demanding choice. Their high energy, intelligence and need for companionship suit experienced, active owners more than beginners.

**Do Weimaraners like being left alone?**
No — they are prone to separation anxiety and thrive on company and activity rather than long stretches alone.

## Conclusion

The Weimaraner is a striking, athletic and intensely loyal companion for an active household. Give it ample exercise, steady training and plenty of togetherness, and the Grey Ghost will reward you with energetic, devoted friendship for years.
