The Green Iguana (*Iguana iguana*) is a large, arboreal lizard native to the tropical forests of Central and South America. Often sold cheaply as a tiny hatchling, it grows into a demanding reptile that needs a great deal of space, specialized equipment, and a long-term commitment. It is one of the most commonly surrendered exotic pets precisely because owners underestimate its adult size and needs.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 4-6 feet total length (including tail) |
| Lifespan | 12-20 years with good care |
| Enclosure size | Large custom enclosure, minimum roughly 6 x 6 x 6 feet for an adult |
| Temperament | Can be tamed but often territorial, especially mature males |
| Care level | Advanced |

## Appearance & temperament

Green Iguanas are bright green as juveniles, often developing grayer or duller tones with age. They have a prominent dorsal crest of soft spines, a large round scale (the subtympanic shield) beneath the ear, and a dewlap under the chin used for display and temperature regulation. Their long tail can be used as a defensive whip.

Temperament varies widely between individuals. Patient, consistent handling from a young age helps, but breeding-season hormones can make adult males aggressive even toward familiar keepers. They are not naturally cuddly animals, and trust must be earned slowly.

## Habitat & enclosure

As tree-dwelling lizards, iguanas need vertical space more than floor area. Hatchlings can start in a smaller terrarium, but they grow fast and most adults require a custom-built enclosure or a converted closet or room section. Provide sturdy, sloped branches for climbing and basking, along with sight barriers and foliage so the animal feels secure.

Surfaces should be easy to clean, since iguanas produce a lot of waste. Avoid loose substrates that could be swallowed and cause impaction.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Provide a thermal gradient with a basking spot around 95-100 F (35-38 C) and a cooler end near 75-85 F (24-29 C), dropping a few degrees at night. Use guarded ceramic or basking bulbs and always monitor temperatures with reliable thermometers.

UVB lighting is essential, not optional. Without proper UVB, iguanas cannot synthesize vitamin D3 and develop metabolic bone disease. Use a high-output UVB tube replaced on schedule, and maintain humidity around 60-80 percent through misting and water sources.

## Diet & feeding

Green Iguanas are strict herbivores. A correct diet is built around dark leafy greens such as collard, mustard, turnip, and dandelion greens, with smaller amounts of other vegetables and occasional fruit. Avoid animal protein, which can cause long-term kidney damage.

Calcium supplementation, often with a phosphorus-free powder, supports healthy bones, and a UVB-supported diet helps prevent deficiencies. Offer fresh food daily and provide clean water at all times.

## Handling & health

Support the body fully when lifting and never grab or restrain by the tail, which can break off. Move calmly to avoid startling the animal. Watch for common health issues including metabolic bone disease, mouth rot, parasites, and obesity from poor diet.

A reptile-experienced veterinarian should examine new and ailing iguanas. Signs of trouble include soft or swollen jaws, lethargy, refusing food, swollen limbs, or labored breathing.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q: Are Green Iguanas good pets for beginners?** No. Their large adult size, specialized lighting and humidity needs, strict diet, and potential aggression make them best suited to experienced keepers prepared for a multi-year commitment.

**Q: Is it legal to own a Green Iguana?** Legality varies by region. They are restricted or banned in some areas because escaped iguanas can become invasive, so always check local and national laws before acquiring one.

## Conclusion

A Green Iguana can be a rewarding, long-lived companion for a dedicated keeper, but only with a large vertical enclosure, strong UVB, high humidity, and a careful herbivorous diet. Anyone considering one should plan for an animal that may reach six feet and live for nearly two decades.
