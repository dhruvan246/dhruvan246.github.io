The Hovawart is a large, powerful working breed from Germany, traditionally bred to guard farms and estates. Its name comes from old German words meaning "yard watchman." Devoted, intelligent, and naturally protective, the Hovawart bonds closely with its family while remaining reserved toward strangers.

## Appearance & temperament

The Hovawart is a sturdy, slightly longer-than-tall dog standing roughly 23-28 inches at the shoulder. It carries a long, dense double coat that lies close to the body, most often seen in black, blond, or black-and-gold. The breed has a broad head, dark eyes, and a confident, balanced gait.

In temperament, the Hovawart is loyal, even-tempered, and highly devoted to its people. It is naturally watchful and makes an excellent guardian, but it should be neither nervous nor aggressive. The breed is intelligent and independent, which means it thinks for itself and responds best to an owner it respects rather than one who simply gives commands.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet formulated for large breeds, with portions matched to your dog's age, weight, and activity level. Because Hovawarts grow slowly and reach full maturity later than many breeds, puppies benefit from a large-breed puppy food that supports controlled, steady growth rather than rapid weight gain.

Divide daily food into two meals to support digestion. Keep an eye on body condition, since excess weight strains the joints of a large, active dog. Fresh water should always be available, and treats are best kept to a small share of total calories.

## Exercise & training

This is an active, working breed that needs substantial daily exercise. Long walks, hikes, off-leash runs in safe areas, and games keep a Hovawart physically satisfied, while training, scent work, tracking, and obedience tasks give the mental challenge it craves. A bored Hovawart can become stubborn or destructive.

Start training and socialization early. The breed is intelligent but independent, so consistent, positive, reward-based methods work far better than harsh corrections. Early exposure to people, other dogs, and varied environments helps channel the natural guarding instinct into calm, reliable behavior.

## Grooming & care

The Hovawart's long double coat is fairly low-maintenance for its length. Brush once or twice a week to remove loose hair and prevent tangles, increasing frequency during seasonal shedding. The coat is naturally weather-resistant and does not require frequent bathing.

Check ears regularly for dirt or moisture, trim nails as needed, and maintain a routine of dental care. Because the breed is alert and active, daily engagement matters as much as physical grooming.

## Health & lifespan

Hovawarts are generally robust and typically live around 10-14 years. As with many large breeds, they can be prone to hip dysplasia, and some lines may carry other joint or thyroid concerns. Responsible breeders screen breeding stock for hereditary issues.

Routine veterinary checkups, weight management, joint-conscious exercise during growth, and parasite prevention all support a long, healthy life. Always consult your veterinarian for guidance tailored to your individual dog.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Hovawarts good family dogs?**
Yes. With early socialization and consistent training, Hovawarts are devoted, gentle family companions that bond strongly with their household, though supervision around young children is always wise.

**Do Hovawarts make good guard dogs?**
Very much so. Bred as estate guardians, they are naturally watchful and protective without being needlessly aggressive, making them reliable and confident watchdogs.

## Conclusion

The Hovawart is a loyal, intelligent, and protective large breed best suited to an active owner who can provide firm, kind leadership and plenty of physical and mental stimulation. Given proper training, exercise, and companionship, it rewards its family with steadfast devotion and dependable guardianship for many years.
