The Yakutian Laika is an ancient Arctic working breed developed by the Indigenous people of the Sakha (Yakutia) region of northeastern Siberia. Bred to haul sleds, herd reindeer, and hunt in some of the coldest inhabited places on Earth, this medium-sized dog combines remarkable endurance with an affectionate, people-oriented nature.

## Appearance & temperament

The Yakutian Laika is a compact, well-muscled dog standing roughly 21-23 inches at the shoulder. Its dense double coat features a soft insulating undercoat beneath a longer, coarse outer coat, with a pronounced ruff around the neck. Coats come in many colors and patterns, often white with patches, and the eyes may be brown, blue, or one of each.

Temperamentally, these dogs are friendly, energetic, and deeply attached to their families. Centuries of close partnership with humans have made them sociable and eager to cooperate. They generally get along well with children and, when properly socialized, with other dogs. They are alert and will announce visitors but are not typically aggressive.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality diet formulated for active, medium-sized dogs. Because the breed evolved as a hardworking sled dog, working or highly active individuals may need more calories, while pets with lighter activity can gain weight if overfed. Divide daily food into two meals, provide constant access to fresh water, and monitor body condition rather than relying on bag guidelines alone. Adjust portions for age, activity level, and the season, and consult your veterinarian about any specific dietary needs.

## Exercise & training

This is an athletic breed with substantial stamina, so plan on at least an hour of daily activity. Long walks, jogging, hiking, and dog sports such as sledding, skijoring, or canicross suit its heritage well. A bored or under-exercised Yakutian Laika may dig, bark, or become restless.

Training should be positive, consistent, and started early. These dogs are intelligent and willing to please but retain an independent streak from their working past. Reward-based methods and early socialization yield a confident, well-mannered companion. A secure yard is wise, as some individuals enjoy roaming.

## Grooming & care

The double coat sheds steadily and "blows" heavily once or twice a year, typically in spring and fall. Brush at least weekly, and daily during heavy shedding, to remove loose undercoat and prevent matting. Bathe only as needed, since frequent washing strips natural oils. Routine care includes nail trimming, dental brushing, and checking the ears for debris or irritation. The coat provides excellent cold protection, but these dogs need shade and water in hot weather.

## Health & lifespan

The Yakutian Laika is generally a robust, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of about 10-13 years. As a relatively rare breed with a working background, it has not developed many breed-specific problems, though responsible breeders screen for hip and elbow dysplasia and hereditary eye conditions. Maintaining a healthy weight, keeping vaccinations and parasite prevention current, and scheduling regular veterinary checkups all support a long, active life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Yakutian Laikas good family dogs?**
Yes. They are affectionate, sociable, and bond closely with their people, generally doing well with children when raised and socialized properly.

**Do Yakutian Laikas need a lot of exercise?**
They do. As an Arctic working breed, they thrive on at least an hour of daily activity and enjoy hiking, running, and pulling sports.

## Conclusion

The Yakutian Laika is a hardy, affectionate, and energetic companion best suited to active owners who can meet its exercise and grooming needs. With consistent training, plenty of activity, and regular coat care, this resilient Siberian breed makes a loyal and rewarding family dog.
