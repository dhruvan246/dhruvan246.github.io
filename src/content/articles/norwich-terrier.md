The Norwich Terrier is one of the smallest of the working terriers, a compact and hardy dog originally bred in England to bolt foxes and control vermin around farms and stables. Despite its tiny stature, it carries a confident, energetic personality and a surprisingly weatherproof coat. It is closely related to the Norfolk Terrier, the two breeds differing chiefly in their ears: the Norwich has erect, pricked ears, while the Norfolk's fold forward.

## Appearance & temperament

The Norwich Terrier is a low, stocky dog with a slightly rounded skull, dark expressive eyes, and small upright ears. Its hard, wiry topcoat lies close over a softer undercoat and comes in shades of red, wheaten, black and tan, or grizzle. A modest ruff frames the face. Though small enough to tuck under an arm, the breed is robustly built rather than dainty.

In temperament, the Norwich is alert, affectionate, and fearless. These terriers are people-oriented and generally less scrappy with other dogs than many of their relatives, often described as having a "demon to deliver" working drive paired with a gregarious, sociable home life. They make spirited watchdogs but adapt well to apartment or country living alike.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality complete dog food formulated for small breeds, portioned to the dog's weight, age, and activity level. Because Norwich Terriers are small and can be enthusiastic eaters, measure meals carefully and limit treats to prevent weight gain, which strains the joints and back. Divide the daily ration into two meals. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian before changing diets or if weight creeps up.

## Exercise & training

Norwich Terriers are lively and need daily exercise: a couple of brisk walks plus play and the chance to explore satisfy most individuals. They enjoy games, earthdog activities, and agility, which channel their hunting instincts. A securely fenced area is wise, as their prey drive may send them chasing small animals.

Training should be consistent, upbeat, and reward-based. These intelligent terriers learn quickly but have an independent streak, so keep sessions short and engaging. Early socialization helps them become well-rounded adults, and recall practice is valuable given their instinct to chase.

## Grooming & care

The wiry coat is best maintained by hand-stripping a few times a year to preserve its proper harsh texture and color; clipping softens the coat over time. Weekly brushing removes loose hair and prevents matting, especially around the ears and legs. Routine care includes nail trims, regular tooth brushing to guard against dental disease common in small breeds, and checking the ears for debris. The breed sheds modestly.

## Health & lifespan

Norwich Terriers are generally hardy and often live 12 to 15 years. Conditions seen in the breed include a breathing issue sometimes called "upper airway syndrome," dental problems, hip dysplasia, luxating patella (slipping kneecaps), and occasional eye conditions. Responsible breeders screen breeding stock. Keep your dog at a healthy weight, maintain dental and parasite-prevention routines, and schedule regular veterinary checkups to catch issues early.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Norwich Terriers good apartment dogs?**
Yes. Their small size makes them suited to apartments, provided they receive daily walks and play. They can be vocal watchdogs, so training to limit excessive barking is helpful.

**What is the difference between a Norwich and a Norfolk Terrier?**
The breeds are very similar but distinguished mainly by their ears: the Norwich has erect, pointed ears, while the Norfolk has ears that drop forward. They were recognized as separate breeds in the 1960s.

## Conclusion

The Norwich Terrier packs the courage and drive of a working terrier into a small, affectionate package. With regular exercise, consistent positive training, occasional coat stripping, and attentive health care, this sturdy little dog makes a devoted and entertaining companion for active individuals and families alike.
