The Stabyhoun is a rare, versatile dog breed from the Friesland region of the Netherlands, where it was traditionally kept on farms as an all-purpose hunter, watchdog and family companion. Though still uncommon outside its homeland, the breed has earned a devoted following for its calm, friendly nature and willingness to work. Its name is often linked to the Frisian phrase meaning "stand by me dog," which suits this loyal, people-oriented breed well.

## Appearance & temperament

The Stabyhoun is a sturdy, balanced medium-sized dog, with males generally standing around 53 cm at the shoulder and females slightly smaller. The coat is medium in length, lying sleek but with possible slight waves over the rump, and most often appears black and white, though brown and white and orange and white also occur. Feathering on the legs, chest and tail adds to its soft outline.

Temperamentally, the Stabyhoun is known for being gentle, affectionate and intelligent. It tends to bond closely with its family, is generally patient with children, and is usually friendly without being overly excitable. The breed can be somewhat reserved with strangers and makes an alert, sensible watchdog, but it is not typically aggressive.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed your Stabyhoun a complete, balanced dog food appropriate for its age, size and activity level. As an active working-type breed of moderate size, it generally does well on quality food split into two meals a day. Measure portions to maintain a lean, healthy body condition, since extra weight strains the joints. Keep fresh water available at all times, and consult your veterinarian before making major diet changes or adding supplements.

## Exercise & training

The Stabyhoun has moderate exercise needs and enjoys daily walks, play and the chance to use its nose and mind. Aim for a solid hour or more of activity each day, including some off-leash exploration in safe areas, since this breed retains strong hunting and retrieving instincts. Mental stimulation through scent games, training and interactive play is just as important as physical activity.

Highly intelligent and eager to please, the Stabyhoun usually responds best to patient, positive, reward-based training. The breed can have a soft, sensitive side, so harsh methods are counterproductive. Early socialization and consistent, gentle guidance help produce a well-mannered, confident adult.

## Grooming & care

The Stabyhoun's coat is relatively low-maintenance and is considered fairly weather-resistant and self-cleaning. Weekly brushing is usually enough to remove loose hair and prevent tangles, with extra attention during seasonal shedding. Bathe only as needed, since the coat tends to repel dirt naturally. As with any dog, check and clean the ears regularly, trim the nails, and maintain a routine of dental care.

## Health & lifespan

The Stabyhoun is generally a hardy, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of around 13 to 15 years. Because the breed comes from a small population, responsible breeders carefully manage genetic diversity. Health considerations sometimes discussed in the breed include hip dysplasia and, less commonly, certain inherited conditions. Choosing a breeder who screens their breeding dogs, along with routine veterinary checkups, supports a long and healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Stabyhouns good family dogs?**
Yes. The Stabyhoun is typically gentle, loyal and patient, and it usually bonds strongly with its family, including children, making it a well-suited companion for an active household.

**Do Stabyhouns shed a lot?**
They shed a moderate amount year-round, with heavier seasonal shedding. Regular weekly brushing keeps the coat healthy and helps manage loose hair around the home.

## Conclusion

The Stabyhoun is a charming, versatile companion that combines a willing working heritage with a calm, affectionate temperament. With moderate exercise, simple coat care, patient positive training and regular veterinary attention, this rare Dutch breed can be a devoted and rewarding family dog for many years.
