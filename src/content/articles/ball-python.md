The Ball Python (*Python regius*) is one of the most popular pet snakes in the world, prized for its manageable size, docile nature, and striking patterns. Native to the grasslands and forests of West and Central Africa, it earns its name from its habit of curling into a tight ball when stressed. With proper care, it makes a rewarding companion for beginner and experienced keepers alike.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| Adult size | Roughly 3–5 ft (0.9–1.5 m) |
| Lifespan | 20–30 years in captivity |
| Enclosure size | 40-gallon equivalent or larger for adults |
| Temperament | Calm, shy, slow-moving |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & temperament

Ball Pythons are stocky, muscular snakes with a relatively small head and smooth, glossy scales. Wild-type individuals show brown and tan blotches over a dark background, while captive breeding has produced hundreds of color and pattern variations known as "morphs." They are generally shy and gentle, rarely biting, and prefer to retreat or ball up rather than show aggression. Their calm disposition is a major reason for their popularity.

## Habitat & enclosure

A single adult needs a secure enclosure equivalent to at least a 40-gallon tank, with larger being better for activity. A tight-fitting, escape-proof lid is essential, as these snakes are strong and curious. Provide at least two hides—one on the warm side and one on the cool side—so the snake feels secure throughout its temperature range. Suitable substrates include cypress mulch, coconut fiber, or aspen, which help hold humidity. Add sturdy branches and clutter for enrichment.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Create a thermal gradient with a warm basking area around 88–92°F (31–33°C) and a cooler side near 75–80°F (24–27°C). Use a thermostat-controlled heat source and verify temperatures with reliable thermometers. While Ball Pythons are nocturnal and do not strictly require UVB, low-level UVB lighting can support overall health. Maintain humidity around 55–65%, raising it during shedding. A large water bowl and occasional misting help keep humidity stable.

## Diet & feeding

Ball Pythons are carnivores that eat appropriately sized rodents, typically mice or rats matching the width of the snake's body. Hatchlings eat roughly every 5–7 days, while adults eat every 1–2 weeks. Frozen-thawed prey is safest and recommended over live rodents, which can injure the snake. It is common for Ball Pythons to refuse food for weeks, especially during cooler months or breeding season; an occasional fast is usually normal in an otherwise healthy snake.

## Handling & health

Handle gently and support the snake's body, keeping sessions short and avoiding handling right after feeding or during a shed. Common health concerns include respiratory infections, scale rot from overly wet conditions, mites, and incomplete sheds, which often signal husbandry problems. Watch for wheezing, prolonged appetite loss, or unusual skin. Always consult a qualified reptile veterinarian for diagnosis and treatment rather than attempting home remedies.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Ball Pythons good for beginners?** Yes. Their calm temperament, modest size, and straightforward care make them one of the best starter snakes, provided their heating and humidity needs are met consistently.

**Is it normal for a Ball Python to stop eating?** Often, yes. Healthy Ball Pythons may fast for weeks, particularly in winter, but persistent refusal with weight loss warrants a vet visit.

## Conclusion

With a secure enclosure, careful temperature and humidity control, and a steady feeding routine, a Ball Python can thrive for decades. Legality and availability vary by region, so check local regulations, and seek specialist reptile care when needed. Their gentle nature and long lifespan make them a deeply rewarding reptile companion.
