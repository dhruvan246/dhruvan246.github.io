The cockatiel is one of the most popular pet birds in the world, prized for its gentle nature, charming crest, and ability to bond closely with people. Native to the arid interior of Australia, this small member of the cockatoo family is hardy, affectionate, and well suited to first-time bird keepers willing to offer daily attention.

## Overview

| Trait | Typical range |

## Appearance & temperament

Cockatiels are slender, long-tailed birds easily recognized by their upright crest and rounded orange cheek patches. The classic "wild type" is grey with a yellow face, but selective breeding has produced many color variations, including lutino, pied, pearl, cinnamon, and whiteface. Males of the grey type usually show brighter yellow heads, while females tend to retain barring under the tail.

Temperamentally, cockatiels are affectionate, curious, and generally calm. Hand-raised birds often enjoy being scratched around the crest and perching on a shoulder. The crest is a useful mood indicator: held high signals alertness or excitement, while a flattened crest can mean fear or aggression.

## Housing & cage setup

Provide the largest cage you can, with a minimum of roughly 24 inches wide so the bird can stretch and flap. Bar spacing should be about half an inch to prevent escapes or trapped heads. Horizontal bars help with climbing.

Include several perches of varying diameter and natural texture to keep feet healthy; avoid filling the cage with sandpaper perches. Add food and water dishes, a few safe toys, and a cuttlebone for calcium. Place the cage in a draft-free room with regular family activity but away from kitchen fumes, which are toxic to birds. Cockatiels also need daily out-of-cage time in a supervised, bird-proofed space.

## Diet & nutrition

A balanced diet is the foundation of a long, healthy life. A formulated pellet should make up the bulk of the diet, supplemented with fresh vegetables and leafy greens such as carrot, broccoli, and kale. Seed mixes are enjoyed but are fatty, so offer them sparingly rather than as a staple.

Provide fresh water daily and a constant source of calcium through cuttlebone or a mineral block. Avoid avocado, chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, and salty or sugary human foods, all of which can be harmful or fatal. Introduce new foods gradually, since cockatiels can be cautious eaters.

## Enrichment & handling

Cockatiels are intelligent and social, and they can become bored or develop feather-plucking habits without stimulation. Rotate toys regularly, offer shreddable items, foraging puzzles, and a swing or ladder. Many cockatiels love to whistle along to tunes and respond well to gentle, consistent training.

Handle your bird daily to maintain trust. Move slowly, speak softly, and reward calm behavior. Because they are flock animals, a lone cockatiel needs you to act as its companion, so plan for regular interaction.

## Health & lifespan

With good care, cockatiels commonly live 15 to 20 years, and some exceed that. Watch for warning signs such as fluffed feathers, lethargy, tail bobbing while breathing, discharge, or changes in droppings or appetite. Birds hide illness well, so any change warrants prompt attention.

Find an avian or exotics veterinarian before problems arise, and consider periodic wellness checks. Keep the environment clean, avoid airborne toxins like nonstick cookware fumes and aerosols, and provide good lighting and sleep.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are cockatiels good for beginners?** Yes. They are hardy, affordable, and gentle, making them one of the best starter birds, though they still need daily attention and a long-term commitment.

**Do cockatiels talk?** They learn only a few words at most, but they are talented whistlers and can mimic melodies, beeps, and household sounds.

## Conclusion

Cockatiels reward attentive owners with years of companionship, whistled tunes, and affectionate personalities. With a roomy cage, a balanced diet, daily handling, and access to avian veterinary care, this friendly Australian parrot makes a delightful and manageable pet. Regulations on keeping birds vary by region, so check local rules before bringing one home.
