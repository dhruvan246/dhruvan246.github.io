The Pumi is a lively, intelligent herding breed from Hungary, instantly recognizable by its curly coat and expressive, semi-erect ears that give it a perpetually alert, whimsical look. Bred to drive and gather livestock, the Pumi is energetic, vocal, and deeply attached to its people.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |

| Lifespan | 12-13 years |

| Energy | High |
| Good with kids | Yes, with early socialization |

## Appearance & temperament

The Pumi stands roughly 15-19 inches at the shoulder and carries a square, agile build suited to nimble, fast-paced work. Its most distinctive features are the corkscrew-curly coat, which combines a soft undercoat with a harsher topcoat, and the high-set ears that tip forward at the top. Coats come in gray, black, white, and shades of fawn.

Temperamentally, the Pumi is alert, bold, and quick to react. It bonds closely with its family and tends to be reserved with strangers, making it a natural watchdog. This is a vocal breed that often "talks" and barks readily, a trait inherited from its livestock-driving heritage.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet formulated for the Pumi's age and activity level. Because this is an active working breed, look for quality protein to support lean muscle. Most adult Pumik do well on two measured meals a day. Monitor body condition closely, as the curly coat can hide weight gain. Keep fresh water available, and use treats thoughtfully during the breed's frequent training sessions to avoid overfeeding.

## Exercise & training

The Pumi needs substantial daily exercise, both physical and mental. Long walks, runs, and active play should be paired with jobs that engage its sharp mind. The breed excels at dog sports such as agility, obedience, herding, and rally, and thrives when given problems to solve.

Highly trainable and eager to work, the Pumi learns quickly with positive, reward-based methods. Early, consistent training helps channel its energy and manage its tendency to bark. Socialization from puppyhood is important to build confidence around new people, dogs, and environments. A bored or under-exercised Pumi can become noisy and restless.

## Grooming & care

The Pumi's curly coat is relatively low-shedding but needs regular attention to keep its characteristic texture. Comb or mist and scrunch the coat every few weeks to maintain the curls, and trim periodically rather than brushing it out straight. Many owners have the coat hand-tidied a few times a year. Routine care includes regular nail trims, dental brushing, and checking the ears for moisture or debris. Bathe only as needed, then allow the coat to air-dry so the curls reform naturally.

## Health & lifespan

The Pumi is generally a hardy, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of about 12-13 years. As with many breeds, responsible breeders screen for conditions such as hip dysplasia, patellar luxation, and certain eye disorders. Routine veterinary checkups, parasite prevention, dental care, and weight management all support a long, active life. Always consult your veterinarian for guidance tailored to your individual dog.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Pumik good apartment dogs?**
They can adapt to smaller homes if given plenty of daily exercise and mental work, but their tendency to bark means training and consideration for neighbors are essential.

**Does the Pumi shed a lot?**
The Pumi is a relatively low-shedding breed, though no dog is truly hypoallergenic. Its curly coat needs regular maintenance to stay healthy and keep its shape.

## Conclusion

The Pumi is a spirited, clever companion best suited to active owners who can meet its needs for exercise, training, and engagement. Reward its sharp mind, manage its vocal nature, and maintain its distinctive curly coat, and the Pumi will be a devoted, energetic partner for years.
