The Patterdale Terrier is a small, tough, and fearless working terrier that originated in the Lake District of northern England. Bred to hunt foxes and other quarry across rugged fell country, it remains one of the most determined and hard-working of the small terrier breeds.

## Appearance & temperament

The Patterdale Terrier is a compact, muscular dog standing roughly 10 to 15 inches tall and weighing around 11 to 13 pounds. Its head is wedge-shaped with a strong jaw, and its body is sturdy and athletic rather than refined. The coat comes in three types: smooth, broken, and rough, and is commonly seen in black, though red, liver, chocolate, and black-and-tan also occur.

Temperamentally, the Patterdale is confident, energetic, and unusually bold for its size. Bred to face quarry underground, it is tenacious and self-assured. These terriers are affectionate and loyal with their families but retain strong prey drive and can be reserved with strangers and combative with other dogs if not socialized early.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality dog food appropriate for a small, active breed. Because Patterdales are working dogs with fast metabolisms, they often need calorie-dense, protein-rich nutrition relative to their size. Divide the daily amount into two meals to maintain steady energy.

Portion sizes should reflect the dog's age, weight, and activity level; working Patterdales burn far more than couch companions. Monitor body condition closely, as their dense build can hide weight gain. Always provide fresh water, and treat measured snacks as part of the daily calorie total.

## Exercise & training

This is a high-energy breed that needs substantial daily exercise. Expect to provide at least an hour of activity through brisk walks, off-lead running in secure areas, and games. Mentally stimulating activities such as scent work, earthdog trials, and puzzle toys help satisfy their working instincts and prevent boredom-driven digging or barking.

Patterdales are intelligent but independent and strong-willed. Training should be consistent, firm, and reward-based, starting in puppyhood. Recall can be challenging because of their intense prey drive, so a securely fenced yard and lead work near small animals are essential. Early socialization with people and other dogs is strongly recommended.

## Grooming & care

Grooming needs are modest. The smooth and broken coats require only weekly brushing, while the rough coat benefits from occasional hand-stripping to maintain texture. Bathe only when necessary to preserve natural oils.

Routine care includes checking and cleaning ears, trimming nails regularly, and brushing teeth several times a week to support dental health. After working or running through dense cover, inspect the coat and skin for burrs, ticks, and minor scrapes.

## Health & lifespan

The Patterdale Terrier is generally a hardy, robust breed with a typical lifespan of around 11 to 13 years. As a working dog developed for function over appearance, it tends to have fewer inherited problems than many pedigree breeds.

Conditions occasionally reported include eye disorders, luxating patella, and a hereditary skin condition sometimes seen in the breed. Their fearless nature also makes them prone to injuries from rough activity. Regular veterinary checkups, dental care, parasite prevention, and weight management all support a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Patterdale Terriers good apartment dogs?**
They can adapt to smaller homes if given enough daily exercise and mental stimulation, but their high energy and tendency to bark mean they thrive best with an active owner and access to safe outdoor space.

**Do Patterdale Terriers get along with other pets?**
Their strong prey drive makes them risky around small pets like rodents, rabbits, and sometimes cats. With early socialization they can live with other dogs, but some remain dog-reactive.

## Conclusion

The Patterdale Terrier is a small but mighty working breed suited to active owners who appreciate its courage, drive, and loyalty. With plenty of exercise, consistent training, and early socialization, this hardy terrier makes a spirited and devoted companion.
