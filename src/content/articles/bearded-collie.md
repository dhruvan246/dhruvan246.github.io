Bouncy, shaggy and irrepressibly cheerful, the Bearded Collie is an old Scottish herding breed prized for its long flowing coat and lively, affectionate nature. Bred to drive sheep and cattle across the rugged Highlands in all weather, the "Beardie" remains an energetic, people-loving companion that thrives on activity and family life.

## Appearance & temperament

The Bearded Collie is a lean, athletic herding dog of medium build, slightly longer than it is tall. Its hallmark is the long, harsh outer coat over a soft, dense undercoat, complete with the characteristic "beard" of hair on the chin. Coat colors include black, brown, blue and fawn, often with white markings, and many Beardies fade or shift in shade as they mature. The expressive face and gently inquiring eyes give the breed a friendly, alert appearance.

In temperament, the Bearded Collie is outgoing, confident and famously bouncy. It is affectionate with its family, generally good with children and other dogs, and tends to greet life with enthusiasm. Beardies are intelligent and independent-minded, a legacy of working at a distance from the shepherd, so they can be playfully stubborn. They are vocal dogs that may bark to communicate.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality complete dog food appropriate to your Beardie's age, size and activity level. Active working or sporting dogs need more calories than couch companions, so adjust portions to maintain a trim waist that you can feel but not see. Divide the daily ration into two meals to support steady energy.

Because the breed is prone to weight gain when under-exercised, measure food carefully and keep treats modest. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian before making major dietary changes.

## Exercise & training

This is a high-energy breed that needs substantial daily exercise, typically an hour or more of walking, running and play. Beardies excel at dog sports such as agility, herding trials, obedience and flyball, which channel both body and mind. A bored or under-exercised Bearded Collie may become noisy, mischievous or destructive.

Training should start early and stay positive. Beardies respond well to reward-based methods, variety and patience, but they can switch off in the face of harsh or repetitive drilling. Early socialization helps temper their exuberance, and a reliable recall is essential given their herding instincts.

## Grooming & care

The long double coat demands serious commitment. Brush thoroughly several times a week, ideally working down to the skin to prevent the dense undercoat from matting, especially behind the ears, under the legs and around the beard. The beard itself collects food and water and benefits from regular wiping.

Bathe as needed, and check ears, trim nails and brush teeth on a routine schedule. Expect heavy seasonal shedding when the coat blows. Some owners keep pets in a shorter trim for easier upkeep.

## Health & lifespan

Bearded Collies are generally hardy and typically live 12-14 years. Like many breeds, they can be predisposed to certain conditions, including hip dysplasia, autoimmune disorders such as hypoadrenocorticism (Addison's disease), hypothyroidism and some eye conditions. Responsible breeders screen their stock for known hereditary issues.

Routine veterinary care, parasite prevention, dental hygiene and weight management all support a long, healthy life. Watch for unusual lethargy, appetite changes or skin and coat problems, and seek prompt veterinary advice when concerns arise.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Bearded Collies shed a lot?**
Yes. They carry a heavy double coat and shed year-round, with much heavier seasonal sheds. Frequent brushing is essential to control loose hair and prevent matting.

**Are Bearded Collies good family dogs?**
They can be excellent family companions. Beardies are affectionate, playful and typically good with children, though their high energy and bounciness mean they suit active households that can meet their exercise needs.

## Conclusion

The Bearded Collie rewards an active, committed owner with joyful companionship, athletic versatility and a personality full of bounce. Given plenty of exercise, consistent positive training and diligent coat care, this shaggy Scottish herder makes a devoted and entertaining member of the family.
