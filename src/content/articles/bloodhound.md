Famous for owning perhaps the most powerful nose in the dog world, the Bloodhound is a large, dignified scent hound long prized for tracking and search-and-rescue work. Behind the droopy ears and deeply wrinkled face is a gentle, affectionate and famously stubborn companion that follows its nose above all else.

## Overview

| Trait | Detail |

## Appearance & temperament

The Bloodhound is a powerful, substantial dog with loose, wrinkled skin, very long pendulous ears and a soulful, slightly mournful expression. Coats are short and typically seen in black and tan, liver and tan, or red. Adults usually stand around 58–69 cm at the shoulder. Temperamentally they are gentle, patient and affectionate, often tolerant with children and other dogs. They are also independent and single-minded, especially once they pick up an interesting scent, and can be reserved rather than boisterous indoors.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet formulated for large breeds, with portions matched to age, weight and activity level. Because the Bloodhound is a deep-chested breed at higher risk of bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus), many owners divide the daily ration into two or more smaller meals and avoid heavy exercise immediately before and after eating. Keep fresh water available and monitor body condition, since excess weight adds strain to joints. Your veterinarian can advise on the most suitable food and feeding routine.

## Exercise & training

Bloodhounds need plenty of daily exercise and enjoy long walks and the chance to follow trails, which satisfies their natural urge to scent. Always keep them on lead or within secure, well-fenced areas, as a compelling smell can lead them away for great distances and recall is notoriously unreliable. Training requires patience: they are intelligent but stubborn and easily distracted by their nose. Short, positive, reward-based sessions, started early alongside thorough socialisation, work far better than repetition or harsh corrections.

## Grooming & care

The short coat is low-maintenance and benefits from weekly brushing to remove loose hair. The breed's most important care needs relate to its distinctive features: the long, low-hanging ears trap moisture and need regular checking and gentle cleaning to help prevent infections, and the facial wrinkles should be kept clean and dry. Prospective owners should also be prepared for substantial drooling, so keeping cloths handy is wise. Routine nail trims and dental care round out a sensible grooming schedule.

## Health & lifespan

With a typical lifespan of roughly 10 to 12 years, Bloodhounds are generally robust but, like many large breeds, are predisposed to certain conditions. These commonly include bloat, hip and elbow dysplasia, ear infections linked to their long ears, and some eye conditions. Choosing a breeder who health-tests their dogs, maintaining a healthy weight, and scheduling regular veterinary check-ups all support a longer, more comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Bloodhounds drool a lot?**
Yes. The breed is well known for heavy drooling, especially after eating or drinking, so owners should expect to keep towels on hand and accept some mess as part of life with the breed.

**Can a Bloodhound be trusted off-lead?**
Usually not. Their tracking instinct can override recall, and a scent may lead them far from home, so off-lead freedom is generally only safe within securely fenced spaces.

## Conclusion

The Bloodhound is a gentle, affectionate and remarkable scent hound best suited to owners who can accommodate drool, stubbornness and serious nose-driven behaviour. With secure boundaries, patient positive training, attentive ear and wrinkle care, and good large-breed health management, this soulful tracker makes a devoted and characterful family companion.
