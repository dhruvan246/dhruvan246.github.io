The Glen of Imaal Terrier is a sturdy, low-slung working terrier from the remote Glen of Imaal valley in County Wicklow, Ireland. Bred to dig out badgers and foxes and to work farms, this rare breed combines surprising strength with a notably calm, easygoing nature for a terrier.

## Appearance & temperament

The Glen is a big dog on short legs. Males typically stand around 12.5-14 inches at the shoulder and weigh roughly 32-40 pounds, giving a low, powerful, longer-than-tall outline. The harsh medium-length outer coat covers a soft undercoat, and colors include wheaten, blue, and brindle. Bowed front legs and a strong head are breed hallmarks.

Temperamentally, the Glen is gentler and quieter than many terriers. It is courageous and game when working, yet relaxed at home, rarely yappy, and devoted to its family. Like most terriers, it can be stubborn and may not back down from other dogs, so early socialization matters.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food suited to the Glen's age and activity level. Because this is a compact, muscular breed prone to weight gain when under-exercised, measure portions and limit treats. Two meals a day works well for most adults, with puppies fed more frequently. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian about the right amount and any joint-supportive nutrition for a long-bodied dog.

## Exercise & training

Despite the short legs, Glens are athletic and enjoy moderate daily activity: a couple of brisk walks plus playtime or a digging-friendly outlet will keep one content. They love to dig and may chase small animals, so a secure yard and a leash in open areas are wise.

Training should be firm, consistent, and reward-based. Glens are intelligent but independent and can be willful, so short, motivating sessions beat repetitive drilling. Early socialization with people, dogs, and handling builds confidence and curbs the breed's natural scrappiness toward other dogs.

## Grooming & care

The harsh double coat needs brushing about once a week to remove loose hair and prevent matting. The coat is traditionally hand-stripped a couple of times a year to maintain its texture; clipping is an option for pets but softens the coat. Bathe only as needed. Check and clean ears, trim nails regularly, and brush teeth several times a week to support dental health.

## Health & lifespan

Glens are generally hardy and typically live 10-15 years. Responsible breeders screen for hip dysplasia and for progressive retinal atrophy and cone-rod dystrophy, an inherited eye condition seen in the breed. As a long-backed dog, the Glen benefits from healthy weight and careful management of jumping. Routine veterinary checkups, parasite prevention, and dental care help keep this breed thriving.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Glen of Imaal Terriers good family dogs?**
Yes. They are loyal, patient, and calmer than many terriers, making good companions for families. Supervise interactions with very young children and other small pets.

**Do Glen of Imaal Terriers shed a lot?**
They shed relatively little. The harsh coat sheds modestly with weekly brushing, though hand-stripping a few times a year keeps it in best condition.

## Conclusion

The Glen of Imaal Terrier offers the spirit of a working terrier in a calm, manageable package. With moderate exercise, consistent training, and simple coat care, this rare Irish breed makes a loyal, characterful companion for owners who appreciate its rugged history and steady temperament.
