The Mystery Snail (*Pomacea bridgesii*, also called *Pomacea diffusa*) is a peaceful freshwater apple snail and one of the most popular invertebrates in the aquarium hobby. Hardy, colourful, and endlessly busy grazing on algae, it makes a rewarding addition to a community tank for beginners and experienced keepers alike.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|---|---|
| Size | 2.5–5 cm (1–2 in) shell diameter |
| Lifespan | 1–3 years |
| Housing | Heated freshwater aquarium, 19 L (5 gal) minimum per snail |
| Temperament | Peaceful, non-aggressive |
| Care level | Beginner / easy |

## Appearance & behaviour

Mystery Snails carry a rounded, spiralled shell that comes in shades of gold, blue, ivory, purple, and brown, often with a striped pattern. Their soft body bears two long sensory tentacles and a siphon they extend to the surface to breathe air. They move slowly across glass and decor, grazing constantly. Unlike some apple snails, *Pomacea bridgesii* rarely eats healthy live plants, making it plant-safe. They are active during the day and night and may occasionally float or seal into their shell with an operculum (trapdoor) when resting or stressed.

## Housing & environment

Provide a cycled, heated aquarium with a secure, tight-fitting lid, as these snails can climb above the waterline and explore. Allow roughly 19 litres (5 gallons) per snail to keep waste manageable. Aim for a temperature of 20–28°C (68–82°F), pH of 7.0–8.0, and moderately hard water. Calcium and stable mineral levels are essential for shell health, so harder water is preferable to soft. Leave a small air gap between the water surface and the lid so the snail can reach air with its siphon. Avoid copper-based medications and fertilisers, which are toxic to snails and other invertebrates.

## Diet & feeding

Mystery Snails are omnivorous scavengers that graze on algae, biofilm, and leftover food. Supplement their diet with blanched vegetables such as zucchini, spinach, kale, and cucumber, plus sinking algae wafers and quality invertebrate pellets. Calcium-rich foods or supplements like cuttlebone help maintain a strong, smooth shell. Feed only what they can finish in a few hours and remove uneaten vegetables to keep water clean. A varied diet prevents shell pitting and supports healthy growth.

## Handling & care

These snails are best observed rather than handled. If you must move one, scoop it gently with a cup rather than pulling it off the glass, as forcing it can damage the foot or shell. Perform regular partial water changes (around 25% weekly) to keep nitrates low and minerals topped up. Acclimate new snails slowly, as they are sensitive to sudden changes in temperature and chemistry. They mix well with peaceful fish, shrimp, and other snails.

## Health

A healthy Mystery Snail moves regularly, feeds, and has a smooth, intact shell. White spots, pitting, or a thin, cracking shell usually signal low calcium or acidic water. Long periods of inactivity can be normal rest, but a snail that floats limply with an open operculum and gives off a strong odour has likely died and should be removed promptly. Avoid copper and ammonia spikes, the most common causes of illness. For persistent health concerns, consult an aquatics specialist or aquatic veterinarian.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Mystery Snails reproduce quickly in a tank?** They lay egg clutches above the waterline, but unlike some pest snails they will not overrun a tank, and clutches can simply be removed if you do not want babies.

**Are Mystery Snails legal everywhere?** No. Apple snails are restricted or banned in some regions because of agricultural risk, so check local regulations before buying, and never release them into the wild.

## Conclusion

The Mystery Snail is a colourful, low-maintenance invertebrate that earns its place as a natural clean-up crew. With stable warm water, plenty of calcium, and a varied diet, it will stay active and healthy for its full lifespan. A secure lid and steady parameters are the only real essentials for keeping this charming snail thriving.
