Sturdy, alert and quietly devoted, the Cardigan Welsh Corgi is one of the oldest herding breeds in Britain, developed to drive cattle across the farms of Wales. Distinguished from its Pembroke cousin by a long, fox-like tail and rounded ears, the Cardigan pairs a low-slung, powerful body with a thoughtful, loyal nature.

## Appearance & temperament

The Cardigan is a long, low dog with a deep chest, short legs and a full brush tail carried low. Heavier and slightly larger-boned than the Pembroke, it stands around 27–33 cm at the shoulder. The weather-resistant double coat comes in many colours, including brindle, blue merle, red, sable and black, often with white markings.

Temperamentally, Cardigans are intelligent, affectionate and adaptable. They tend to be more reserved with strangers than the outgoing Pembroke, making them watchful, vocal companions who form strong bonds with their families. Their herding heritage means they are observant and quick to alert.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced food suited to the dog's age and activity level. Because Cardigans have long backs and a tendency to gain weight, portion control is especially important; excess weight strains the spine and joints. Measure meals rather than free-feeding, and account for treats used in training. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian about ideal body condition and any dietary adjustments.

## Exercise & training

Despite their short legs, Cardigans are athletic and need daily activity, typically a couple of brisk walks plus play or mental games. They excel at herding, agility, obedience and scent work, all of which channel their working drive. Avoid repetitive high-impact jumping that can stress the long back, particularly in puppies.

These dogs are highly trainable and respond well to positive, reward-based methods. Early socialisation helps temper their natural wariness, and teaching a "quiet" cue can manage their tendency to bark. Consistent, engaging training prevents boredom-driven mischief.

## Grooming & care

The double coat sheds steadily year-round, with heavier seasonal moults. Weekly brushing keeps loose hair under control, increasing to several times a week during shedding seasons. Bathe occasionally as needed. Check and trim nails regularly, brush teeth often, and keep ears clean and dry. Given the breed's build, monitor weight closely to protect the back.

## Health & lifespan

Cardigan Welsh Corgis are generally hardy and often live 12 to 15 years. Their elongated frame makes them prone to intervertebral disc disease (IVDD), so discourage jumping from heights and keep them lean. Other conditions seen in the breed include progressive retinal atrophy, hip dysplasia and degenerative myelopathy. Responsible breeders screen for eye and hip health and DM status. Routine veterinary care, weight management and prompt attention to any back or mobility changes support a long, comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Cardigan Welsh Corgi the same as the Pembroke?**
No. They are separate breeds. The Cardigan has a long tail, rounded ears and a heavier build, and tends to be more reserved, while the Pembroke is typically smaller and tailless.

**Do Cardigan Welsh Corgis bark a lot?**
They can be quite vocal due to their herding and watchdog instincts. Early training and a reliable "quiet" cue, along with enough exercise and mental stimulation, help keep barking manageable.

## Conclusion

The Cardigan Welsh Corgi is a clever, loyal and surprisingly versatile companion that thrives on activity and close family life. With sensible weight management to protect its long back, consistent training and regular grooming, this ancient Welsh herder makes a devoted and characterful pet for many years.
