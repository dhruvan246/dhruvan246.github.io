Prairie dogs are highly social, burrowing rodents native to the grasslands of North America. The black-tailed prairie dog is the species most often kept as a pet. They are intelligent, vocal, and deeply bonded to their family group, which makes them rewarding but demanding companions that are not suited to casual or first-time exotic owners.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Size | 12–16 in (30–40 cm) long; 1.5–3 lb (0.7–1.4 kg) |
| Lifespan | 8–10 years in captivity |
| Housing | Large, tall multi-level enclosure with digging substrate |
| Temperament | Social, affectionate, vocal, territorial when mature |
| Care level | Advanced |

## Appearance & temperament

Prairie dogs are stocky ground squirrels with sandy to tan fur, short legs, small ears, and a short tail tipped in black on the black-tailed species. They have strong claws built for digging and prominent incisors that grow continuously.

In the wild they live in large colonies called "towns" and communicate through an elaborate system of barks, chirps, and a distinctive "jump-yip" call. As pets they crave constant companionship and may become anxious if isolated. Hand-raised individuals can be remarkably affectionate, but intact adults often go through a hormonal "rut" each year, becoming territorial and nippy.

## Housing & environment

Provide the largest enclosure you can. A tall, multi-level cage or a custom habitat several feet wide is ideal, with bar spacing narrow enough to prevent escape. Because prairie dogs are obsessive diggers, include a deep bedding area of aspen shavings, paper bedding, or a dig box so they can express this natural behavior. Avoid cedar and pine shavings, which release irritating oils.

Add hides, tunnels, and ramps to mimic burrow systems. Keep the habitat at normal room temperature, away from drafts and direct sun. Prairie dogs are clean and can often be litter-trained to a corner. Daily supervised time outside the cage in a prairie-dog-proofed room is essential for exercise and mental health.

## Diet & nutrition

A correct diet is built around high-fiber grass hay. Offer unlimited timothy, orchard, or other grass hay at all times to support digestion and wear down ever-growing teeth. Supplement with a measured amount of a high-fiber, low-fat pellet formulated for prairie dogs or rabbits.

Fresh vegetables and small amounts of greens can be offered, but avoid sugary fruits, seeds, nuts, and high-fat or high-protein foods, which contribute to obesity and dental disease. Provide clean, fresh water daily via a bottle or heavy bowl. Many keepers feed more sparingly in late autumn and winter, when wild prairie dogs naturally reduce activity.

## Handling & enrichment

Start gentle, frequent handling early to build trust. Support the body fully and never grab by the tail. Once bonded, prairie dogs enjoy sitting with their owners, grooming, and "kissing." Respect their moods during the seasonal rut, when even tame animals may bite.

Enrichment is critical for these clever, busy animals. Offer digging opportunities, safe chew items to manage tooth growth, tunnels, foraging puzzles, and plenty of social interaction. A bored, lonely prairie dog can become stressed and destructive.

## Health, legality & ethics

Common health concerns include obesity, dental overgrowth, respiratory infections, and a heart condition sometimes seen in older or overweight animals. Find an exotics-experienced veterinarian before bringing one home, and schedule routine wellness checks. Many vets recommend spaying or neutering to reduce hormonal aggression and certain health risks.

Legality varies widely by region. Some areas restrict or ban prairie dog ownership, and historic disease concerns have shaped these rules, so confirm local laws first. Ethically, source only captive-bred animals from reputable breeders and never wild-caught individuals.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I keep just one prairie dog?** It is possible, but they are intensely social. A single prairie dog needs a great deal of daily human interaction to stay psychologically healthy, and many do better with a companion.

**Do prairie dogs hibernate as pets?** Black-tailed prairie dogs do not truly hibernate, but they often become less active and eat less in the colder months, which is normal seasonal behavior.

## Conclusion

Prairie dogs are charming, intelligent, and deeply social pets, but they require advanced care, a spacious digging-friendly habitat, a hay-based diet, and an owner prepared for their seasonal mood changes. With the right commitment and an exotics vet on call, a well-socialized prairie dog can be a loyal, entertaining companion for a decade.
