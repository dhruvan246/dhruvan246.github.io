Poison dart frogs (family Dendrobatidae) are small, brilliantly coloured amphibians from the rainforests of Central and South America. Popular hobby species include the dyeing dart frog (*Dendrobates tinctorius*), the green-and-black (*D. auratus*), and the strawberry frog (*Oophaga pumilio*). They are prized as living jewels for planted terrariums, but they need stable, humid conditions and a constant supply of tiny live food.

## Appearance & behaviour

Dart frogs are famous for their bold warning colours—blue, yellow, orange, green, and red in striking patterns that signal toxicity to predators. They are diurnal, meaning they are active during the day, which makes them rewarding display animals. Most are bold once settled, hopping across the substrate and foraging on leaves. Males of many species call with a soft buzz or trill to attract mates and defend territory.

## Habitat & water

A tall or cube-shaped glass vivarium with a tight-fitting lid is ideal, as these frogs are escape artists. Build a bioactive setup with a drainage layer, a screen barrier, and a substrate of coconut fibre and sphagnum moss topped with leaf litter. Live plants such as bromeliads, pothos, and ferns provide cover and humidity. Maintain humidity at 80–100 percent with daily misting or an automated mister, and keep temperatures between 70–80°F (21–27°C); most species suffer above 85°F. Provide a shallow water dish with dechlorinated water. Low-level UVB and gentle LED lighting support plants and the frogs' wellbeing.

## Diet & feeding

Poison dart frogs eat tiny live invertebrates. The staple in captivity is flightless fruit flies (*Drosophila*), supplemented with springtails and pinhead crickets. Their small mouths cannot handle large prey. Feed adults every one to two days and juveniles daily. Crucially, dust feeder insects with a calcium and vitamin supplement (including vitamin A and D3) at most feedings—nutritional deficiency is a leading cause of health problems and is often what dulls a frog's colour over time.

## Handling & health

Dart frogs should not be handled. Their delicate, permeable skin readily absorbs oils, salts, and chemicals from human hands, which can harm or kill them. Move them only when necessary using a clean, wet container. Note that captive-bred dart frogs are non-toxic: their toxicity in the wild comes from a diet of certain ants and mites, so captive animals raised on fruit flies lose the alkaloids that make them dangerous. Watch for signs of illness such as weight loss, lethargy, bloating, or a frog that stops eating, and consult an exotics veterinarian. Always buy captive-bred animals from reputable breeders.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are captive poison dart frogs dangerous to keep?** No. They acquire toxins from wild prey, so captive-bred frogs fed fruit flies and crickets are harmless. Still, wash your hands before and after maintenance.

**Can I keep poison dart frogs together?** Some species tolerate small groups in a large, well-planted vivarium, but many become territorial. Research your specific species and avoid mixing different species. Legality and import rules vary by region, so check local laws.

## Conclusion

Poison dart frogs reward attentive keepers with dazzling colour and lively daytime behaviour. Success comes down to a stable, humid bioactive vivarium, a reliable supply of supplemented live insects, and a strict hands-off approach. With the right setup and captive-bred stock, these little gems can thrive for well over a decade.
