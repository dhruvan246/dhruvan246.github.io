The Wolfspitz is the largest of the German Spitz varieties and is known internationally as the Keeshond. Distinguished by its wolf-grey coloring and plush double coat, this medium-sized companion combines a lively, affectionate personality with a striking "spectacled" facial pattern. Once a watchdog on Dutch riverboats and farms, the Wolfspitz today is valued mainly as a devoted family dog.

## Appearance & temperament

The Wolfspitz has a foxlike head, small triangular erect ears, and a tightly curled tail carried over the back. Its hallmark is the wolf-grey coat with cream undertones and black-tipped guard hairs, plus the characteristic dark markings and pale "spectacles" around the eyes. A pronounced ruff frames the neck.

Temperamentally, the breed is alert, friendly, and people-oriented. Wolfspitze bond closely with their families and make excellent watchdogs, quick to announce visitors, though they are rarely aggressive. They are intelligent and sensitive, thriving on companionship and disliking long periods of isolation.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet formulated for the dog's life stage and activity level. As a medium breed prone to weight gain, the Wolfspitz benefits from measured portions divided into two meals a day rather than free feeding. The thick coat can hide excess weight, so check body condition by feeling for the ribs rather than relying on appearance.

Provide constant access to fresh water and keep treats to a small fraction of daily calories. Adjust portions for less active or senior dogs to prevent obesity, which strains the joints and heart. Consult your veterinarian before any major diet change.

## Exercise & training

Despite its companion role, the Wolfspitz is active and enjoys daily walks, play sessions, and chances to explore. Around 45 minutes to an hour of activity per day usually keeps the breed content. Because it is clever and eager to please, the Wolfspitz also benefits from mental challenges such as puzzle toys, scent games, and trick training.

Training tends to be straightforward with positive, reward-based methods; harsh handling can make this sensitive breed anxious or stubborn. Early socialization helps temper its natural watchfulness and barking. Many Wolfspitze excel in obedience and agility.

## Grooming & care

The double coat needs regular care. Brush thoroughly at least twice a week to prevent matting, and more often during the two heavy shedding seasons each year, when the soft undercoat is released in large amounts. A slicker brush and undercoat rake work well.

Avoid shaving the coat, as it insulates against both heat and cold; trimming should be minimal. Bathe only as needed, since the coat is fairly self-cleaning. Routine nail trimming, ear checks, and dental care round out maintenance.

## Health & lifespan

The Wolfspitz is generally hardy, with a typical lifespan of about 12 to 15 years. Like many purebred dogs, it can be predisposed to certain conditions, including hip dysplasia, luxating patella, and some inherited eye and heart issues. Hypothyroidism and skin problems are also seen in the breed.

Choosing a breeder who screens breeding stock, keeping up with routine veterinary visits, and maintaining a healthy weight all support a long life. Watch for early signs of illness and seek prompt veterinary advice when concerned.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Wolfspitz the same as a Keeshond?**
Yes. The Wolfspitz is the wolf-grey German Spitz variety, and in many countries this same dog is registered under the name Keeshond.

**Does the Wolfspitz shed a lot?**
Yes. The dense double coat sheds steadily and blows out heavily twice a year, so regular brushing and seasonal grooming are essential.

## Conclusion

The Wolfspitz is a handsome, good-natured companion that suits families willing to meet its grooming and exercise needs. With its loyal temperament, alert watchfulness, and striking wolf-grey coat, it rewards attentive owners with affection and a long, lively partnership.
