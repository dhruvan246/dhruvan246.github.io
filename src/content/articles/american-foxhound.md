Bred in the United States to chase fox over long distances, the American Foxhound is a lean, tireless scent hound with a melodious voice and an easygoing, friendly nature. Athletic and pack-oriented, it thrives in active homes that can match its considerable stamina.

## Appearance & temperament

The American Foxhound is taller and lighter-boned than its English cousin, built for speed and endurance over rough country. It has long legs, a deep chest and a short, hard coat that comes in many colors, with tricolor and tan-and-white being common. Temperamentally, the breed is sweet-natured, sociable and easygoing at home, but it was developed to work in packs and generally gets along well with other dogs. As a true scent hound it is independent, can be stubborn, and will follow an interesting trail with single-minded focus. Many are also notably vocal, with a deep, far-carrying bay.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet appropriate to the dog's age, size and activity level. Active, working-type Foxhounds may need more calories than a sedentary pet, so adjust portions to keep the dog lean. Because the breed is deep-chested, splitting the daily ration into two meals and avoiding heavy exercise right around feeding time are sensible habits. Keep fresh water available and monitor body condition, as inactive Foxhounds can gain weight. Discuss specific portions and any dietary concerns with your veterinarian.

## Exercise & training

This is a high-energy breed that needs substantial daily exercise — long walks, jogging, hiking or running in a secure area. Without enough activity, a Foxhound can become bored, restless and noisy. Because the breed's instinct to follow a scent is strong, always exercise on leash or in a securely fenced space; recall can be unreliable once a trail is found. Training should be patient, consistent and reward-based, using food and praise. Early socialization helps these friendly dogs become well-rounded companions.

## Grooming & care

Grooming needs are modest. The short, hard coat benefits from a weekly brushing to remove loose hair and keep it healthy, with more frequent attention during seasonal shedding. Bathe as needed. Check and clean the long, drop ears regularly, since the shape can trap moisture and debris and contribute to ear problems. Trim nails routinely and maintain regular dental care.

## Health & lifespan

The American Foxhound is generally a hardy, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of around 11 to 13 years. As with many active dogs, hip dysplasia can occur, and the long ears make routine ear checks worthwhile to reduce infection risk. A breed-linked condition sometimes discussed is thrombocytopathy, an inherited platelet disorder. Choosing a responsible breeder who screens their dogs, keeping up with routine veterinary care, and maintaining a healthy weight all support a long, comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are American Foxhounds good apartment dogs?**
Generally no. Their high exercise needs and tendency to bay loudly make them better suited to active homes with space and secure outdoor access.

**Do American Foxhounds get along with other dogs?**
Yes. Bred to work in packs, they are typically very sociable and tend to enjoy the company of other dogs.

## Conclusion

The American Foxhound is a loyal, good-natured and athletic companion for people who can meet its need for exercise and accept its independent, vocal nature. Give it plenty of activity, secure boundaries and patient training, and this classic American hound will be a cheerful, devoted family member.
