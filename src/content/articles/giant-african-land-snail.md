The Giant African Land Snail (often *Achatina* or *Lissachatina* species) is one of the largest land snails in the world and a popular, low-cost invertebrate pet. They are quiet, fascinating to watch, and well suited to patient keepers, though their care has some important specifics.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Size | Shell typically 10-20 cm long when mature |
| Lifespan | Around 5-7 years, sometimes longer |
| Housing | Humid, ventilated terrarium with deep substrate |
| Temperament | Calm, slow, harmless, nocturnal |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & behaviour

These snails have a tall, conical, brown-and-cream banded shell and a soft grey-to-brown muscular body. They glide on a slime-coated foot and explore using two pairs of tentacles, the upper pair carrying simple eyes. They are most active at night and in damp conditions, often burrowing into substrate to rest or escape dryness. As hermaphrodites, each snail has both male and female organs, and they can reproduce prolifically, so most keepers manage or remove eggs.

## Housing & environment

A glass or plastic terrarium with a secure, ventilated lid works well; a single adult needs at least a few litres of space, with more being better. Provide 5-8 cm of moist, chemical-free substrate such as coir or topsoil so the snail can burrow. Mist regularly to keep humidity high (around 75-90%) and maintain temperatures of roughly 20-26 C. Avoid direct sunlight and household chemicals. Add cork bark or a half-log for hiding, and spot-clean waste while doing a fuller clean every few weeks.

## Diet & feeding

Giant African Land Snails are herbivores that enjoy a wide variety of fresh vegetables and fruit, such as lettuce, cucumber, courgette, carrot, apple, and pumpkin. Wash produce well and remove uneaten food before it spoils. A constant source of calcium is essential for shell growth and strength, usually offered as a cuttlebone or a piece of natural chalk that the snail rasps. Avoid salty, processed, or citrus-heavy foods. Provide a shallow, very shallow dish of fresh water or simply mist the enclosure for hydration.

## Handling & care

Handle gently and infrequently, supporting the snail's full body rather than pulling it from a surface, which can damage the foot or shell. Wet your hands first so the snail glides comfortably and to avoid sticking. Never pull a snail that is firmly attached; instead, mist it and wait. Always wash your hands before and after handling, as snails can carry bacteria and parasites. Keep handling sessions short and low to a soft surface in case the snail is dropped.

## Health

A healthy snail is active, feeds well, and has a smooth, undamaged shell. Cracks or thin, pitted shells often point to low calcium or humidity and should be corrected promptly. Retracting deep into the shell for long periods, excess mucus, or refusing food can signal stress, poor conditions, or illness. Mites and mould usually trace back to dirty substrate, so cleanliness is the best prevention. An exotic-pet veterinarian can advise on persistent problems.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Giant African Land Snails legal to keep?** Laws vary widely by region; in some countries, including parts of the United States, they are restricted or banned as an invasive pest. Always check local regulations before acquiring one.

**Do they need a companion?** No. They are solitary and do perfectly well alone, and keeping pairs often leads to large clutches of eggs that must be managed responsibly.

## Conclusion

With a humid, ventilated tank, plenty of fresh greens, and a steady calcium source, Giant African Land Snails are rewarding, easy-care pets. Confirm that they are legal where you live, handle them gently, and keep their home clean for a long, healthy life.
