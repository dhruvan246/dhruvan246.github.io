The Mexican Red-Knee Tarantula (*Brachypelma hamorii*) is one of the most popular pet tarantulas in the world. Known for its striking colours, calm disposition and remarkable longevity, it is widely recommended as an ideal first tarantula for responsible keepers.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Size | 5-6 inch (13-15 cm) leg span |
| Lifespan | Females 20-30 years; males 5-10 years |
| Housing | Terrestrial terrarium with deep substrate |
| Temperament | Docile, slow-moving, defensive rather than aggressive |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & behaviour

This species is instantly recognisable by its dark body contrasted with vivid orange to red "knees" on the leg joints. The carapace is tan or cream with a dark border. It is a terrestrial, burrowing spider that spends much of its time sitting at the entrance of a retreat.

Red-knees are slow and deliberate, but when threatened they prefer to flick urticating (irritating) hairs from their abdomen rather than bite. These hairs can irritate skin and eyes, so care is needed during maintenance.

## Housing & environment

A single adult needs a terrarium roughly 30 x 30 cm (12 x 12 inches), with more floor space than height since this is a ground-dwelling species. Provide 8-10 cm of dry substrate such as coconut fibre or a peat-soil mix for shallow burrowing.

Add a half-log, cork bark or plant pot as a hide. Keep temperatures around 24-28°C (75-82°F) at room temperature; supplemental heating is rarely needed. These spiders come from arid regions of western Mexico, so humidity should stay low to moderate (around 60-65%). A shallow, always-full water dish provides drinking water and gentle ambient moisture. Avoid mesh lids that risk trapped legs; a well-ventilated solid lid is safer.

## Diet & feeding

Mexican Red-Knees are insectivores. Feed appropriately sized live prey such as crickets, roaches, mealworms or locusts. As a rough guide, offer prey no larger than the spider's body. Spiderlings eat small insects twice a week, while adults need only one or two prey items every week or two.

Always remove uneaten prey within 24 hours, as crickets can stress or injure a moulting spider. Tarantulas naturally fast for weeks or even months, especially before a moult, so a refused meal is usually normal.

## Handling & care

Handling is best kept to a minimum. Although docile, a tarantula can be injured by even a short fall, and its urticating hairs or a defensive bite (mild, comparable to a bee sting) can cause discomfort. If you must move the spider, gently coax it into a container rather than picking it up.

Routine care is simple: spot-clean waste and prey remains, refill the water dish, and observe behaviour weekly. Before moulting, the spider may stop eating, darken in colour and lie on its back, which is normal and not a sign of death.

## Health

A healthy red-knee is active around its enclosure, eats periodically and moults cleanly. Common problems stem from incorrect conditions: dehydration from a dry enclosure with no water, or stress from over-handling. Never disturb a moulting spider, as the new exoskeleton is soft and easily damaged.

Watch for a "death curl" (legs tucked tightly underneath), which often signals dehydration. If you see signs of illness or a failed moult, consult an exotics veterinarian experienced with invertebrates.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Mexican Red-Knee Tarantulas dangerous?** No. Their venom is mild and medically insignificant to most people, comparable to a bee sting. The main hazard is the irritating hairs they flick when stressed.

**Is owning one legal?** *Brachypelma hamorii* is a CITES-protected species, so captive-bred animals are widely sold but wild collection is restricted. Legality and permit rules vary by region, so check local laws before purchasing.

## Conclusion

With its calm nature, low-maintenance needs and impressive lifespan, the Mexican Red-Knee Tarantula is a rewarding companion for beginners and experienced keepers alike. Provide a dry, secure enclosure, fresh water, occasional feedings and minimal handling, and this iconic spider can thrive for decades.
