The Leopard Gecko (*Eublepharis macularius*) is one of the most popular pet reptiles in the world, and for good reason. Hardy, ground-dwelling, and slow-moving, it tolerates handling well and stays small enough for a modest enclosure. These traits make it a frequent recommendation for first-time reptile keepers willing to learn proper husbandry.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 7–10 inches (including tail) |
| Lifespan | 10–20 years with good care |
| Enclosure size | 20-gallon long minimum for one adult |
| Temperament | Docile, calm, easy to tame |
| Care level | Beginner-friendly |

## Appearance & temperament

Leopard Geckos are named for the dark, leopard-like spots scattered over a yellow or cream body, though decades of selective breeding have produced many color and pattern "morphs." Unlike most geckos, they have movable eyelids and lack the adhesive toe pads that let other species climb glass, so they stay firmly on the ground. They store fat in their thick tails, which is a useful indicator of health. By nature they are crepuscular, most active around dawn and dusk, and they are generally placid and tolerant once accustomed to gentle handling.

## Habitat & enclosure

A single adult needs at least a 20-gallon long terrarium, with more floor space always being better. Floor area matters far more than height since these geckos do not climb. Provide at least three hides: one on the warm side, one on the cool side, and a humid hide (filled with damp moss or paper towel) to assist shedding. Suitable substrates include reptile carpet, tile, or paper towel; loose particulate sand is best avoided for young geckos because of the risk of accidental ingestion and impaction.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Leopard Geckos rely on belly heat to digest food, so an under-tank heater or a low-wattage overhead source controlled by a thermostat is essential. Aim for a warm basking zone of about 88–92°F (31–33°C) and a cooler end near 75°F (24°C), with a gentle nighttime drop. Always use a thermostat to prevent overheating. While they are not strongly dependent on UVB, providing a low-level UVB bulb supports vitamin D3 synthesis and overall health. Keep humidity low to moderate, roughly 30–40%, and supply the humid hide for shedding.

## Diet & feeding

These geckos are strict insectivores; they do not eat fruit or vegetables. Staple feeders include crickets, dubia roaches, and mealworms, with waxworms offered only as occasional treats due to their fat content. Feeder insects should be gut-loaded and dusted with a calcium supplement, plus a vitamin/D3 supplement on a regular schedule. Juveniles eat daily, while adults typically feed every two to three days. Provide a shallow dish of fresh water and a small dish of plain calcium at all times.

## Handling & health

Support the body and never grab or restrain the tail, which can detach as a defense mechanism and, although it regrows, looks different afterward. Handle for short sessions and let new geckos settle in for a week or two first. Common health concerns include metabolic bone disease from inadequate calcium or D3, impaction, dysecdysis (retained shed, especially on the toes), and obesity from overfeeding fatty insects. A reptile-experienced veterinarian should evaluate lethargy, weight loss, or a noticeably thinning tail.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Leopard Geckos need a UVB light?** They can survive without it if supplemented correctly, but a low-output UVB bulb is increasingly recommended to support natural vitamin D3 production and long-term wellbeing.

**Can two Leopard Geckos live together?** Two males will fight, and cohabiting often causes stress, competition for food, and injury. Keeping them individually is the safest approach for most owners.

## Conclusion

With a properly heated, appropriately sized enclosure, a varied and supplemented insect diet, and gentle handling, a Leopard Gecko can thrive for well over a decade. Their calm nature and manageable needs make them rewarding companions, though laws on keeping exotic reptiles vary by region, so check local regulations before acquiring one.
