The Madagascar hissing cockroach (*Gromphadorhina portentosa*) is one of the largest cockroach species in the world and a popular choice for first-time invertebrate keepers, classrooms, and exotic-pet enthusiasts. Native to the forest floors of Madagascar, it is wingless, slow-moving, and famous for the hissing sound it produces by forcing air through its breathing pores. These calm, hardy insects are inexpensive to house and easy to care for, making them an excellent introduction to keeping invertebrates.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |

## Appearance & behaviour

Madagascar hissing cockroaches have glossy, oval bodies in shades of brown to mahogany with dark and amber banding. Unlike many roaches, they are wingless and cannot fly. Males develop prominent bumps, called pronotal "horns," on the thorax and use them in shoving contests over territory and mates. Both sexes can hiss, but males hiss most often, using the sound to defend space, court females, and signal alarm. They are primarily nocturnal and largely silent during the day.

## Housing & environment

A 5- to 10-gallon glass tank suits a small group, with a tightly fitting lid because these roaches are surprisingly good climbers. A band of petroleum jelly around the upper inside wall helps prevent escapes. Provide 2 to 3 inches of substrate such as coconut fibre, along with bark, cardboard tubes, or egg-carton pieces for hiding. Maintain temperatures of roughly 75 to 90 degrees Fahrenheit (24 to 32 degrees Celsius) using an under-tank heater if needed, and keep humidity moderate by lightly misting one side of the enclosure.

## Diet & feeding

These cockroaches are omnivorous scavengers and accept a wide range of foods. Offer fresh vegetables and fruit such as carrots, leafy greens, apple, and squash, supplemented with dry items like oats, fish flakes, or quality dog and cat kibble for protein. Remove uneaten fresh food within a day or two to prevent mould and mites. Provide water through a shallow dish with cotton, a water-gel product, or by misting, since open water can drown them.

## Handling & care

Madagascar hissing cockroaches tolerate gentle handling well and rarely bite. Let one walk calmly across your hands rather than gripping it, and move slowly to avoid startling it into hissing or clinging tightly with its spined legs. Always wash your hands before and after handling. Spot-clean waste and old food regularly and do a full substrate change every couple of months to keep the enclosure clean and odour-free.

## Health

A properly heated, clean enclosure prevents most problems. The most common pests are tiny grain or substrate mites, which thrive in overly wet conditions and around decaying food; reducing humidity and removing leftovers usually controls them. Watch for sluggishness, which often signals temperatures that are too low. Roaches periodically moult, appearing pale and soft afterward, and should not be handled until their new shell hardens. Exotic-invertebrate ownership is restricted in some regions, so check local laws before acquiring one.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Madagascar hissing cockroaches bite or carry disease?**
They very rarely bite and are not known to spread human disease. As with any animal, simply wash your hands after handling and after cleaning the enclosure.

**Can I keep just one, or do they need company?**
A single roach will live happily alone. They are social and tolerate group housing, but mixed-sex groups breed quickly, so keep same-sex individuals if you do not want offspring.

## Conclusion

The Madagascar hissing cockroach is a hardy, fascinating, and genuinely low-maintenance pet that suits beginners and curious naturalists alike. With a warm, secure enclosure, simple omnivorous feeding, and a careful, gentle approach to handling, these long-lived insects make rewarding companions and a memorable window into the invertebrate world.
