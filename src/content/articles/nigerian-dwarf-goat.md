The Nigerian Dwarf Goat is a small, friendly dairy breed prized by hobby farmers and homesteaders for its manageable size, gentle nature, and surprisingly rich milk. Originally descended from West African stock, these compact goats thrive in backyards and small acreages where a full-sized goat would be impractical.

## Overview

| Trait | Details |
|---|---|
| Size | 17-21 in (43-53 cm) tall; 30-75 lb (14-34 kg) |
| Lifespan | 12-15 years |
| Space needs | Secure pasture or pen; minimum ~135 sq ft per goat plus shelter |
| Temperament | Friendly, curious, social, easygoing |
| Care level | Moderate; daily attention required |

## Appearance & temperament

Nigerian Dwarf Goats are miniature in stature but balanced in proportion, resembling small dairy goats rather than stocky pygmy goats. They come in a wide range of colors and patterns, including black, gold, chocolate, and white, often with striking blue eyes. Many are naturally polled or are disbudded as kids.

Temperamentally, they are calm, playful, and people-oriented. They form strong bonds with their herd mates and handlers, and bottle-raised kids in particular can become very affectionate. Their docile nature makes them popular with families and 4-H youth.

## Housing & space

These goats need a dry, draft-free shelter to escape rain, wind, and cold, along with a secure outdoor area for exercise and grazing. Fencing is critical: Nigerian Dwarfs are agile climbers and escape artists, so woven-wire or sturdy field fencing at least 4 feet high is recommended. Avoid gaps a small goat could squeeze through.

Goats are herd animals and should never be kept alone. Plan to keep at least two so they have constant companionship; a solitary goat becomes stressed, noisy, and prone to behavioral problems. Provide rocks, stumps, or sturdy platforms for climbing and play.

## Diet & nutrition

The bulk of the diet should be good-quality grass hay or browse, supplemented with fresh pasture. Goats are browsers and enjoy shrubs, weeds, and leaves more than grass alone. A loose, goat-specific mineral supplement (with adequate copper and selenium) should be available free-choice, as goat needs differ from sheep.

Grain or pelleted concentrate should be fed sparingly and mainly to pregnant or milking does. Always provide clean, fresh water. Avoid sudden diet changes, moldy feed, and toxic plants such as rhododendron, azalea, and wilted cherry leaves.

## Care & handling

Hooves grow continuously and require trimming roughly every 6-8 weeks to prevent overgrowth and lameness. Brushing keeps the coat clean and is a good bonding routine. Handle goats calmly and regularly so they accept hoof work, deworming, and vaccinations without stress.

Males not intended for breeding are best wethered (castrated), which makes them gentler pets and avoids strong odor. Intact bucks have a powerful scent and are generally not ideal as companion animals.

## Health & lifespan

With good care, Nigerian Dwarfs commonly live 12-15 years. Routine health management includes vaccination (commonly CDT for clostridial diseases and tetanus), parasite monitoring via fecal testing, and watching for signs of bloat, which is a serious digestive emergency. Wethers should be fed to prevent urinary calculi.

Establish a relationship with a livestock or goat-experienced veterinarian. Watch for off-feed behavior, isolation, scouring, or labored breathing, which warrant prompt attention.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I keep just one Nigerian Dwarf Goat?** No. Goats are highly social herd animals and a single goat will be stressed and lonely. Always keep at least two.

**Do Nigerian Dwarf Goats produce milk?** Yes. Despite their small size, does are productive dairy goats, yielding sweet, high-butterfat milk, though they must be bred to lactate.

## Conclusion

Nigerian Dwarf Goats reward attentive owners with affection, entertainment, and even milk in a compact, manageable package. With secure fencing, companionship, a forage-based diet, and routine hoof and health care, they make delightful and long-lived additions to a small farm or backyard homestead.
