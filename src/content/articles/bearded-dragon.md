The Bearded Dragon (*Pogona vitticeps*) is one of the most popular pet reptiles in the world, prized for its calm nature and curious personality. Native to the arid woodlands and deserts of central Australia, it adapts well to captive life when its specific heat, light, and dietary needs are met.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 16-24 inches (40-60 cm) including tail |
| Lifespan | 8-12 years, sometimes longer |
| Enclosure size | 40-120 gallon (minimum 4 ft long for an adult) |
| Temperament | Docile, social, tolerant of handling |
| Care level | Beginner to intermediate |

## Appearance & temperament

Bearded dragons get their name from the spiny "beard" under the throat, which they can puff out and darken when stressed, displaying, or regulating temperature. Their bodies are flattened and covered with rows of soft spines along the sides. Wild types are tan to brown, but selective breeding has produced many color "morphs." They are generally placid, alert lizards that recognize their keepers and often tolerate gentle handling well, which is a large part of their appeal.

## Habitat & enclosure

Adults need a spacious enclosure, ideally at least 4 feet (120 cm) long, as floor space matters more than height for these ground-dwelling lizards. A 40-gallon tank suits juveniles, but adults benefit from larger 75-120 gallon setups. Use a non-loose substrate such as reptile carpet, tile, or compacted excavator clay, especially for young dragons, to reduce the risk of impaction from swallowed loose particles. Provide sturdy basking rocks or branches, a hide, and visual barriers so the animal feels secure.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Proper heating and lighting are the most important parts of bearded dragon care. Create a thermal gradient with a basking spot of 95-110°F (35-43°C) at one end and a cooler zone around 75-85°F (24-29°C) at the other. Night temperatures can safely drop to the low 70s°F (around 21-24°C). A high-quality UVB tube spanning much of the enclosure is essential for vitamin D3 synthesis and calcium absorption; without it, dragons develop metabolic bone disease. Replace UVB bulbs every 6-12 months as their output fades. Keep humidity low, roughly 30-40%, to mimic their arid habitat.

## Diet & feeding

Bearded dragons are omnivores whose needs shift with age. Juveniles eat a higher proportion of insects (such as crickets, dubia roaches, and black soldier fly larvae) to fuel growth, while adults lean more toward leafy greens and vegetables like collard greens, mustard greens, squash, and bell pepper. Dust feeder insects with calcium powder and use a calcium-with-D3 or multivitamin supplement on a schedule appropriate to your lighting setup. Avoid feeding wild-caught insects and limit fatty treats like mealworms or fruit. Provide fresh water and mist greens for added moisture.

## Handling & health

Support the body fully when handling and keep sessions calm and brief at first so the dragon acclimates. Common health issues include metabolic bone disease (from poor UVB or calcium), impaction, parasites, and respiratory infections from cold or damp conditions. Signs of trouble include lethargy, weight loss, swollen limbs, sunken eyes, or refusing food. Brumation, a winter slowdown, is normal in some adults. Always wash hands after handling, as reptiles can carry *Salmonella*. Consult a reptile-experienced veterinarian for any persistent concerns.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q: Can bearded dragons live together?** Generally no. They are largely solitary and can become territorial, leading to stress, bullying, and injuries, so housing one per enclosure is safest.

**Q: Are bearded dragons legal to keep?** In many regions they are widely available and legal, but laws vary, and they are protected for export from their native Australia. Check your local regulations before acquiring one.

## Conclusion

With the correct enclosure size, a proper basking and UVB setup, and an age-appropriate omnivorous diet, a bearded dragon can be a hardy, engaging companion for a decade or more. Meeting these well-established husbandry needs is the key to a healthy, long-lived pet.
