The Gordon Setter is the largest and most substantial of the setter breeds, instantly recognizable by its glossy black coat with rich chestnut or mahogany tan markings. Developed in Scotland as a bird dog, this devoted, hard-working gundog combines stamina in the field with deep loyalty at home.

## Appearance & temperament

The Gordon Setter is a sturdy, well-balanced dog with a noble head and long, feathered ears. Its hallmark is the shining black coat marked with clearly defined tan points on the muzzle, legs, chest and over the eyes. The feathering on the ears, belly, legs and tail gives the breed an elegant outline.

In temperament, the Gordon is loyal, affectionate and famously devoted to its family, often bonding closely with one or two people. It tends to be more reserved with strangers than the flashier Irish Setter, and can be a thoughtful watchdog. Intelligent and eager but sometimes strong-willed, the breed thrives on companionship and does not do well left alone for long periods.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced diet formulated for an active, large-breed dog, choosing a life-stage appropriate food for puppies, adults or seniors. Because Gordon Setters are deep-chested, divide the daily ration into two meals rather than one large feeding, and avoid vigorous exercise immediately before and after eating. Measure portions and monitor body condition, since extra weight stresses the joints. Always provide fresh water, and adjust amounts to the dog's age, workload and metabolism.

## Exercise & training

This is an athletic working breed that needs substantial daily exercise: long walks, runs, hikes or supervised off-lead time in a secure area, plus chances to use its keen nose. A well-exercised Gordon is calm indoors, while a bored one may become restless or destructive. Mental work matters too; scent games, retrieving and canine sports suit the breed well.

Gordon Setters are smart but can be sensitive and independent, so training should be consistent, patient and reward-based. Start socialization and basic obedience early. Harsh corrections tend to backfire, while positive methods build the cooperative, confident dog this breed can be.

## Grooming & care

The medium-length coat needs brushing several times a week to prevent mats and tangles, with extra attention to the feathering behind the ears, on the legs and under the belly. Periodic trimming keeps the dog tidy. Check the long, hanging ears regularly and keep them clean and dry, as the shape can trap moisture. Routine nail trims, dental care and occasional baths round out a sensible grooming schedule.

## Health & lifespan

Gordon Setters are generally hardy and typically live around 10 to 13 years. Like many large breeds, they can be prone to hip and elbow dysplasia, and the deep chest carries a risk of bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus), a sudden emergency requiring immediate veterinary attention. The breed is also associated with certain eye conditions, including progressive retinal atrophy. Responsible breeders screen their stock, and regular veterinary check-ups, a healthy weight and good preventive care support a long, comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Gordon Setters good family dogs?** Yes. They are loyal and affectionate companions that bond strongly with their families and can do well with children, especially when raised with them, socialized early and given enough exercise.

**Do Gordon Setters need a lot of exercise?** Absolutely. As an active gundog, the Gordon needs vigorous daily activity and mental stimulation; it is best suited to owners who enjoy an outdoor, active lifestyle.

## Conclusion

The Gordon Setter is a handsome, devoted and capable companion for active households that can meet its needs for exercise, grooming and company. Reward its loyalty with consistent training, regular coat care and attentive health monitoring, and this dignified Scottish gundog will be a steadfast friend for many years.
