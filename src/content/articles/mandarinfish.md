The Mandarinfish (*Synchiropus splendidus*) is one of the most beautifully coloured fish in the marine aquarium hobby. Despite its stunning looks, it is best suited to experienced keepers because of its very specialised feeding habits.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 2.5–3 inches (6–8 cm) |
| Lifespan | 5–8 years with good care |
| Minimum tank size | 30 gallons (mature reef) |
| Water type | Saltwater (marine reef) |
| Care level | Advanced |

## Appearance & behaviour

The Mandarinfish is famous for its psychedelic pattern of swirling blue, orange, and green markings over a base of mottled colour. It is a member of the dragonet family, with a flattened body, large pelvic fins, and bulging eyes that move independently. Rather than a slimy mucus coat, it secretes a thick, bitter, foul-smelling layer over its skin that deters predators.

Mandarinfish are slow, deliberate swimmers that hover and hop along the rockwork and substrate, constantly hunting for tiny prey. They are peaceful and somewhat shy, spending much of the day picking at live rock. Males are generally larger with a longer, more pointed first dorsal spine.

## Tank setup & water parameters

The single most important requirement is a mature, well-established reef aquarium with plenty of live rock. These fish graze continuously on the microfauna that live in established rock and sand, so a tank should run for at least six to twelve months before adding one. A 30-gallon or larger tank with abundant rockwork provides the surface area needed to sustain a healthy copepod population.

Target stable parameters: temperature 72–78°F (22–26°C), salinity 1.023–1.025 specific gravity, pH 8.1–8.4, and zero ammonia and nitrite with low nitrate. A refugium helps continuously replenish copepods and amphipods. Mandarinfish are not jumpers and tolerate moderate flow.

## Diet & feeding

Diet is where most Mandarinfish are lost. In the wild they eat live copepods, amphipods, and other tiny invertebrates almost constantly throughout the day. A tank that cannot supply enough live food will lead to slow starvation, even though the fish appears to be eating.

Many specimens never accept prepared food, so a thriving pod population is essential. With patience, some can be trained onto live or frozen brine shrimp, *Mysis*, and enriched foods, but never rely on this. Seeding the tank with cultured copepods and running a refugium keeps the food supply steady. A healthy Mandarinfish has a rounded, full belly rather than a pinched, hollow look.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Mandarinfish are peaceful and do best with calm, non-competitive tank mates such as gobies, clownfish, and other gentle reef species. Avoid fast, aggressive feeders like large wrasses or damsels that will outcompete them for food and pods.

House only one male per tank, as males will fight. A male and female pair can coexist and may even spawn. They are fully reef-safe and will not bother corals or invertebrates.

## Health & common issues

The most common cause of death is starvation due to inadequate live food, which presents as a sunken belly and gradual wasting. Choose a specimen that is already plump and actively foraging. Because of their protective skin coating, Mandarinfish are quite resistant to common parasites like marine ich, but they are still vulnerable to poor water quality and bacterial infections.

Quarantine new arrivals carefully and avoid copper-based medications when possible, as these scaleless fish can be sensitive. Stable, pristine water and a steady food supply prevent most problems.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Mandarinfish hard to keep?** Yes. Their appearance is easy, but their constant need for live copepods makes them suitable only for mature reef tanks and patient, experienced aquarists.

**Can I keep two Mandarinfish together?** Only as a male-female pair, and ideally in a larger tank with ample food. Two males will fight, sometimes fatally.

## Conclusion

The Mandarinfish rewards a well-prepared aquarist with breathtaking colour and fascinating, mellow behaviour. Success hinges on a mature reef system with a self-sustaining pod population and stable water. Note that the legality and collection of marine species varies by region, so source captive-bred or sustainably collected fish where possible. Get the food supply right, and this living jewel can thrive for years.
