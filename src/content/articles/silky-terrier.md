Lively, confident and refined, the Silky Terrier is a true toy terrier that pairs a glamorous, flowing coat with the bold, busy spirit of its working ancestors. Developed in Australia by crossing Australian Terriers with Yorkshire Terriers, the breed is sometimes called the Australian Silky Terrier. It thrives as an alert, devoted companion that is small enough for apartment life yet far too feisty to be considered a lap-only pet.

## Appearance & temperament

The Silky Terrier is a compact dog, typically standing about 9-10 inches at the shoulder and weighing roughly 8-11 pounds. Its hallmark is a fine, silky single coat that lies flat and parts down the back, usually in blue and tan. Beneath the elegant coat is a wiry, low-set terrier body with pricked ears, keen dark eyes and a confident carriage.

In temperament, the Silky is bright, curious and self-assured. It forms strong bonds with its family and makes an excellent watchdog, often quick to announce visitors. True to its terrier roots, it can be vocal, determined and prone to chasing small animals.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality diet formulated for small or toy breeds, which offers appropriately sized kibble and concentrated nutrition. Because Silky Terriers are small, portion control matters: even modest overfeeding can lead to weight gain that stresses their joints. Divide the daily amount into two measured meals and limit treats. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian to tailor portions to your dog's age, weight and activity level.

## Exercise & training

Despite its size, the Silky Terrier is an active, athletic breed that needs daily exercise. A couple of brisk walks plus play sessions and some mental enrichment usually keep it satisfied. It enjoys games, puzzle toys and dog sports such as agility and earthdog activities.

Training should be consistent, positive and engaging. Silkies are intelligent and capable but also independent and occasionally stubborn, so reward-based methods work best. Early socialization helps temper their tendency to bark and chase, and teaching a reliable recall is wise given their prey drive.

## Grooming & care

The glossy coat is the breed's signature feature and requires regular care. Brush several times a week, ideally most days, to prevent tangles and mats, paying attention to areas behind the ears and under the legs. Bathe periodically to keep the coat clean and shining. Because the coat is hair-like rather than heavily shedding, many owners keep it long or opt for a trimmed style. Routine care also includes nail trimming, ear checks and regular dental hygiene, as small breeds are prone to dental issues.

## Health & lifespan

Silky Terriers are generally hardy and often live 12-15 years. Like many small breeds, they can be predisposed to certain conditions, including patellar luxation (slipping kneecaps), dental disease, Legg-Calve-Perthes disease, tracheal collapse and some eye disorders. Choosing a responsible breeder, keeping your dog at a healthy weight and scheduling routine veterinary checkups all support a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Silky Terrier the same as a Yorkshire Terrier?**
No. They are related and look similar, but the Silky is a distinct breed developed in Australia. It is generally larger and sturdier than the Yorkshire Terrier, with its own breed standard.

**Are Silky Terriers good apartment dogs?**
Yes, their small size suits apartment living, but they need daily exercise and mental stimulation. Training to manage barking is helpful, since they are alert and can be vocal.

## Conclusion

The Silky Terrier offers the best of both worlds: a beautiful, eye-catching coat and a bold, engaging terrier personality. With regular grooming, consistent training, daily activity and routine veterinary care, this spirited companion can be a loyal and entertaining family member for well over a decade.
