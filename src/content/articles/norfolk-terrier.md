The Norfolk Terrier is one of the smallest of the working terriers, a sturdy, low-set little dog with a confident attitude that far exceeds its size. Once bred in England to hunt vermin and bolt foxes, today it is a popular companion known for being friendly, plucky, and surprisingly adaptable.

## Appearance & temperament

The Norfolk Terrier is a compact, short-legged dog with a hard, wiry topcoat over a soft undercoat, typically in shades of red, wheaten, black and tan, or grizzle. Its defining feature is its drop (folded) ears, which distinguish it from the closely related Norwich Terrier, which carries prick ears. The two were considered one breed until they were separated in the mid-20th century.

In temperament, Norfolks are alert, fearless, and full of energy, yet noticeably more sociable and even-tempered than many terriers. They generally enjoy the company of people and other dogs and make cheerful, affectionate family members. Their hunting heritage means they retain a strong prey drive toward small animals.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality diet formulated for small breeds, divided into two measured meals a day. Because of their small frame, Norfolk Terriers can gain weight easily, so portion control is important. Use treats sparingly, especially during training, and keep fresh water available at all times. Choose a food appropriate to the dog's age and activity level, and consult your veterinarian if you are unsure about quantities.

## Exercise & training

Despite their small size, Norfolks are active and need regular daily exercise: brisk walks, play sessions, and opportunities to explore safely. They enjoy games and dog sports such as earthdog trials and agility, which suit their working instincts. A securely fenced yard is ideal, and they should be kept leashed in open areas because they may chase small animals.

Norfolks are intelligent and eager but can show an independent streak. Positive, reward-based training with consistency and early socialization brings out their best. They respond poorly to harsh methods.

## Grooming & care

The wiry coat needs brushing once or twice a week to prevent matting. To maintain the proper hard texture and color, the coat is traditionally hand-stripped a couple of times a year rather than clipped; clipping is acceptable for pets but softens the coat. Routine care includes regular nail trimming, dental brushing, and checking the ears for cleanliness.

## Health & lifespan

The Norfolk Terrier is generally a hardy, long-lived breed, often reaching 12 to 16 years. Like many breeds, it can be prone to certain conditions, including patellar luxation, hip dysplasia, dental issues, and some heart and eye concerns. Responsible breeders screen their stock, and routine veterinary checkups, dental care, and weight management help keep these dogs healthy.

## Frequently asked questions

**What is the difference between a Norfolk and a Norwich Terrier?**
The main visible difference is the ears: Norfolk Terriers have folded, drop ears, while Norwich Terriers have upright, pointed ears. They were once the same breed and remain very similar otherwise.

**Are Norfolk Terriers good apartment dogs?**
Yes. Their small size and adaptable nature suit apartment living, provided they receive daily exercise and mental stimulation to keep them content.

## Conclusion

The Norfolk Terrier packs a big, good-natured personality into a small, rugged body. With modest grooming, consistent training, and regular activity, this hardy little terrier makes a devoted and lively companion for individuals and families alike.
