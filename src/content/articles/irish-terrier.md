The Irish Terrier is one of the oldest terrier breeds, easily recognized by its fiery red coat, lively spirit, and deep loyalty. Often nicknamed the "Daredevil," this medium-sized dog blends courage, intelligence, and a strong devotion to its family.

## Overview

| Trait | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Size | Medium (around 25–27 lbs, 18 inches tall) |
| Lifespan | 13–15 years |
| Coat | Dense, wiry, weather-resistant single coat |
| Energy | High |
| Good with kids | Yes, with supervision |

## Appearance & temperament

The Irish Terrier has a graceful yet sturdy build, standing roughly 18 inches at the shoulder and weighing about 25 to 27 pounds. Its hallmark is a solid coat that ranges from bright red to red-wheaten or golden red, complemented by a distinctive beard and bushy eyebrows. The body is longer in leg than many terriers, giving it an athletic, racy outline.

Temperamentally, the breed is bold, animated, and affectionate. Irish Terriers are deeply devoted to their people and often described as having a sense of humor. They are alert watchdogs and can be feisty toward other dogs, but with their families they are gentle and loving. Early socialization helps temper their natural assertiveness.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed an Irish Terrier a high-quality diet formulated for active, medium-sized dogs. Most adults do well on two measured meals per day, with portions adjusted to age, weight, and activity level. Because this breed enjoys exercise but can gain weight if overfed, monitor body condition and avoid excessive treats.

Look for foods with a named animal protein as the first ingredient. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian before changing diets or adding supplements. Puppies should eat a growth-appropriate formula until maturity.

## Exercise & training

This is an energetic, athletic breed that needs daily physical and mental stimulation. Aim for at least an hour of activity through brisk walks, play sessions, and a securely fenced area where they can run. Irish Terriers excel at canine sports such as agility, obedience, and earthdog trials.

Training requires patience and consistency. These dogs are intelligent and quick to learn, but their independent, terrier nature means they respond best to positive, reward-based methods rather than harsh correction. Keep sessions engaging to hold their interest.

## Grooming & care

The wiry coat is relatively low-shedding but benefits from regular care. Brush once or twice weekly to remove loose hair and prevent matting in the beard and legs. To maintain coat texture and color, many owners hand-strip the coat a couple of times a year; clipping is an easier alternative but softens the texture.

Bathe only as needed, and trim nails regularly. Check and clean the ears, and brush the teeth several times a week to support dental health.

## Health & lifespan

Irish Terriers are generally a hardy, healthy breed with a typical lifespan of 13 to 15 years. Like all breeds, they can be prone to certain conditions, including hip issues, hyperkeratosis (corny pads), and occasional eye problems. Responsible breeders screen their stock to reduce inherited concerns.

Routine veterinary checkups, vaccinations, parasite prevention, and a healthy weight all contribute to a long, active life. Early attention to any changes in mobility or skin helps catch issues promptly.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Irish Terriers good family dogs?**
Yes. They are loyal, playful, and affectionate with their families, and generally good with children when socialized early and supervised during play.

**Do Irish Terriers shed a lot?**
No, they are considered low-shedding. Their wiry coat sheds minimally but needs regular brushing and periodic hand-stripping to stay healthy.

## Conclusion

The Irish Terrier is a spirited, devoted companion that rewards an active household with loyalty and charm. With consistent training, daily exercise, and modest grooming, this courageous redhead can be a wonderful lifelong friend.
