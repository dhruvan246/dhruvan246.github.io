The most common pet hedgehog is the African pygmy hedgehog, a small, spiny, nocturnal insectivore. Though endearing, they are exotic animals with specific needs and a reserved temperament, making them better suited to patient, quiet owners than to young children.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Size | 6-9 inches (15-23 cm), 250-600 g |
| Lifespan | 3-6 years |
| Housing | Large solid-bottom enclosure, kept warm |
| Temperament | Shy, solitary, nocturnal |
| Care level | Intermediate to advanced |

## Appearance & temperament

Hedgehogs are covered on their back and sides with stiff, non-barbed quills, while the face, belly, and legs are soft-furred. When startled they roll into a tight ball, presenting only spines. Colors range from salt-and-pepper to cinnamon and albino.

By nature they are solitary and most active at night. Many are timid at first and may huff, pop, or raise their quills when nervous. With consistent, gentle handling they relax considerably, though most remain independent rather than affectionate in the way a dog or cat is. They should be housed alone, as adults often fight if kept together.

## Housing & environment

Provide the largest enclosure you reasonably can, with a minimum floor space of roughly 4 square feet. A solid floor is essential; wire-bottom cages cause foot injuries. Suitable bedding includes paper-based litter or kiln-dried aspen shavings. Avoid cedar and pine, which can irritate the respiratory tract.

Temperature is the single most critical factor. Hedgehogs need a stable ambient range of about 72-80F (22-27C). If they get too cold they may attempt hibernation, which is dangerous and potentially fatal for captive pygmy hedgehogs. Use a thermostat-controlled ceramic heat emitter and a thermometer to monitor the cage.

Equip the habitat with a solid-surface exercise wheel (at least 11-12 inches), a hide for sleeping, and a shallow dish each for food and water. Spot-clean daily and do a full bedding change weekly.

## Diet & nutrition

Hedgehogs are insectivores that require a high-protein, moderate-fat, low-sugar diet. A quality commercial hedgehog food, or a high-protein cat food as a base, works well for many keepers. Supplement with appropriate insects such as mealworms, crickets, or dubia roaches a few times a week.

Small amounts of cooked lean meat or egg and occasional bits of vegetables or fruit can be offered as treats. Avoid grapes, raisins, avocado, onions, chocolate, and dairy. Because they are prone to obesity, measure portions and provide a wheel for nightly exercise. Fresh water should always be available.

## Handling & enrichment

Build trust slowly. Scoop the hedgehog gently from below with both hands rather than grabbing from above. Frequent short sessions help them learn your scent; a worn t-shirt placed in the cage can aid bonding. Always handle during their evening waking hours rather than disturbing daytime sleep.

For enrichment, offer a wheel, tunnels, foraging toys, and supervised floor time in a safe, enclosed area. Some owners provide a shallow warm bath occasionally, especially to clean feet.

## Health, legality & ethics

Common health concerns include obesity, dental disease, mites, respiratory infections, and a neurological condition called Wobbly Hedgehog Syndrome. A reduced appetite, weight loss, quill loss, or wobbling warrants prompt attention. See a veterinarian experienced with exotic pets, as not all general practitioners treat hedgehogs.

Legality varies significantly by region: hedgehogs are banned or restricted in some U.S. states, parts of Europe, and elsewhere. Confirm local laws before acquiring one, and buy only from a reputable breeder who screens for genetic conditions.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are hedgehogs good pets for children?** Not ideally. Their nocturnal habits, spiny defense, and need for careful, calm handling make them better suited to older, patient keepers than young children.

**Do hedgehogs need a companion?** No. They are solitary animals and are typically housed alone; adults kept together may fight, so a single hedgehog per enclosure is recommended.

## Conclusion

A pet hedgehog can be a fascinating, low-noise companion for an owner prepared to meet its needs: steady warmth, a roomy enclosure, a protein-rich diet, and gentle nighttime handling. Verify local laws, line up an exotics vet, and approach bonding with patience for a rewarding experience.
