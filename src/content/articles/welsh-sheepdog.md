The Welsh Sheepdog (Welsh: *Ci Defaid Cymreig*) is a traditional working herding dog from the hill farms of Wales. Bred for centuries to manage sheep and cattle across rugged terrain, it remains primarily a farm worker rather than a show dog, prized for its intelligence, stamina, and independent problem-solving ability.

## Appearance & temperament

The Welsh Sheepdog is a moderately built, athletic dog weighing roughly 30–45 pounds (14–20 kg). It varies more in appearance than standardised breeds, as it has been bred for function over uniformity. Coats may be black-and-white, red-and-white, tricolour, or merle, and the texture ranges from short and smooth to slightly longer and wavy. Ears can be pricked, semi-erect, or dropped.

Temperamentally, this is an alert, energetic, and highly intelligent dog with strong herding instincts. It tends to be loyal and affectionate with its family but may be reserved with strangers and naturally watchful. Without a job to do, the breed can become bored and develop unwanted behaviours.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food suited to an active medium-sized breed. Working individuals burn considerable energy and may need more calories than a pet living a quieter life, so adjust portions to the dog's actual activity level and body condition. Divide the daily amount into two meals to support steady energy. Always provide fresh water, and monitor weight regularly, since lean condition is healthier than excess weight for such an active dog. Consult your veterinarian about specific dietary needs at different life stages.

## Exercise & training

The Welsh Sheepdog is one of the most demanding breeds for exercise and mental stimulation. It thrives with a job and plenty of daily activity, ideally an hour or more of vigorous exercise plus problem-solving tasks. Herding, long walks, running, and dog sports such as agility or obedience help channel its drive.

Training should start early and use positive, consistent methods. This breed is quick to learn but independent-minded, so it responds best to fair, engaging guidance rather than repetitive drills. Early socialisation helps temper its natural wariness of strangers.

## Grooming & care

Grooming needs are modest. The weather-resistant double coat benefits from a weekly brush, increasing to more frequent sessions during seasonal shedding to remove loose hair. Bathe only when necessary to preserve natural oils. Check and clean the ears regularly, trim nails as needed, and maintain a routine of dental care. As an outdoor working breed, it generally copes well with varied weather but still needs shelter, fresh water, and protection from extreme conditions.

## Health & lifespan

The Welsh Sheepdog is generally a robust, hardy breed with few exaggerated features, and many live healthy lives of 12–15 years. As with many herding dogs, responsible breeders screen for conditions such as hip dysplasia and eye disorders. Merle-coloured dogs carry a risk of associated hearing or sight issues when bred carelessly. Routine veterinary check-ups, parasite control, vaccination, and a sensible diet support a long, active life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Welsh Sheepdog the same as the Border Collie?**
No. Although both are British herding breeds, the Welsh Sheepdog is a distinct, less standardised landrace traditionally used on Welsh farms, with a different herding style and more varied appearance.

**Does the Welsh Sheepdog make a good pet?**
It can, but only for active owners. Its high energy, intelligence, and strong working drive mean it needs ample exercise, mental stimulation, and ideally a job to stay happy and well-behaved.

## Conclusion

The Welsh Sheepdog is a capable, intelligent, and hardworking herding breed best suited to homes that can match its considerable energy and need for purpose. With proper exercise, training, and care, it makes a devoted and impressive companion for an active household or working farm.
