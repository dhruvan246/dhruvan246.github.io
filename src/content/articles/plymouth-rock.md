The Plymouth Rock is one of America's most popular backyard chicken breeds, prized for its calm nature, cold hardiness, and steady supply of brown eggs. Developed in New England in the 19th century, it is a true dual-purpose bird, raised for both eggs and meat. The Barred Plymouth Rock, with its black-and-white striped feathers, is the most recognizable variety.

## Overview

| Trait | Details |
|---|---|
| Size | Hens around 6.5–7.5 lbs; roosters 7.5–9.5 lbs |
| Lifespan | 8–10 years (sometimes longer) |
| Space needs | 4 sq ft coop + 8–10 sq ft run per bird |
| Temperament | Docile, friendly, calm |

## Appearance & temperament

Plymouth Rocks are medium-to-large birds with a broad, full body, a single red comb, and clean yellow legs. While the Barred variety is iconic, the breed also comes in White, Buff, Partridge, Silver Penciled, and other colors. Their feathers are loose and soft, giving them a plump look.

Temperament is a key selling point. These chickens are gentle, curious, and tolerant of handling, which makes them excellent for families and first-time keepers. Hens rarely turn aggressive, and the breed generally sits near the bottom of pecking-order squabbles. Roosters tend to be more mellow than many other breeds, though individual personalities vary.

## Housing & space

Provide a secure, predator-proof coop with roughly 4 square feet of floor space per bird and a covered run offering at least 8–10 square feet each. Plymouth Rocks tolerate confinement reasonably well but are happiest when allowed to free-range or roam a spacious run. Include sturdy roosting bars set a foot or two off the ground, since they like to perch at night.

Supply one nesting box per three to four hens, lined with clean straw or shavings. Good ventilation near the roofline keeps the air dry without creating cold drafts. Their dense feathering makes them very cold-hardy, but they need shade and fresh water in hot weather to avoid heat stress.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed laying hens a complete layer ration containing about 16–18% protein, which supplies the calcium needed for strong eggshells. Chicks start on a higher-protein starter feed before transitioning. Always offer free-choice grit to aid digestion and a separate dish of crushed oyster shell so hens can top up calcium as needed.

Fresh, clean water must be available at all times. Kitchen scraps, leafy greens, and mealworms make good occasional treats, but keep treats to under 10% of the diet so the balanced feed remains the foundation. Avoid feeding moldy food, avocado, or anything salty.

## Care & handling

Plymouth Rocks are low-maintenance. Refresh bedding regularly, clean waterers, and do a quick daily health check. Their docile nature means most birds tolerate gentle pick-ups; support the body and keep wings secured. Provide a dry dust-bathing area, which helps them control mites and lice naturally.

## Health & lifespan

This is a robust, long-lived breed with few inherited problems. Routine concerns are the same as for most chickens: external parasites, intestinal worms, and respiratory infections. Watch for scaly leg mites and keep the coop clean and dry to reduce disease risk. Hens typically lay around 200 or more brown eggs per year, slowing with age. Consult a poultry-savvy veterinarian if a bird shows lethargy, labored breathing, or sudden drops in laying.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Plymouth Rocks good for beginners?** Yes. Their calm temperament, hardiness, and reliable egg production make them one of the best starter breeds for new backyard chicken keepers.

**How many eggs do they lay?** A healthy hen lays roughly 200–250 large brown eggs per year, generally most consistently in her first two or three laying seasons.

## Conclusion

The Plymouth Rock combines friendly temperament, cold hardiness, and dependable egg laying in one easygoing package. With a secure coop, balanced layer feed, and basic routine care, this classic American breed rewards keepers of all experience levels with years of companionship and fresh eggs.
