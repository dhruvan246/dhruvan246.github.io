The Nova Scotia Duck Tolling Retriever, affectionately called the "Toller," is the smallest of the retrievers. Developed in the Little River area of Nova Scotia, Canada, this breed was bred to "toll," or lure, waterfowl within gun range by playing along the shoreline before retrieving downed birds. The result is a compact, athletic, intensely driven dog that blends a working spirit with strong devotion to family.

## Appearance & temperament

The Toller is a well-muscled, medium-sized retriever with a fox-like appearance and an alert expression. The coat is some shade of red or orange, usually with white markings on the chest, feet, tail tip, or face. A water-repellent double coat with feathering on the legs, chest, and tail suits the breed for cold-water work.

Tollers are intelligent, affectionate, and energetic. They form close bonds with their people and tend to be reserved with strangers rather than openly outgoing. Many are vocal, producing a distinctive high-pitched "Toller scream" when excited. They thrive when given a job and plenty of mental engagement.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food appropriate for the Toller's age, weight, and activity level. Active working dogs may need more calories than companion pets, so portions should be adjusted to keep the dog lean. Because the breed is athletic and food-motivated, watch for excess weight gain, which strains joints. Divide daily food into measured meals, provide fresh water at all times, and consult your veterinarian for guidance on the right diet and any supplements.

## Exercise & training

This is a high-energy breed that needs substantial daily exercise — brisk walks, running, fetch, and especially swimming, which Tollers usually love. Without enough physical and mental outlet, they can become bored and develop unwanted behaviors.

Tollers are quick learners and excel in activities such as obedience, agility, dock diving, and field work. They respond best to positive, reward-based training and consistent, patient handling. Early socialization helps the naturally reserved Toller become a confident, well-mannered adult.

## Grooming & care

The double coat needs regular brushing, typically a few times a week, to manage loose hair and prevent matting, with heavier shedding during seasonal coat changes. Bathe only as needed to preserve the coat's natural water resistance. Routine care includes regular nail trimming, dental brushing, and checking the ears for signs of irritation, especially since these water-loving dogs often get their ears wet.

## Health & lifespan

Tollers are generally healthy and often live around 12 to 14 years. Like many breeds, they can be predisposed to certain conditions, including hip and elbow concerns, eye disorders such as progressive retinal atrophy, and some autoimmune and inherited conditions seen in the breed. Reputable breeders screen breeding dogs through recommended health testing. Routine veterinary checkups, parasite prevention, dental care, and weight management support a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Nova Scotia Duck Tolling Retrievers good family dogs?**
Yes. Tollers are devoted, affectionate companions that generally do well with respectful children, provided they receive enough exercise, training, and attention. They tend to be reserved with strangers.

**Do Tollers need a lot of exercise?**
Absolutely. They are high-energy working dogs that need daily vigorous activity plus mental stimulation. They are happiest with active families who can provide running, swimming, fetch, or dog sports.

## Conclusion

The Nova Scotia Duck Tolling Retriever is a bright, energetic, and loyal companion for active households. With consistent training, ample exercise, regular grooming, and routine veterinary care, a well-matched Toller can be a joyful, devoted family member for many years.
