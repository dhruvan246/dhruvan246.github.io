The degu (*Octodon degus*) is a small, intelligent rodent native to central Chile. Closely related to chinchillas and guinea pigs, degus are highly social, active during the day, and form strong bonds with their cage mates and keepers. They make rewarding pets for people prepared to meet their fairly demanding social and dietary needs.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Size | 25-31 cm including tail; 170-300 g |
| Lifespan | 6-8 years (occasionally longer) |
| Housing | Large multi-level wire cage, kept in pairs or groups |
| Temperament | Social, curious, active, vocal |

## Appearance & temperament

Degus have stocky bodies covered in agouti-brown fur, a paler underside, and a slim tail tipped with a black tuft. Their large eyes and rounded ears suit a diurnal, alert lifestyle. They are remarkably social animals that live in colonies in the wild and communicate through an extensive range of chirps, squeaks, and warbles. Kept alone, a degu becomes stressed and lonely, so they should always be housed with at least one companion of the same sex. With patient daily handling they become tame, confident, and interactive.

## Housing & environment

Provide the largest cage you can, ideally a tall multi-level wire enclosure with a solid (not wire) floor to protect their feet. Horizontal bar spacing should be no more than about 1.5 cm to prevent escapes. Furnish with wooden ledges, tunnels, ropes, and a large solid-surface exercise wheel at least 28-30 cm across. Use a paper-based or aspen substrate, and avoid pine or cedar shavings. Degus love to gnaw, so offer plenty of safe wooden chews and avoid plastic furnishings. Keep the cage out of direct sun and away from draughts, ideally at a steady 15-20°C, as degus overheat easily.

## Diet & nutrition

This is the most critical aspect of degu care. Degus are highly prone to diabetes and cannot process sugar, so their diet must be strictly low in sugar and starch. Base the diet on unlimited grass hay and a measured portion of plain, sugar-free chinchilla or degu pellets. Supplement with small amounts of fresh leafy greens and herbs. Never feed fruit, carrots, sweetcorn, dried fruit, nuts, or commercial treats containing molasses or honey. Provide constant access to fresh water through a bottle.

## Handling & enrichment

Degus are intelligent and need daily mental and physical stimulation. Never pick a degu up by its tail, as the skin can slough off and the tail will not regrow. Instead, scoop gently with both hands. Offer supervised out-of-cage time in a secure, chew-proofed area, plus foraging toys, tunnels, and dig boxes. They also require regular dust baths, using chinchilla dust offered for a few minutes several times a week, to keep their coat clean and healthy.

## Health & lifespan

Well-cared-for degus live around 6 to 8 years. The most common health problems stem from diet: diabetes (often shown by cataracts and excessive drinking) and dental disease, since their teeth grow continuously and need constant wear from hay and chewing. Watch for weight loss, runny eyes, overgrown teeth, or changes in drinking. Always seek an exotics-experienced vet, as degus are unusual patients. Legality and availability vary by region, so check local exotic-pet rules before acquiring one.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can degus be kept alone?** No. Degus are intensely social and suffer when isolated. Always keep at least two of the same sex, ideally littermates introduced young.

**Why can't degus eat fruit or sugary food?** Degus are extremely prone to diabetes and process sugar poorly. Sugary foods can trigger serious illness, so the diet must stay sugar-free.

## Conclusion

Degus are lively, affectionate, and endlessly entertaining companions for keepers willing to commit to their social and dietary needs. Provide company, a spacious gnaw-proof home, a strict sugar-free diet, and regular dust baths, and a degu will reward you with years of curious, interactive personality.
