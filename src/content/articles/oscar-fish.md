The Oscar (*Astronotus ocellatus*) is a large, intelligent South American cichlid native to the Amazon basin. Famous for recognising their owners and "begging" at the glass, Oscars have personalities that rival many pets. They are rewarding but demanding, requiring big tanks, strong filtration, and committed long-term care.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | Typically 10-12 inches (25-30 cm) |
| Lifespan | Around 10-15 years with good care |
| Minimum tank size | 55 gallons for one; 75+ gallons recommended |
| Water type | Freshwater, tropical |
| Care level | Intermediate |

## Appearance & behaviour

Oscars have a robust, oval body with a large head and mouth. Wild fish are dark with orange or red marbling, while selective breeding has produced popular varieties such as Tiger Oscars, Red Oscars, Albino, and Lutino forms. A distinctive "eyespot" near the tail is thought to confuse predators.

Behaviourally, Oscars are bold, curious, and surprisingly interactive. They learn feeding routines, follow movement outside the tank, and may rearrange decor or dig substrate. They can be territorial and aggressive, especially as they mature or during breeding.

## Tank setup & water parameters

Because of their size and waste output, Oscars need spacious tanks with powerful filtration. A single adult needs at least 55 gallons, with 75 gallons or more strongly preferred; pairs or groups require much larger setups.

Aim for these parameters:

- **Temperature:** 74-81°F (23-27°C)
- **pH:** 6.0-8.0, with neutral values ideal
- **Hardness:** soft to moderately hard

Use a secure lid, as Oscars can be startled and jump. Provide smooth rocks, driftwood, and sturdy decor anchored firmly. Avoid sharp objects. Choose a fine sand or smooth gravel substrate, since Oscars love to dig. Weekly water changes of 25-50% are essential to control nitrate from their heavy bioload.

## Diet & feeding

Oscars are opportunistic omnivores that lean carnivorous. A balanced diet built around high-quality cichlid pellets provides core nutrition. Supplement with frozen or fresh foods such as shrimp, earthworms, mussels, and occasional insects.

Avoid feeding live "feeder fish," which carry disease risk and offer poor nutrition. Skip mammalian meats like beef heart, which are hard to digest. Feed juveniles a couple of times daily and adults once daily, offering only what they finish in a few minutes. Overfeeding pollutes water quickly.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Oscars are best kept alone or with carefully chosen large, robust companions. Small fish will be eaten, and aggressive species may fight. Possible tank mates in very large aquariums include other large cichlids (such as Severums or Jack Dempseys), Silver Dollars, large plecos, and Bichirs, but compatibility varies by individual temperament.

Always provide ample space and hiding spots, monitor closely, and be ready to separate fish. Many keepers find a single Oscar in a species-only tank is the simplest, safest choice.

## Health & common issues

The most common Oscar ailment is "hole-in-the-head" disease (head and lateral line erosion), often linked to poor water quality, nutritional gaps, or stress. Maintaining pristine water and a varied diet is the best prevention. Oscars are also prone to ich, fin rot, and bloat, which are usually water-quality related.

Watch for clamped fins, loss of appetite, or unusual spots. Quarantine new fish and address problems early. For persistent illness, consult an aquatic veterinarian rather than guessing at treatments.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I keep two Oscars together?** Yes, but only in a large tank (often 100+ gallons). Pairs can bond or clash, so provide space, structure, and be prepared to separate them if aggression escalates.

**Do Oscars really recognise their owners?** Many keepers report that Oscars learn to recognise people and respond to them at feeding time. They are among the more intelligent and interactive aquarium fish.

## Conclusion

Oscars are charismatic, long-lived fish that reward dedicated keepers with genuine personality. Success comes down to a big tank, strong filtration, consistent water changes, and a varied diet. Note that local rules on keeping large fish vary by region. With proper commitment, an Oscar can be a centrepiece companion for over a decade.
