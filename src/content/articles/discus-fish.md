The Discus (*Symphysodon* species) is a round, disc-shaped cichlid native to the slow-moving waters of the Amazon River basin. Prized for its vivid colours and graceful movement, it is often called the "king of the aquarium." Discus are rewarding but demanding fish best suited to aquarists who enjoy attentive, hands-on care.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| Adult size | Around 5-6 inches (12-15 cm) in diameter |
| Lifespan | Typically 10-15 years with good care |
| Minimum tank size | About 50-55 gallons for a small group |
| Water type | Warm, soft, acidic freshwater |
| Care level | Intermediate to advanced |

## Appearance & behaviour

Discus have a distinctive laterally compressed, near-circular body that sets them apart from most aquarium fish. Wild forms show browns, blues, greens, and reds, while captive-bred strains come in an enormous range of patterns and colours. They are social, schooling cichlids that feel most secure in groups of five or more. Despite belonging to a family known for aggression, Discus are relatively peaceful and can be shy, especially in sparse or busy tanks. They establish gentle pecking orders and may pair off when breeding.

## Tank setup & water parameters

Discus need a tall, spacious tank with calm water and plenty of cover. Aim for a temperature of roughly 82-86°F (28-30°C), which is warmer than most community fish prefer. Keep the pH slightly acidic, around 6.0-7.0, with soft to moderately soft water. Stable, pristine conditions matter enormously: ammonia and nitrite must read zero, and nitrates should be kept low through frequent partial water changes. Provide gentle filtration, subdued lighting, driftwood, and tall plants or smooth vertical surfaces. Many keepers use a bare-bottom tank to simplify the regular cleaning Discus require.

## Diet & feeding

Discus are omnivores with a hearty appetite and benefit from variety. Offer high-quality flakes or pellets formulated for cichlids alongside frozen or live foods such as bloodworms, brine shrimp, and blackworms. Many breeders feed beef-heart based mixes, though these should be used carefully to avoid fouling the water. Feed small amounts two to three times daily for adults, and more often for growing juveniles. Remove uneaten food promptly, as leftover waste quickly degrades the clean conditions these fish depend on.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Because of their warm-water and calm temperament, Discus pair best with peaceful, heat-tolerant species. Suitable companions include cardinal and rummy-nose tetras, corydoras catfish, and other gentle, similarly sized fish. Avoid fin-nippers, boisterous species, and anything that cannot tolerate the high temperatures Discus need. A species-only tank or a small Discus group is often the safest and most attractive option.

## Health & common issues

Discus are sensitive to poor water quality and stress, which can trigger problems such as bloating, loss of appetite, darkening colour, and clamped fins. Internal parasites and bacterial infections are not uncommon, so quarantining new arrivals is wise. The single best preventive measure is consistent, excellent water management. If a fish shows persistent signs of illness, consult an aquatic veterinarian or experienced fishkeeper rather than guessing at treatments.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Discus good fish for beginners?**
Not usually. Their need for warm, pristine, stable water and frequent maintenance makes them better suited to aquarists with some experience.

**Why should Discus be kept in groups?**
Discus are social and feel stressed in isolation. A group of five or more spreads out mild social tension and encourages natural, confident behaviour.

## Conclusion

Discus reward dedicated keepers with stunning colour and engaging personalities. With a roomy tank, warm and immaculate water, a varied diet, and the right companions, these Amazonian beauties can thrive for well over a decade. Availability and regulations vary by region, so source captive-bred stock responsibly and be ready to provide the specialist care they deserve.
