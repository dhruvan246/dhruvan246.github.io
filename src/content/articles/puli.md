Instantly recognisable by its long, naturally corded coat, the Puli is a nimble, intelligent herding dog from Hungary. Bred for centuries to move and guard flocks of sheep, this compact "mop dog" combines remarkable agility with a devoted, watchful nature.

## Appearance & temperament

The Puli is a square, medium-sized dog whose most striking feature is its weatherproof double coat, which forms tight cords as it matures. Coat colours include black, various shades of grey, and white, with black being the most familiar. Beneath all that hair is a lean, athletic body built for sudden turns and bursts of speed.

In character the Puli is energetic, smart and intensely loyal to its family. As a working herder it is naturally alert and makes an excellent watchdog, often reserved with strangers. This is a thinking dog that bonds closely with its people and does best when given a job and plenty of interaction.

## Diet & nutrition

A Puli does well on a complete, good-quality diet matched to its age, size and activity level.

- Measure meals and feed to maintain a lean, athletic build.
- Adjust portions for highly active or working dogs versus more sedentary pets.
- Because the dense coat can hide weight changes, check body condition by feel, not just sight.
- Count training treats within the daily food allowance to avoid excess calories.

## Exercise & training

The Puli is an active breed that needs at least an hour of daily exercise plus regular mental challenges. Walks, play, herding games and dog sports such as agility suit its quick, athletic nature well. Without enough stimulation, a bored Puli can become noisy or develop unwanted habits.

These dogs are highly intelligent and learn quickly, but their independent, problem-solving streak means training should be consistent, positive and engaging. Early, thorough socialisation is important to temper their natural wariness of strangers and to raise a confident, well-mannered adult.

## Grooming & care

The corded coat is distinctive but demands real commitment.

- As the puppy coat changes, the dense fur must be separated regularly by hand into individual cords to prevent matting.
- Once established, cords need occasional separating rather than brushing.
- Bathing and, especially, thorough drying take considerable time, as wet cords hold moisture for a long while.
- Keep up routine nail trims, dental care, and check the ears and eye area regularly.

## Health & lifespan

The Puli is generally a hardy, long-lived breed, often reaching the mid-teens. As with many breeds, responsible owners should be aware of a few possible concerns, including hip dysplasia, eye conditions such as cataracts and progressive retinal atrophy, and occasional issues like deafness. Keeping your dog at a healthy weight, providing regular exercise and maintaining routine veterinary check-ups and parasite prevention all support a long, active life. Always consult your veterinarian about any health concerns rather than self-treating.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Pulik shed a lot?**
The corded coat sheds very little, trapping loose hair within the cords instead of dropping it. However, this means the coat needs regular maintenance to stay clean and matt-free.

**Is the Puli a good first dog?**
It can be, for an active owner prepared for its grooming needs and mental energy. The breed's intelligence and independence suit people who enjoy training and staying engaged with their dog.

## Conclusion

The Puli is a spirited, clever and loyal herding dog wrapped in an unmistakable corded coat. Give one ample exercise, plenty of mental stimulation, early socialisation and patient coat care, and you will gain an agile, devoted companion well suited to an active home.
