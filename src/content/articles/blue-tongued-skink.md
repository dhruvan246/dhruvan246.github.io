The blue-tongued skink (genus *Tiliqua*) is one of the most popular pet lizards, prized for its calm nature, expressive face, and the bright blue tongue it flashes to startle predators. Native to Australia, Indonesia, and New Guinea, these stout, short-legged reptiles are hardy and personable, making them a great choice for beginners willing to provide adequate space and a varied diet.

## Overview

| Trait | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Adult size | 18–24 inches (45–60 cm) |
| Lifespan | 15–20 years (sometimes longer) |
| Enclosure size | 4 x 2 x 1.5 ft (120 gallons or larger) |
| Temperament | Docile, curious, easily tamed |
| Care level | Beginner to intermediate |

## Appearance & temperament

Blue-tongued skinks have a heavy, elongated body, a triangular head, and small limbs that give them a slow, deliberate gait. Coloration varies by species and locality, ranging from banded browns and grays to brighter orange and tan tones. Their signature feature is the cobalt-blue tongue, which they display defensively. Most captive-bred individuals become remarkably tame, tolerating regular handling and even appearing to recognize their keepers at feeding time.

## Habitat & enclosure

These are terrestrial, ground-dwelling lizards that need floor space far more than height. An adult should have an enclosure of at least 4 x 2 feet, with a footprint of 8 square feet or more being ideal. Provide a thick layer of loose substrate such as cypress mulch, aspen, or a soil mix, allowing 3–4 inches for burrowing. Include hides on both the warm and cool ends, a sturdy water bowl, and flat objects for security. Wood and plastic enclosures hold heat and humidity better than open screen tanks.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Set up a thermal gradient: a basking spot of 95–105°F (35–40°C) at one end and a cool side around 75–80°F (24–27°C). A nighttime drop into the low 70s°F is acceptable. Use an overhead halogen or basking bulb rather than heat rocks, which can cause burns. Provide a UVB tube spanning part of the enclosure to support calcium metabolism and prevent metabolic bone disease. Humidity needs depend on species: Australian skinks prefer 25–40%, while Indonesian skinks need higher levels around 60–80%. Always research your specific subspecies.

## Diet & feeding

Blue-tongued skinks are omnivores with a roughly 50/50 plant-to-protein balance in adulthood. Offer leafy greens (collards, dandelion, endive), vegetables, and a smaller portion of fruit. Protein sources include high-quality wet dog or cat food, cooked egg, insects, and the occasional pinky mouse for larger species. Dust meals with calcium and a reptile multivitamin. Juveniles eat more protein and feed every 1–2 days, while adults eat 2–3 times weekly. Avoid feeding too much fatty protein, which leads to obesity.

## Handling & health

These skinks tolerate handling well once acclimated. Support the body fully and avoid grabbing the tail, which some species can drop. Handle for short, regular sessions to build trust. Common health issues include obesity, mites, respiratory infections from cold or damp conditions, and metabolic bone disease from inadequate UVB or calcium. Signs of trouble include lethargy, wheezing, swollen joints, or refusing food. A reptile-experienced veterinarian should evaluate any persistent symptoms.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are blue-tongued skinks good for beginners?** Yes. Their docile temperament, hardiness, and tolerance of handling make them one of the best starter lizards, provided you supply proper space, heat, UVB, and a varied diet.

**Do I need a permit to keep one?** It depends on your region. Some species are heavily regulated, especially Australian natives, and legality varies by country, state, and locality. Always check local exotic-pet laws before acquiring one.

## Conclusion

A blue-tongued skink rewards thoughtful care with years of companionship and personality. Give it a spacious ground-level enclosure, a proper heat and UVB setup, humidity matched to its species, and a balanced omnivorous diet, and you'll have a calm, engaging reptile that often becomes a favorite household pet.
