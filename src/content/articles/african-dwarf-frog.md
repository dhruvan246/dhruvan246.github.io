The African Dwarf Frog (genus *Hymenochirus*) is a small, fully aquatic amphibian from the freshwater streams and pools of Central Africa. Hardy, peaceful, and endlessly entertaining as they dart to the surface for air, they have become a popular choice for community aquariums and dedicated frog tanks alike.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Size | 1–1.5 inches (2.5–4 cm) |
| Lifespan | 5–10 years with good care |
| Enclosure | Aquarium of 5+ gallons, fully aquatic |
| Temperament | Peaceful, social, mostly active at dusk |
| Care level | Beginner–intermediate |

## Appearance & behaviour

African Dwarf Frogs have flattened bodies, webbed front and back feet, and small eyes set on the sides of the head. They range from olive-green to brownish-grey, usually with dark spots. They are easily confused with the larger, more aggressive African Clawed Frog, but ADFs have webbed front feet and a slimmer build.

These frogs are social and often do best in small groups of two or more. They are most active in the evening, swimming in bursts, hovering mid-water in the "zen" floating pose, and surfacing regularly to gulp air, since they breathe with lungs.

## Habitat & water

Because they are fully aquatic, ADFs need a stable, well-cycled aquarium rather than a land setup. Provide a minimum of 5 gallons for a pair, with a secure lid, as they can occasionally jump or escape through small gaps. Keep water shallow enough that they can reach the surface easily.

Aim for a temperature of 72–78°F (22–26°C), a pH of 6.5–7.8, and zero ammonia and nitrite. A gentle filter is ideal, as strong currents stress these weak swimmers. Add live or silk plants, smooth gravel or sand, and caves or driftwood for hiding. Perform regular partial water changes to maintain quality.

## Diet & feeding

African Dwarf Frogs are carnivores with poor eyesight, so they hunt by smell and touch. Offer sinking foods such as frozen or live bloodworms, brine shrimp, daphnia, and quality sinking frog or carnivore pellets. Feed adults every other day, giving only what they finish in a few minutes.

Because they are slow eaters, take care that faster tankmates do not steal their food. Target-feeding with tongs or a turkey baster placed near the frog helps ensure they get enough. Remove uneaten food to protect water quality.

## Handling & health

ADFs should not be handled. Their delicate, permeable skin absorbs oils and contaminants, and they breathe partly through it. Always wash and rinse your hands before any necessary contact, and never remove them from water for long.

Common health issues include fungal infections (cottony patches), bloat or dropsy (swelling from fluid retention), and "red leg." Most problems trace back to poor water quality, so prevention through clean, stable water is key. Consult an exotics or aquatic veterinarian for persistent illness.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can African Dwarf Frogs live with fish?**
Yes, they generally do well with small, peaceful community fish like tetras and guppies. Avoid large, aggressive, or fin-nipping species and any fish that may outcompete the frogs for food.

**Do African Dwarf Frogs need a heater?**
Usually yes. They thrive in tropical temperatures around 72–78°F (22–26°C), so a small aquarium heater keeps the water stable in most homes, especially during cooler months.

## Conclusion

African Dwarf Frogs are charming, low-maintenance amphibians that reward stable water and a calm environment with years of quirky, active behaviour. With a properly cycled tank, gentle filtration, and a varied carnivorous diet, these little frogs make a delightful addition for both new and experienced aquarists. Always check local regulations, as keeping exotic amphibians can vary by region.
