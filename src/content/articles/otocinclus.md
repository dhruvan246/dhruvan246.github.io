The Otocinclus, often called the "oto" or dwarf sucker, is a tiny South American catfish prized by aquarists for its gentle nature and appetite for algae. Despite their small size, these fish have specific needs that catch out many beginners. With a mature, well-planted tank and a reliable food supply, otos make rewarding, hard-working community residents.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Adult size | 1.5–2 inches (4–5 cm) |
| Lifespan | 3–5 years |
| Minimum tank size | 20 gallons (76 L) |
| Water type | Freshwater |
| Care level | Intermediate |

## Appearance & behaviour

Otocinclus are slender, torpedo-shaped fish with a flattened underside and a sucker-like mouth used to graze surfaces. Most species show a dark horizontal stripe running from the snout to the tail along a tan or olive body, with a mottled belly. Several species are sold under the generic "oto" name, and they look very similar.

These are shy, peaceful, social fish that should be kept in groups of six or more. In a comfortable tank they spend the day calmly rasping algae from glass, plants, and decor. Otos kept singly or in pairs often become stressed and reclusive.

## Tank setup & water parameters

Otos thrive in established aquariums with a steady film of natural algae and biofilm; brand-new tanks rarely have enough to sustain them, which is the most common cause of early loss. Provide live plants, smooth driftwood, and rocks that grow grazing surfaces. Gentle to moderate flow and good oxygenation suit them well.

Target these conditions:

- Temperature: 72–79°F (22–26°C)
- pH: 6.5–7.5
- Hardness: soft to moderately hard
- Ammonia and nitrite: 0 ppm; nitrate kept low

Otos are very sensitive to poor water quality and to swings during acclimation, so add them only to a cycled, stable tank and acclimate slowly.

## Diet & feeding

Otocinclus are dedicated herbivores that feed on soft green algae, diatoms, and biofilm. Even a clean-looking tank may not produce enough to feed a group, so supplement regularly. Offer blanched vegetables such as zucchini, cucumber, or spinach, plus quality algae wafers and spirulina-based foods.

Watch their bellies: a healthy oto has a gently rounded abdomen, while a sunken, pinched belly signals starvation. Many otos die slowly from lack of food in tanks that simply do not have enough algae, so err toward feeding.

## Tank mates & compatibility

Their peaceful temperament makes otos excellent community fish. They pair well with small tetras, rasboras, danios, peaceful livebearers, dwarf shrimp, and snails. Always keep them in a shoal of their own kind for security.

Avoid large, aggressive, or boisterous tank mates such as cichlids and big barbs that may bully or eat them. Fast, greedy feeders can also outcompete slow-grazing otos, leaving them undernourished.

## Health & common issues

The greatest risks to otos are starvation and the stress of being introduced to immature tanks. Many fish arrive at stores already weakened and underfed, so choosing plump, active individuals improves survival odds. Quarantine new arrivals when possible.

Like most scaleless and small catfish, otos are sensitive to medications and to copper, so treat cautiously and follow product guidance. Maintain pristine water, stable parameters, and a constant food source to prevent most problems. Consult an aquatic veterinarian for persistent illness.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I keep just one Otocinclus?** No. Otos are social and become stressed when alone. Keep a group of at least six so they feel secure and behave naturally.

**Will Otocinclus eat all my algae?** They love soft green algae and diatoms but ignore tough black-beard or hair algae. They help control algae rather than eliminate every type.

## Conclusion

Otocinclus are charming, industrious little catfish that reward patient keepers. Give them a mature, planted tank, a steady supply of algae and supplemental greens, and a few companions of their own kind, and they will graze contentedly for years. Their sensitivity makes them better suited to aquarists who can provide stable, established conditions.
