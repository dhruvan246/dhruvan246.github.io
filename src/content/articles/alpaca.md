The alpaca is a domesticated South American camelid prized for its soft fleece and calm, curious nature. Originally bred in the Andes, alpacas have become popular smallholding and hobby-farm animals across the world. They are gentle, social, and relatively low-maintenance compared with many livestock, but they have specific needs that owners must meet.

## Overview

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Size | 32-39 in at the withers; 100-185 lb |
| Lifespan | 15-20 years |
| Space needs | Roughly 5-10 alpacas per acre of quality pasture |
| Temperament | Gentle, social, curious, generally docile |
| Care level | Moderate; requires herd companionship |

## Appearance & temperament

Alpacas come in two breeds: the Huacaya, with dense, crimped, fluffy fleece, and the rarer Suri, with long, silky, dreadlock-like locks. Their fleece grows in over 20 natural colors, from white and fawn to brown, grey, and black. Alpacas are smaller than their llama cousins, with shorter ears and a more rounded face.

By nature they are timid and herd-oriented, preferring to keep a little distance from people while remaining alert and inquisitive. They hum to communicate and may spit when stressed or competing over food, though spitting at humans is uncommon. With patient handling they become accustomed to their keepers.

## Housing & space

Alpacas need access to grazing pasture along with a simple three-sided shelter or barn for shade, wind, and rain protection. Secure fencing roughly 4-5 feet high keeps them safe from dogs and other predators, which are a greater threat than escape since alpacas rarely challenge fences.

Crucially, alpacas must never be kept alone. They are herd animals and a single alpaca will become stressed and depressed, so keep at least two, and ideally three or more. They tolerate cold well thanks to their fleece but need shade and water in hot weather to avoid heat stress.

## Diet & nutrition

The bulk of an alpaca's diet is grass and good-quality grass hay. As efficient grazers with a three-compartment stomach, they digest forage well and need only modest supplementation. A vitamin and mineral supplement formulated for camelids is often recommended, especially where soil is low in certain minerals.

Provide constant access to clean, fresh water. Grain should be limited, as overfeeding leads to obesity and health problems. Avoid feeding alpacas anything toxic to livestock, and introduce any dietary changes gradually.

## Care & handling

Alpacas require shearing once a year, typically in spring, to keep them comfortable and to harvest their fleece. Their toenails need regular trimming, and their teeth may occasionally require attention. Routine care also includes parasite monitoring and vaccinations as advised locally.

Handle alpacas calmly and quietly. They dislike being grabbed but generally accept being led with a halter once trained. A useful habit they form is using a communal dung pile, which makes pasture cleanup and parasite control easier.

## Health & lifespan

With good care, alpacas commonly live 15-20 years. Common health concerns include internal and external parasites, heat stress, and obesity from overfeeding. In some regions the meningeal worm, carried by deer, poses a serious risk and warrants preventive planning.

Establish a relationship with a veterinarian experienced in camelids, since not all livestock vets work with alpacas. Routine vaccinations, body-condition scoring, dental checks, and annual shearing all contribute to a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I keep just one alpaca?** No. Alpacas are herd animals and suffer when kept alone. Always keep at least two, and preferably more, so they feel secure.

**Do alpacas spit at people?** They can, but it is uncommon. Spitting is mostly directed at other alpacas over food or social disputes, and humans usually only get caught in the crossfire.

## Conclusion

Alpacas are rewarding, gentle animals that suit small farms and hobbyists willing to provide pasture, shelter, companionship, and annual shearing. Keep them in groups, feed them mainly hay and grass, and line up a camelid-savvy vet, and these calm camelids can thrive for two decades or more.
