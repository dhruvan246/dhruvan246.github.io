The American Bulldog is a powerful, athletic working dog descended from the old English bulldogs brought to North America by early settlers, where they were used as all-purpose farm and stock dogs. Confident and loyal, the breed nearly disappeared after World War II before dedicated breeders revived it. Today the American Bulldog is valued as a devoted family guardian and capable working companion.

## Appearance & temperament

The American Bulldog is a muscular, well-balanced dog standing roughly 20-27 inches at the shoulder. Males are noticeably larger than females. The breed appears in two general types: the stockier "Bully" (Johnson) type and the leaner, more agile "Standard" (Scott) type, with many dogs falling between the two. The coat is short and smooth, most often white or white with patches of brindle, red, brown, or black.

Temperamentally, American Bulldogs are confident, affectionate, and deeply bonded to their families. They are naturally protective and alert, which makes early socialization essential. With proper raising they are gentle and patient with children, though their size and exuberance mean young kids should always be supervised.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality diet formulated for large, active breeds, with protein supporting their substantial muscle mass. Most adults do well on measured meals twice a day rather than free-feeding, which helps prevent the weight gain this breed is prone to. Puppies should eat a large-breed puppy formula to support steady, controlled growth and protect developing joints. Always provide fresh water, and adjust portions based on activity level and body condition. Consult your veterinarian about appropriate amounts and any breed-specific dietary concerns.

## Exercise & training

This is an energetic, athletic breed that needs substantial daily exercise: brisk walks, jogging, fetch, tug, and structured play. A bored or under-exercised American Bulldog may become destructive or develop unwanted behaviors. They excel at activities like weight pull, agility, and obedience work that engage both body and mind.

Training should begin early and rely on firm, consistent, positive-reinforcement methods. American Bulldogs are intelligent but can be strong-willed, so a confident handler and clear boundaries are important. Early socialization with people and other animals is critical given their protective instincts.

## Grooming & care

The short coat is low-maintenance. Weekly brushing removes loose hair and keeps the coat healthy, with more frequent brushing during seasonal shedding. Bathe only as needed. Clean facial wrinkles (if present) to prevent moisture buildup, check and clean the ears regularly, trim nails as needed, and brush the teeth several times a week to support dental health.

## Health & lifespan

American Bulldogs typically live 10-14 years. The breed is generally robust but can be prone to hip and elbow dysplasia, certain hereditary neurological conditions, allergies, and skin issues. Some lines may experience cherry eye or entropion. Buying from a reputable breeder who screens breeding stock, maintaining a healthy weight, and scheduling regular veterinary checkups all help support a long, healthy life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are American Bulldogs good family dogs?**
Yes. When well-socialized and properly trained, they are loyal, affectionate, and protective companions. Their size and energy mean interactions with very young children should be supervised.

**Do American Bulldogs need a lot of exercise?**
Yes. They are an athletic, high-energy breed that needs vigorous daily activity and mental stimulation to stay happy, fit, and well-behaved.

## Conclusion

The American Bulldog is a strong, courageous, and devoted breed that thrives with an active owner who can meet its exercise and training needs. With early socialization, consistent guidance, and good preventive care, this loyal working dog makes a steadfast and loving family guardian.
