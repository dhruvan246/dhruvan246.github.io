African cichlids are a large group of vividly coloured freshwater fish from the great Rift Valley lakes of East Africa, chiefly Lakes Malawi, Tanganyika and Victoria. Prized for their intense colours and bold personalities, they reward keepers who can meet their specific water and social needs.

## Overview

| Attribute | Typical range |
|---|---|
| Adult size | Around 3–8 in (8–20 cm), varies by species |
| Lifespan | About 5–10 years with good care |
| Minimum tank size | 55 gallons (around 200 L) for most communities |
| Water type | Freshwater, hard and alkaline |
| Care level | Intermediate |

## Appearance & behaviour

African cichlids span an enormous range of colours and patterns, including electric blues, bright yellows, oranges and intricate stripes or spots. Many Malawi mbuna are boldly striped, while Tanganyikan species range from tiny shell-dwellers to larger predators. They are intelligent, active and often territorial. Males frequently display brighter colours and defend patches of rock. This natural aggression is a defining trait and must be managed through tank size, stocking and aquascaping rather than eliminated.

## Tank setup & water parameters

Replicate the rocky Rift Lake environment. Provide plenty of rockwork to create caves, territories and sight breaks, with a sand or fine gravel substrate. A 55-gallon tank is a sensible minimum for most community setups, with larger species needing more. Aim for warm water around 75–82°F (24–28°C), a high pH near 7.8–8.6, and hard, alkaline water with elevated mineral content. Strong filtration and regular partial water changes are essential because these fish are messy and sensitive to poor water quality. Keep the tank securely covered, as some species jump.

## Diet & feeding

Diet depends heavily on species. Many Malawi mbuna are herbivores that thrive on algae- and spirulina-based foods and can suffer bloat from too much protein. Other cichlids are omnivores or carnivores that take quality pellets, flakes and occasional frozen foods such as brine shrimp or krill. Research your specific species and choose a staple food formulated for African cichlids. Feed small amounts once or twice daily and avoid overfeeding to protect water quality and digestive health.

## Tank mates & compatibility

These fish are best kept with other African cichlids of similar origin and temperament. Mixing fish from different lakes is generally discouraged because of differing diets and behaviour. Overstocking slightly within filtration limits can actually reduce aggression by spreading it among many fish. Avoid housing them with small, slow or long-finned community fish, which may be harassed or eaten. Keeping a higher ratio of females to males can also ease territorial disputes.

## Health & common issues

Common problems include "Malawi bloat," often linked to incorrect diet or stress, along with ich, fin damage from fighting and bacterial infections tied to poor water quality. Maintaining stable, clean water and an appropriate diet prevents most issues. Quarantine new fish before adding them. If a fish stops eating, swells, or shows abnormal swimming, consult a vet or experienced aquarist familiar with cichlids rather than self-medicating with guesswork.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are African cichlids good for beginners?** They suit dedicated beginners willing to research, but their aggression and specific hard-water needs make them better as an intermediate-level fish.

**Can I keep just one African cichlid?** It is possible, but most are social, active fish that do best in properly stocked groups within a suitably large tank.

## Conclusion

African cichlids are among the most colourful and characterful freshwater fish you can keep. With a spacious, rocky tank, hard alkaline water, a species-appropriate diet and careful attention to compatibility, they make a rewarding and long-lived aquarium centrepiece.
