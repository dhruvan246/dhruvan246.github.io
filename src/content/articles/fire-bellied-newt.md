The Fire-Bellied Newt (genus *Cynops*, most often the Chinese or Japanese species) is one of the most popular beginner amphibians. Named for the vivid orange-to-red blotches on its underside, it is hardy, undemanding, and rewarding to watch. With cool, clean water and a steady routine, these small salamanders thrive for many years in a home aquarium.

## Overview

| Attribute | Typical range |
|-----------|---------------|
| Size | 8-12 cm (3-5 in) total length |
| Lifespan | 10-20+ years with good care |

## Appearance & behaviour

Fire-Bellied Newts have a dark brown-to-black back, often with a slightly rough or granular skin texture, and a striking belly marked with orange or red patches that warn predators of their mild toxicity. They are mostly aquatic as adults but will rest on land. Expect slow, deliberate movement, periodic trips to the surface to gulp air, and more activity around feeding time. They are social enough to be kept in small groups but do not require company.

## Habitat & water

A semi-aquatic setup works best: a larger water area roughly 15-20 cm deep, plus a dry or floating land section the newt can climb onto. Use a gentle filter, as these newts dislike strong currents. Keep the water cool, ideally 16-22 C (60-72 F); sustained warmth above 24 C stresses them and can be fatal, so avoid heaters and warm rooms. Always dechlorinate tap water. Live or silk plants, smooth rocks, and hides reduce stress. A secure lid is essential, as newts climb and can escape. Perform regular partial water changes to keep ammonia and nitrite at zero.

## Diet & feeding

Fire-Bellied Newts are carnivores. Offer a varied diet of bloodworms, blackworms, brine shrimp, chopped earthworms, daphnia, and small portions of sinking carnivore or newt pellets. Live or frozen-thawed foods are readily accepted; many newts ignore dry pellets entirely. Feed adults every two to three days and remove uneaten food to protect water quality. Avoid feeder fish and oily meats. Variety helps prevent nutritional deficiencies.

## Handling & health

These newts are display animals, not handling pets. Their skin secretes mild toxins, so handle only when necessary, with clean wet hands, and always wash thoroughly afterward, keeping hands away from eyes and mouth. The most common health problems stem from poor water quality or overheating, leading to skin infections, bloating, or lethargy. Signs of trouble include loss of appetite, floating, cloudy skin, or sores. Quarantine new arrivals and consult a vet experienced with amphibians if illness appears.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Fire-Bellied Newts good for beginners?** Yes. They are hardy and inexpensive, but they need cool, clean, well-maintained water, so they suit owners willing to keep up with regular tank care.

**Are they poisonous?** Their skin produces a mild toxin as a defence. It is not dangerous through normal viewing, but you should wash your hands after any contact and never let pets or children handle them.

## Conclusion

With a cool, filtered semi-aquatic tank, a varied carnivorous diet, and minimal handling, the Fire-Bellied Newt is a long-lived and engaging amphibian. Note that ownership rules for exotic species vary by region, so check local laws before buying. Provide stable conditions and these charming newts can be companions for well over a decade.
