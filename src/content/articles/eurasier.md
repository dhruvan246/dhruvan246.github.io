The Eurasier is a balanced, dignified spitz-type breed developed in Germany in the 1960s by crossing the Chow Chow, Keeshond (Wolfspitz) and later the Samoyed. Bred specifically as a calm family companion, the Eurasier is prized for its even temperament, close bond with its household and quiet, undemanding nature.

## Appearance & temperament

The Eurasier is a well-proportioned, medium-to-large spitz weighing roughly 40–70 pounds (18–32 kg). It carries a thick double coat with a dense undercoat and longer guard hairs, a bushy tail curled over the back, erect triangular ears and a fox-like expression. Coats come in many colours including fawn, red, wolf-grey, black and black-and-tan; only pure white, white patches and liver are considered undesirable. Some have a blue-black or pink-spotted tongue, a nod to the Chow Chow ancestry.

In temperament the Eurasier is calm, alert and deeply attached to its family. It is typically reserved and watchful with strangers without being aggressive, and rarely a nuisance barker. This is a sensitive breed that does poorly when isolated; it thrives living indoors as a true companion.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, high-quality dog food appropriate to the Eurasier's age, weight and activity level. Because the breed has only moderate energy needs, portions should be measured to prevent weight gain, which strains joints. Divide the daily amount into two meals and limit treats to a small fraction of total calories. Always provide fresh water, and consult your veterinarian before changing diets or adding supplements.

## Exercise & training

The Eurasier needs moderate daily exercise: a couple of walks plus some free play or yard time generally suffice. It enjoys hiking and family outings but is equally content relaxing at home. Mental stimulation matters as much as physical activity.

Training should be gentle, consistent and positive. Eurasiers are intelligent and learn quickly but are sensitive and somewhat independent, so harsh corrections backfire. Early, thorough socialisation helps temper the breed's natural reserve with strangers and builds a confident, well-mannered adult.

## Grooming & care

The thick double coat needs regular brushing—roughly twice a week, and daily during the two heavy seasonal sheds when the undercoat blows out. Routine grooming keeps the coat clean and largely odour-free; baths are needed only occasionally. Check and trim nails, brush teeth regularly, and inspect the ears for debris. The dense coat offers good cold tolerance, but provide shade and water in hot weather.

## Health & lifespan

The Eurasier is generally a healthy, robust breed with a typical lifespan of 12–14 years, helped by responsible breeding within a relatively small gene pool. Conditions occasionally seen include hip and elbow dysplasia, patellar luxation, hypothyroidism and eye disorders. Choosing a breeder who screens parent dogs, keeping your dog at a healthy weight and maintaining routine veterinary care all support a long, comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Eurasiers good family dogs?**
Yes. They were bred specifically as calm, affectionate companions and bond closely with all members of the household, including children, while staying gentle and patient.

**Do Eurasiers bark a lot?**
No. Most Eurasiers are quiet and bark only when there is genuine reason, making them well suited to family and even apartment life if exercised.

## Conclusion

The Eurasier is an even-tempered, loyal companion that combines a striking spitz appearance with a calm, devoted personality. With moderate exercise, regular coat care and plenty of family togetherness, this breed makes a loving, low-drama addition to a committed home.
