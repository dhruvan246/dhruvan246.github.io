The Otterhound is a large, shaggy British scent hound originally bred to hunt otters along rivers. Friendly, boisterous and endearingly clownish, it is also one of the rarest native dog breeds in the world.

## Overview

| Trait | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Size | Large (approx. 30–52 kg) |
| Lifespan | 10–13 years |
| Coat | Rough, dense, water-resistant double coat |
| Energy | Moderate to high |
| Good with kids | Generally very good |

## Appearance & temperament

The Otterhound is a big, powerfully built hound with a distinctive rough, shaggy coat, a broad head, and large webbed feet that make it a strong swimmer. Coats come in many colours, often grizzle or wheaten with darker markings. It has an exceptionally keen nose and a deep, bellowing voice.

In temperament the Otterhound is good-natured, even-tempered and affectionate, with a playful, sometimes goofy streak. As a scent hound it can be independent and single-minded once it picks up a trail, so it is friendly rather than sharply obedient. Most get along well with people, children and other dogs.

## Diet & nutrition

- Feed a complete, good-quality diet appropriate for a large, active dog, with portions matched to age and activity level.
- As a deep-chested breed, the Otterhound may be at higher risk of bloat (gastric torsion); feeding measured meals and avoiding heavy exercise right after eating are sensible precautions.
- Keep an eye on body condition, as a relaxed, food-motivated hound can gain weight easily. Always provide fresh water, especially after swimming or exercise.

## Exercise & training

The Otterhound needs plenty of daily exercise: long walks, secure off-lead runs, and ideally chances to swim, which it loves. Without enough activity it can become bored and restless.

Because it follows its nose, a securely fenced area and a reliable lead are important, as recall can be unreliable once a scent takes over. Training should start early and use patient, positive, reward-based methods. The breed is intelligent but independent, so consistency and good humour go a long way.

## Grooming & care

- The rough double coat needs regular brushing, roughly once or twice a week, to prevent matting; expect more effort during seasonal shedding.
- The beard tends to trap water, dirt and food, so wipe it as needed to keep it clean.
- Check and clean the large, hanging ears regularly to reduce the risk of infection, and keep nails trimmed.
- Bathe when genuinely dirty rather than too often, to preserve the coat's natural water resistance.

## Health & lifespan

Otterhounds typically live around 10 to 13 years. As a large, deep-chested breed, recognised health considerations include hip and elbow dysplasia, bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus), and a hereditary bleeding disorder (Glanzmann thrombasthenia) seen in some lines. Choosing a breeder who health-tests their dogs, keeping your hound at a healthy weight, and maintaining routine veterinary care all support a long, comfortable life. Discuss any specific health concerns with your veterinarian.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Otterhounds rare?**
Yes. The Otterhound is considered one of the most endangered native dog breeds, with relatively few registered each year, so finding a puppy can take patience.

**Do Otterhounds make good family pets?**
For active households they can be wonderful: they are affectionate, tolerant and generally good with children, though their size, energy and shaggy, water-loving nature suit owners with space and time for grooming.

## Conclusion

The Otterhound is a rare, amiable giant with a remarkable nose, a love of water, and a cheerful personality. With ample exercise, regular coat care, sensible feeding and attentive health monitoring, this big-hearted hound makes a devoted and entertaining companion for the right home.
