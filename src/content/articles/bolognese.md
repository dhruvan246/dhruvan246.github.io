The Bolognese is a small, pure-white companion dog of the Bichon family, named for the Italian city of Bologna. Calm, devoted and people-oriented, it has been prized as a lapdog for centuries and thrives on close human company.

## Overview

| Trait | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Size | Small (about 10–12 in / 25–30 cm tall, 5.5–9 lb / 2.5–4 kg) |
| Lifespan | Typically 12–14 years |
| Coat | Long, single, fluffy white coat |
| Energy | Low to moderate |
| Good with kids | Yes, with gentle, supervised handling |

## Appearance & temperament

The Bolognese is a compact, square-built dog with a distinctive coat of long, soft white hair that falls in loose flocks rather than lying flat. The fur covers the whole body, including the face, framing dark, round, expressive eyes and a black nose. The single coat does not shed heavily.

In temperament, the Bolognese is serene, affectionate and deeply attached to its family. It tends to bond closely with one or two people and dislikes being left alone for long periods. Generally quiet and reserved with strangers, it is playful at home and gets along well with children and other pets when socialized early.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a complete, balanced dog food formulated for small breeds, choosing a life-stage formula appropriate for puppies, adults or seniors. Because of their small size, Bolognese need only modest portions, and the calorie-dense nature of small-breed food makes overfeeding easy. Measure meals, divide them into two servings a day, and limit treats to avoid weight gain, which strains small joints. Fresh water should always be available. Adjust amounts based on age, activity and body condition, and consult your veterinarian about any dietary concerns.

## Exercise & training

Despite being a companion breed, the Bolognese benefits from daily activity. A couple of short walks plus indoor play usually satisfy its low-to-moderate energy needs. It adapts well to apartments and does not require a large yard.

This is an intelligent, sensitive breed that learns quickly with positive, reward-based methods. Harsh corrections can make it anxious or withdrawn. Early socialization and gentle, consistent house-training pay off, as some small breeds can be slow to fully housebreak. Because of their attachment to people, gradual practice with short absences helps prevent separation anxiety.

## Grooming & care

The long white coat needs regular care. Brush several times a week, ideally daily, to prevent the soft hair from matting, and bathe periodically to keep the coat bright. Many owners keep their Bolognese in a shorter trim for easier upkeep. Wipe the area around the eyes to manage tear staining, and check the ears for wax or moisture. Routine nail trims and regular tooth brushing round out basic care, as small breeds are prone to dental crowding.

## Health & lifespan

The Bolognese is generally a hardy, long-lived breed, often reaching 12–14 years. Like many small dogs, it can be prone to patellar luxation (slipping kneecaps) and dental disease. Eye conditions and tear staining may also occur. Buying from a responsible breeder, maintaining a healthy weight, and keeping up with regular veterinary checkups, vaccinations and dental care support a long, comfortable life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Bolognese dogs shed a lot?**
No. They have a single, non-flocking coat that sheds very little, but it does require frequent brushing to prevent mats and tangles.

**Are Bolognese good for first-time owners?**
Yes. Their calm nature, modest exercise needs and eagerness to please make them suitable for first-time owners, provided they can offer regular grooming and steady companionship.

## Conclusion

The Bolognese is a devoted, low-energy companion that suits apartment dwellers and anyone wanting a close, affectionate bond. With routine grooming, balanced feeding, gentle training and regular veterinary care, this charming white lapdog can be a loyal friend for well over a decade.
