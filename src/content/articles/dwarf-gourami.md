The Dwarf Gourami (*Trichogaster lalius*) is a small, brightly colored freshwater fish native to the slow-moving waters of South Asia. Prized for its shimmering blues, reds, and oranges, it is a popular centerpiece fish for community aquariums and a manageable choice for hobbyists who can provide stable, calm conditions.

## Overview

| Attribute | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Adult size | About 2 to 3.5 inches (5–9 cm) |
| Lifespan | Around 3 to 5 years |
| Minimum tank size | 10 gallons (larger for groups) |
| Water type | Warm freshwater |
| Care level | Beginner to intermediate |

## Appearance & behaviour

Dwarf Gouramis have a compact, laterally flattened body with thread-like pelvic fins they use to "feel" their surroundings. Males are vividly colored with diagonal stripes, while females tend to be more silvery and subdued. As labyrinth fish, they can gulp air from the surface, so they appreciate access to the top of the tank. They are generally peaceful and somewhat shy, often hovering among plants. Males can become territorial toward one another, so keeping a single male or a male with one or more females usually works best.

## Tank setup & water parameters

A heavily planted tank with gentle filtration suits this species best, as it dislikes strong currents. Provide floating plants and some shaded areas to help it feel secure. Aim for a water temperature of roughly 72–82°F (22–28°C), a pH near 6.0–7.5, and soft to moderately hard water. Keep the tank covered, since the warm, humid air layer above the surface protects the labyrinth organ. Perform regular partial water changes and avoid sudden swings in temperature or chemistry, which stress this sensitive fish.

## Diet & feeding

Dwarf Gouramis are omnivores with a healthy appetite. Offer a varied diet built around a quality flake or micro-pellet, supplemented with frozen or live foods such as bloodworms, brine shrimp, and daphnia. Occasional blanched vegetables or vegetable-based foods round out their nutrition. Feed small amounts once or twice daily, only what they finish in a couple of minutes, to keep water quality high and prevent obesity.

## Tank mates & compatibility

These peaceful fish do well with other calm, similarly sized community species such as small tetras, rasboras, corydoras catfish, and peaceful livebearers. Avoid fin-nippers like some barbs, as well as large or aggressive fish that may bully or outcompete them. Do not house multiple males together in a small tank, since they may squabble over territory.

## Health & common issues

Dwarf Gouramis are unfortunately prone to "Dwarf Gourami Iridovirus" and a bacterial condition sometimes called Dwarf Gourami Disease, both of which can cause loss of color, lethargy, and sores. Buying healthy stock from reputable sources reduces risk. Like most aquarium fish, they can also develop ich, fin rot, and fungal infections, usually linked to poor water quality or stress. Maintain clean, stable water and quarantine new arrivals. Consult an aquatic veterinarian or experienced aquarist for persistent illness rather than guessing at treatments.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I keep two Dwarf Gouramis together?**
You can, but avoid two males in a small tank, as they often become territorial. A single male, or a male with one or more females in a spacious, well-planted tank, is the safer arrangement.

**Are Dwarf Gouramis good for beginners?**
Yes, with care. They are hardy in stable conditions but sensitive to poor water quality and stress, and they carry some disease risk, so a properly cycled, well-maintained tank is essential.

## Conclusion

The Dwarf Gourami rewards attentive keepers with striking color and gentle personality. Give it a planted, calm tank, stable warm water, a varied diet, and peaceful companions, and source healthy stock to sidestep its known disease risks. With consistent care, this little jewel can be a long-lasting highlight of a community aquarium.
