The Tenterfield Terrier is a small, energetic Australian breed developed from working terriers brought by early British settlers. Named after the New South Wales town of Tenterfield, this hardy little dog earned its keep on farms by hunting rats, rabbits and other vermin. Today it is prized as a smart, affectionate and remarkably agile companion.

## Appearance & temperament

The Tenterfield Terrier is a balanced, square-built little dog with clean lines, a fine but sturdy frame and an alert, confident expression. The coat is short and smooth, most often white with black, tan or liver markings. Ears may be erect or semi-erect, and the tail is carried with a jaunty lift.

In character, the breed is bold, friendly and intelligent. Tenterfield Terriers bond closely with their families, enjoy being involved in daily activities and tend to get along well with children and other dogs. Their terrier roots mean they remain keen, curious and quick to alert, making them lively watchdogs without being excessively yappy when well socialised.

## Diet & nutrition

Feed a high-quality complete dog food formulated for small, active breeds. Because adults are light, portion control matters: measure meals rather than free-feeding to prevent weight gain, which strains small joints. Most adults do well on two measured meals a day, while puppies need three to four smaller feedings.

Choose a food with named animal protein among the first ingredients and adjust amounts to your dog's age, weight and activity level. Keep fresh water available, use treats sparingly during training, and consult your vet before any significant diet change.

## Exercise & training

Despite their size, Tenterfield Terriers are athletic and need genuine daily activity. Aim for at least 30–60 minutes split between walks, off-lead play in a secure area and games like fetch. They excel at dog sports such as agility, flyball and earthdog trials, which channel their energy and sharp minds.

Training is usually straightforward thanks to their eagerness to please and quick wits. Use positive, reward-based methods, keep sessions short and varied, and start socialisation early. A strong recall is valuable, as their prey drive may tempt them to chase small animals.

## Grooming & care

Grooming needs are minimal. The short, single coat sheds modestly and only requires a weekly brush to remove loose hair and keep it glossy. Bathe occasionally, as needed.

Trim nails every few weeks, brush the teeth several times a week to support dental health, and check the ears regularly for dirt or signs of irritation, cleaning gently when needed.

## Health & lifespan

The Tenterfield Terrier is generally a robust, long-lived breed, often reaching 12–15 years. As with many small dogs, potential concerns include patellar luxation (slipping kneecaps), dental crowding and occasional hereditary eye issues. Responsible breeders screen their stock to reduce these risks.

Keep your dog at a healthy weight, maintain routine veterinary check-ups, stay current on vaccinations and parasite prevention, and care for the teeth to support a long, active life.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are Tenterfield Terriers good apartment dogs?**
They can adapt to apartments if given enough daily exercise and mental stimulation, but they are active and will not thrive being left idle for long periods.

**Do Tenterfield Terriers bark a lot?**
They are alert and will sound off at unusual activity, but with early training and socialisation they are not typically excessive barkers.

## Conclusion

The Tenterfield Terrier is a spirited, affectionate and adaptable small breed that suits active individuals and families alike. With modest grooming, regular exercise and consistent positive training, this clever little Australian terrier makes a devoted and entertaining lifelong companion.
