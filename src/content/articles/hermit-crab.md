Land hermit crabs are social, long-lived invertebrates that have become popular as low-cost pets, often sold at seaside shops. Despite their reputation as "starter" animals, they have demanding environmental needs and reward attentive keepers with surprisingly active, interesting behaviour.

## Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Size | 0.5–6 inches, depending on species and shell |
| Lifespan | Often 10–20+ years with proper care |
| Housing | Humid, heated glass terrarium ("crabitat") |
| Temperament | Social, shy, mostly nocturnal |
| Care level | Intermediate (specialised setup required) |

## Appearance & behaviour

Hermit crabs have a hard exoskeleton over the front of the body and a soft, curled abdomen they protect inside a borrowed snail shell. They use modified rear legs to grip the shell and large front claws (chelipeds) for feeding and defence. Common pet species include the Caribbean hermit crab (*Coenobita clypeatus*) and the Ecuadorian hermit crab (*Coenobita compressus*).

They are most active at night, climbing, digging, and exploring. Hermit crabs are genuinely social and do best kept in groups of two or more, where they often pile together while resting.

## Housing & environment

A glass aquarium with a secure lid works well; allow at least 5–10 gallons for a couple of small crabs, more as they grow. Provide 4–6 inches of substrate—a mix of play sand and coconut fibre that holds a burrow—so crabs can dig down to molt safely.

Humidity is critical: maintain roughly 70–80% using a hygrometer, and keep temperatures around 75–82°F (24–28°C) with an under-tank or overhead heater. Include climbing branches, hiding spots, and both fresh and salt (marine, dechlorinated) water dishes deep enough to submerge in but with an easy exit.

## Diet & feeding

Hermit crabs are omnivorous scavengers. Offer a varied diet of fresh fruits and vegetables (apple, leafy greens, carrot), plus protein sources like cooked egg, fish, or commercial hermit crab food. Calcium from cuttlebone or crushed eggshell supports the exoskeleton.

Avoid foods with added salt, preservatives, or copper sulfate, which can be harmful. Remove uneaten fresh food daily to prevent mold, and always provide both fresh and saltwater.

## Handling & care

Handle crabs gently and sparingly, especially when newly acquired, supporting them over a soft surface. Avoid grabbing the shell in a way that lets the crab dangle, which can cause it to drop or pinch. Keep the habitat clean by spot-cleaning waste and replacing substrate periodically.

Crucially, supply several empty, well-sized shells of different shapes so crabs can "upgrade" as they grow. Lack of suitable shells causes stress and competition.

## Health

The most important health event is molting, when a crab sheds its exoskeleton to grow. Molting crabs often burrow and stay hidden for weeks—do not dig them up or disturb them, as the new skin is fragile. Provide extra calcium and leave the molted exoskeleton in the tank for the crab to eat.

Warning signs include lethargy above ground, leaving the shell, a strong fishy odor, or limb loss. Poor humidity damages the gills and is a leading cause of illness. For persistent problems, consult a veterinarian experienced with invertebrates.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do hermit crabs need saltwater?** Yes. Land hermit crabs require both fresh dechlorinated water and marine saltwater (made with aquarium salt mix, not table salt) to stay healthy and balance their internal fluids.

**Can hermit crabs live alone?** They can survive alone but are social by nature and thrive in small groups. Keeping two or more usually leads to more natural, active behaviour.

## Conclusion

Hermit crabs are inexpensive to buy but need a properly heated, humid habitat, varied food, and a supply of spare shells to truly flourish. Meet those needs and these quiet, social invertebrates can live for well over a decade, making them a rewarding long-term companion.
