The Greek tortoise (*Testudo graeca*), also called the spur-thighed tortoise, is a hardy Mediterranean species popular with reptile keepers. With the right space, warmth, and a fibre-rich plant diet, it can be a rewarding, exceptionally long-lived companion.

## Overview

| Trait | Details |
|---|---|
| Adult size | Roughly 5–8 in (13–20 cm) shell length, varies by subspecies |
| Lifespan | Commonly 50+ years; many reach 75–100+ with good care |
| Enclosure size | Large; an adult needs at least a 4 x 2 ft floor area, more is better |
| Temperament | Calm, curious, generally docile; not a cuddly pet |
| Care level | Intermediate; long-term commitment |

## Appearance & temperament

Greek tortoises have a rounded, domed carapace patterned in tan, brown, and black, with sturdy scaled limbs. Their common name comes from the small spurs ("spur-thigh") on the back legs. Adults are modestly sized, though size and colour vary among the many subspecies.

They are slow-moving, mild-mannered grazers. Most tolerate gentle observation and routine care but can become stressed by frequent handling. Males may be territorial toward other males.

## Habitat & enclosure

These tortoises need far more floor space than height. A spacious tortoise table or enclosure is preferable to a tall glass tank, which traps stale air. Provide a substrate such as a topsoil-and-sand mix or coir that allows shallow digging, plus hides, flat basking rocks, and edible plants for grazing and cover.

Where climate permits, a secure outdoor pen with sun, shade, and a predator-proof, escape-proof barrier is excellent. A shallow water dish for soaking and drinking should always be available.

## Heating, lighting & humidity

Provide a thermal gradient using a basking lamp, with a warm basking spot around 90–95°F (32–35°C) and a cooler end in the high 60s–70s°F (around 20–24°C). Allow a natural night-time drop.

UVB lighting is essential for vitamin D3 and healthy bone and shell growth; use a quality reptile UVB tube and replace it on schedule. Greek tortoises prefer a relatively dry environment with a humid, moist hide or burrow available. Many keepers cycle a winter brumation period, which should only be attempted with research and a healthy animal.

## Diet & feeding

Greek tortoises are herbivores that thrive on high-fibre, low-protein greens. Offer a varied mix of weeds and leafy plants such as dandelion, plantain, clover, mallow, and other safe broadleaf weeds, supplemented with appropriate flowers.

Avoid fruit, which is too sugary, and skip dog or cat food entirely. Provide a calcium source such as a cuttlebone, and use vet-recommended supplements as advised. Always keep clean water available.

## Handling & health

Handle minimally and close to the ground, supporting the body. Watch for signs of trouble: runny eyes or nose, soft or pyramided shell, lethargy, or refusal to eat. Common issues include respiratory infections, metabolic bone disease from poor lighting or diet, and parasites.

Use a reptile-experienced veterinarian for check-ups. Note that legality and protected status vary by region, so confirm local rules and buy only captive-bred animals.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do Greek tortoises hibernate?** Many do brumate in cooler months, but it should only be done with a healthy tortoise after proper research, or skipped by keeping it warm and active year-round.

**How big do Greek tortoises get?** Most reach about 5–8 inches in shell length as adults, though exact size depends on the subspecies.

## Conclusion

Greek tortoises are gentle, long-lived reptiles that reward attentive care with decades of companionship. Give them ample space, correct heat and UVB, and a varied weed-based diet, and consult a reptile vet to keep your tortoise thriving.
